# The Wider World — 1933

## Global Context
- The Great Depression is in full force. Unemployment is catastrophic across the Western world.
- Franklin Roosevelt has just taken office in the United States (March 1933). The New Deal is being formulated.
- Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany in January 1933. The world has not yet understood what this means.
- Polar exploration is a minor news item. Byrd's second Antarctic expedition (1933-35) is the major Antarctic story — the Starkweather-Moore expedition is too small to attract press attention.

## Why This Matters
The expedition is invisible. If the players return with an impossible story, the world is not listening. This is part of the PROPHET-PARADOX: even truth-telling fails when no one is paying attention. The players cannot rely on institutional support — the institutions are busy with their own crises.

## Academic Climate
- Miskatonic University has distanced itself from Dyer's claims. His career is effectively over.
- The expedition is funded through Starkweather's personal connections and Moore's institutional contacts — not through Miskatonic directly.
- The scientific establishment's attitude: Antarctic anomalies are interesting geology, nothing more. Dyer's account is trauma-induced confabulation.

## Communication Infrastructure
- Transatlantic radio is functional but unreliable. Weather-dependent, with significant lag.
- Once south of approximately 60 degrees, radio contact becomes intermittent.
- Beyond the mountains, radio contact is functionally impossible.
- Moore's radio oversight depends entirely on this fragile link.
