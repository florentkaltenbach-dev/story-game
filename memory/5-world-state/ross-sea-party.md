# Ross Sea Party — Keeper Background Knowledge

## What Happened
The Ross Sea party (1914–17) was the support arm of Shackleton's Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition. Mission: lay supply depots from McMurdo Sound to the Beardmore Glacier so Shackleton's main party could survive the final leg of a transcontinental crossing.

Everything went wrong. The Aurora was torn from its moorings, stranding ten men with almost no supplies. Despite this, they completed the full depot-laying mission — 198 continuous days of sledging, the longest in Antarctic history. Three men died. The depots were never used. Shackleton's Endurance was crushed in the Weddell Sea. The transcontinental crossing never happened.

## The Dead
- **Arnold Spencer-Smith** — collapsed from exhaustion and scurvy, died 9 March 1916, buried in the ice.
- **Aeneas Mackintosh** — walked onto thin sea ice against all advice, 8 May 1916. Vanished.
- **Victor Hayward** — walked onto the ice with Mackintosh. Vanished.

## What Players Find
The Ross Sea party occupied the same sites the 1933 expedition will visit. Their remains are layered on top of Scott's and Shackleton's earlier expeditions:

- **Cape Evans Hut:** Richards' inscription (three names scratched on the wall), improvised canvas clothing, journals, conflicting orders, the unsent cable, scientific instruments, dog harnesses.
- **Hut Point:** Evidence of desperate habitation during the 1915 stranding.
- **Depot sites (Minna Bluff, 80°S, Mount Hope):** Cached supplies, markers, and at Mount Hope — Wild's undelivered letter to his brother.

## The Aurora
The expedition ship was torn away in a gale, drifted locked in ice for nine months, was repaired, returned to rescue the survivors. Then Shackleton sold it. It vanished on a coal run in 1917 — never seen again. Aboard was James Paton, the boatswain, who survived Antarctica only to die in the ordinary ocean.

## Why This Matters to the Keeper
- **Emotional grounding before cosmic horror.** The Ross Sea party's tragedy is human-scale — duty, loyalty, institutional failure. Players who feel the human cost at Cape Evans react differently when they encounter something inhuman in the mountains.
- **The futility question.** The depots were never needed. Three men died for nothing. Richards said it wasn't futile — "it was something the human spirit had accomplished." This is the game's empathy turn in miniature.
- **The patron problem.** Shackleton sent contradictory orders, underfunded the expedition, failed to communicate critical information. This maps directly onto Starkweather-Moore. The historical precedent makes patron dysfunction feel inevitable rather than contrived.
- **Leadership under pressure.** The Mackintosh-Joyce conflict (mission vs. survival) is a template for party dynamics if the player group splits.
- **The dogs.** Four survived the full ordeal: Oscar, Con, Gunner, Towser. Oscar rose to the occasion when everything was desperate. Con was killed by the other dogs. The Keeper can use dog loyalty as an emotional beat.

## Deployment Notes
- This material is tragic, not horrific. It operates in the human register.
- Not every detail needs to surface. The Keeper draws on it when players explore the right locations or ask the right questions.
- The Richards inscription at Cape Evans is one of the most powerful discoverable objects in the preset — scratched names of men who died completing a pointless mission. If a player character dies later, the MC could mirror the gesture.
