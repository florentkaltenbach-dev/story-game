# The Wider World — 1933

## Global Context
- The Great Depression is in full force. Unemployment is catastrophic across the Western world.
- Franklin Roosevelt has just taken office in the United States (March 1933). The New Deal is being formulated.
- Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany in January 1933. The world has not yet understood what this means.
- Polar exploration is a minor news item. Byrd's second Antarctic expedition (1933-35) is the major Antarctic story — the Starkweather-Moore expedition is too small to attract press attention.
- US-USSR diplomatic relations formally established November 1933 — the world's political alignments are shifting.

## Why This Matters
The expedition is invisible. If the players return with an impossible story, the world is not listening. This is part of the PROPHET-PARADOX: even truth-telling fails when no one is paying attention. The players cannot rely on institutional support — the institutions are busy with their own crises.

## Antarctic Expeditions Active 1928–1935
- **Byrd's First Expedition (1928–30):** Established Little America I on the Ross Ice Shelf. First flight over the South Pole (Nov 29, 1929). Academy Award documentary. Returned to ticker-tape parades at the start of the Depression.
- **Byrd's Second Expedition (1933–35):** Two ships (Jacob Ruppert, Bear of Oakland), four planes, four tractors. Little America II, Bay of Whales. Funded through small donations, corporate products, National Geographic. Byrd cultivated Roosevelt, Ford, Rockefeller. Winter 1934: Byrd occupied an advance weather station alone, suffered near-fatal carbon monoxide poisoning.
- **British Graham Land Expedition (1934–37):** Small, austere, two winters on the Antarctic Peninsula.
- **Lincoln Ellsworth's Trans-Antarctic Flight (1935):** First flight across the continent.
- **Territorial politics:** By 1933, seven nations making formal Antarctic territorial claims. No governing framework. The continent is politically contested space.

## Academic Climate
- Miskatonic University has distanced itself from Dyer's claims. His career is effectively over.
- The expedition is funded through Starkweather's personal connections and Moore's institutional contacts — not through Miskatonic directly.
- The scientific establishment's attitude: Antarctic anomalies are interesting geology, nothing more. Dyer's account is trauma-induced confabulation.
- State university budgets cut ~40% in the early 1930s. Private endowments shrank 26%+. Faculty salaries dropped 30–40%.
- Most research funding came from private philanthropies (Carnegie, Rockefeller), not government. Universities actively resisted federal funding.
- Individual wealthy patrons — industrialists, financiers — were the primary source for large expedition funding.

## Science and Discovery
- 1929: Hubble publishes evidence the universe is expanding. Einstein's "greatest blunder."
- 1930: Pluto discovered by Clyde Tombaugh (age 23, self-taught, Kansas farm family).
- 1932: Karl Jansky discovers radio waves from the Milky Way's center — founding radio astronomy.
- 1932: James Chadwick discovers the neutron. Carl Anderson detects antimatter.

## Fringe Science Currents
- Himmler's Ahnenerbe (formally established 1935, roots in 1933) — SS research into "Aryan ancestral heritage." Archaeology, expeditions, occult history.
- Welteislehre (Cosmic Ice Theory) — Austrian engineer's cosmology holding the universe was formed by cosmic ice. Framed conventional science as "Jewish-Liberal." Adopted into Nazi scientific culture.
- Hohlweltlehre (Concave Earth Theory) — humans live inside a hollow sphere. An actual military expedition was sent to Rügen island to spy on the British fleet by pointing cameras upward. It failed.
- The Keeper can deploy these as atmosphere: the 1930s is an era where the boundary between rigorous science and institutionalized delusion is alarmingly thin. This mirrors the players' own crisis of belief.

## Expedition Psychology
- "Polar madness" was a recognized phenomenon by the 1930s. The Belgica (1897–99) documented crew insanity during polar night.
- Documented cases: Professor David (forced to resign leadership, 1907–09), Sidney Jeffryes (paranoid transmissions claiming he was the only sane man, 1911–14).
- ~50% of polar expedition members experience significant mood disruption. ~5% require clinical intervention.
- Winter-over syndrome: depression, anxiety, irritability, sleep disruption, memory loss, paranoia.
- Strong stigma against admitting psychological symptoms. Practical incentives to conceal distress.
- Shackleton selected crew for optimism, selflessness, and social adaptability over technical skill.

## Communication Infrastructure
- Transatlantic radio is functional but unreliable. Weather-dependent, with significant lag.
- Once south of approximately 60 degrees, radio contact becomes intermittent.
- Beyond the mountains, radio contact is functionally impossible.
- Moore's radio oversight depends entirely on this fragile link.
