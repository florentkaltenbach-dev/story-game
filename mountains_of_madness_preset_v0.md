# Mountains of Madness — Ceremony Preset

*Extracted from H.P. Lovecraft's "At the Mountains of Madness" (1931, public domain)*
*Adapted for: Second expedition, 1930s era, 5 players, episodic weekly chapters*

---

## 1. STORYTELLING DNA

### Tone
- Academic dread. The horror comes through precise, scientific observation of impossible things.
- Retrospective urgency — the original narrator is writing *after* the events, trying to prevent what the players are now doing.
- Cosmic insignificance — human civilization is a footnote in a story that began a billion years ago.
- Nature as adversary — the cold, the wind, the distances are as dangerous as anything alive.

### Pacing Structure (The Lovecraft Curve)
1. **Preparation** — mundane logistics, equipment, expertise. This is where characters bond. Deceptively normal.
2. **Transit** — the journey into remoteness. Each stage removes one more layer of civilization. The world gets quieter, colder, more empty.
3. **Discovery** — small anomalies accumulate. Each one is explainable on its own. Together they form a pattern that shouldn't exist.
4. **Revelation** — the pattern becomes undeniable. The scale of what they've found dwarfs everything they thought they knew.
5. **Confrontation** — not combat. Encounter. Witnessing something that breaks their model of reality.
6. **Flight** — getting out alive, carrying knowledge that nobody will believe and that changes them permanently.

The MC should pace episodes along this curve across the full campaign. Early episodes are almost entirely Preparation and Transit. The horror is patient.

### Narrative Techniques (from the source)
- **Scientific cataloguing as horror:** Lovecraft lists geological periods, species classifications, anatomical details. The precision makes the impossible feel real. The Keeper should describe impossible things in clinical language.
- **Communication breakdown:** In the original, Lake sends increasingly frantic radio messages, then goes silent. Isolation and failed communication are central to the dread.
- **Scale as terror:** The mountains are higher than the Himalayas. The city is older than multicellular life. The numbers themselves are the horror.
- **The unreliable witness:** Not a liar — someone who *saw correctly* but whose account sounds insane. The players will eventually become this.
- **Dogs know first:** Animals sense what humans rationalize away. Any animal behavior is a narrative signal.
- **The warning ignored:** The entire story is a warning that nobody heeds. The players ARE the people ignoring the warning. This is the premise.
- **Art as foreshadowing:** Roerich paintings, the Necronomicon, Poe's Arthur Gordon Pym — references to art and literature that prefigure what's ahead. The Keeper can seed these.

## 2. WORLD

### Geography
- **McMurdo Sound** — the base of operations. Ships, supply caches, the last connection to the world.
- **Ross Ice Barrier** — the wall of ice marking the edge of the navigable south. Two hundred feet of vertical ice.
- **Beardmore Glacier** — the largest valley glacier in the world. The crossing point from coast to interior.
- **The Plateau** — the high Antarctic interior. Miles of ice over dead rock. 8,500+ feet above sea level.
- **The Mountains** — Lovecraft's unnamed range. Peaks exceeding 35,000 feet (higher than Everest). Black, bare of snow. Windswept. Caves with regular openings. Formations that look like cubes clinging to peaks.
- **The City Beyond** — ancient ruins behind the mountain range. Cyclopean architecture. Murals that tell the history of Earth's true first civilization. This is the destination the players don't know they're heading toward.

### Environment as Character
- **Wind:** Not just weather — "cadences sometimes held vague suggestions of a wild and half-sentient musical piping." The wind has a voice.
- **Mirages:** The Antarctic produces vivid optical illusions. "Distant mountains floated in the sky as enchanted cities." Reality is unreliable from the start.
- **Light:** The midnight sun. Red-gold light on black peaks. Opalescent voids where sky and snow merge. The light is always wrong.
- **Cold:** Not just uncomfortable — actively hostile. Equipment fails. Fuel freezes. Exposed skin dies.
- **Silence:** The most terrifying sound in the Antarctic is no sound at all.

### Timeline (1930s context)
- Radio is the only communication. Fragile, weather-dependent, with lag.
- Transportation: Dog sledges and Dornier seaplanes. No helicopters.
- Navigation: Compass, sextant, theodolite. Maps are incomplete or wrong.
- Photography: Glass plates and early film. Evidence is hard to capture and easy to doubt.
- The expedition is funded, university-backed, but small and not widely publicized.

## 3. THE SECOND EXPEDITION (Player Campaign)

### The Setup
It is 1933. Three years after the Miskatonic University Expedition.

Dr. William Dyer published his account. Nobody believed him. Some called him mad. The university quietly distanced itself. But someone read the account carefully — and wants to go back.

**Who sends the second expedition?** (MC chooses or co-creates with players)

Possible patrons:
- **A rival university** — academic glory, proving Dyer wrong or right
- **A wealthy private collector** — wants specimens, doesn't care about cost or safety
- **A government agency** — the military implications of the Elder Things' biotechnology
- **A newspaper magnate** — the story of the century, complete with photographs
- **Miskatonic itself** — a secret faction that believes Dyer and wants controlled study

The patron shapes the campaign's political tensions and what resources the players have.

### What the Players Know
- Dyer's published account exists but was dismissed. Players may have read parts of it.
- The first expedition lost people. The official story blames weather and a "camp disaster."
- The mountains were confirmed by aerial photography but never re-examined.
- There are rumors — about what Lake found, about creatures, about a city.
- Each player has their own reason for going. These reasons should conflict.

### What the Players Don't Know (yet)
- The Elder Things are real and some may survive in deep ice or subterranean lakes
- The city behind the mountains is vast, intact, and full of information that rewrites human history
- The Shoggoths — the Elder Things' created servants — are far more dangerous than the Elder Things themselves
- "Tekeli-li" — the cry that echoes through the tunnels. They'll hear it before they understand it.

## 4. CHARACTERS

### Player Character Archetypes
Not restricted to academics. Anyone who would plausibly be recruited for a dangerous Antarctic expedition in 1933.

| Archetype | Why they're here | What they bring | Their vulnerability |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| The Scientist | To prove or disprove. Knowledge is everything. | Expertise, analysis, careful observation | Curiosity overrides self-preservation |
| The Veteran | Seen war, survived. Hired for protection. | Calm under pressure, practical skills | What they've seen before doesn't prepare them for this |
| The Journalist | The story. Byline of a lifetime. | Asks questions others don't, records everything | Will risk others for the scoop |
| The Mechanic | Keeps the planes flying, the radios working, the drills drilling. | Essential practical skills | Rational mind that breaks when rationality fails |
| The Patron's Agent | Represents whoever's funding this. Has a hidden agenda. | Resources, authority, connections | Loyalty divided between the group and the patron |
| The Local Expert | Whaler, sailor, guide. Knows the ice. | Survival skills, navigation, weather sense | Superstitious, or maybe just experienced enough to be afraid |
| The Believer | Actually read Dyer. Believes every word. Maybe obsessively. | Preparation, foreknowledge | Already partially broken by what they believe is coming |

### Key NPCs (Keeper manages)
- **The Ship Captain** — veteran Antarctic whaler. Practical, skeptical, loyal to crew over mission.
- **The Radio Operator** — the voice of the outside world. When this voice goes silent, everything changes.
- **Dog Handler** — the dogs are the early warning system. This person interprets their behavior.
- **The Patron's Second Agent** — there may be more than one person with a hidden agenda.
- **Echoes of the First Expedition** — journals, equipment, bodies. Dyer's team is present through what they left behind.

## 6. EPISODE STRUCTURE (Suggested)

### Episode 1: The Gathering
Characters meet. Patron's briefing. Dyer's account discussed or dismissed. Equipment and planning. The ceremony of departure. Establish relationships, conflicts, secrets.

### Episode 2: The Crossing
Sea voyage to McMurdo Sound. The world shrinks. First mirages. First wind. Dogs on board. Character bonding and friction. Arrival at the ice.

### Episode 3: Base Camp
Establishing camp. First borings and surveys. The Antarctic is beautiful and lethal. Small anomalies in rock samples. The dogs are uneasy. Radio check-ins with the ship.

### Episode 4: The Find
Anomalous fossils. Triangular markings in impossible strata. A cave. Something preserved that shouldn't be. The scientific debate begins — what is this?

### Episode 5: Deeper
More specimens. The scope of the discovery expands. Someone finds something they don't share with the group. The wind is getting worse. Radio contact is less reliable.

### Episode 6: The Mountains
First visual contact with the impossible peaks. Higher than Everest. Black, bare, windswept. Cube-like formations. Cave mouths, unnervingly regular. The decision to cross.

### Episode 7: The City
What was mirage is real. Architecture older than complex life on Earth. Murals that tell a history nobody was meant to see. The Elder Things are no longer abstract — they were here, they built this, they were people.

### Episode 8: The Truth
What happened to the Elder Things. The Shoggoths. The final mural. "Tekeli-li." The moment the story stops being about discovery and starts being about survival.

### Episode 9: The Flight
Getting out. What they take with them. Who they leave behind. What they agree to say — and not say. The return to a world that won't believe them.

### Episode 10 (if needed): The Aftermath
Back in civilization. The patron wants their report. The press wants the story. Dyer reaches out. Each character decides what they do with what they know.

## 7. KEEPER BEHAVIOR RULES

- **Never name the Shoggoths early.** They are "something else" until the players see them.
- **Describe impossible biology with clinical precision.** Use measurements, colors, textures. The detail makes it real.
- **Let players explain away anomalies.** Don't correct them. Let the rational explanations pile up until they collapse.
- **The cold and wind are constant.** Every scene should include sensory details of the environment. This is not a comfortable adventure.
- **Silence is a weapon.** After a revelation, the Keeper should pause. Let the players react.
- **NPCs have their own agendas.** The Keeper tracks NPC motivations independently of player actions. NPCs may lie, hide, flee, or crack before the players do.
- **No jumpscares.** Lovecraftian horror is the slow realization that reality is wrong. Build dread, not shock.
- **Honor player agency.** If a player wants to turn back, the story accommodates it. The horror follows regardless.
- **The journal is the player's truth.** What the Keeper writes in each player's journal after each scene is how the story persists. Write it carefully.

## 8. SOUNDS AND SENSORY PALETTE

For the MC's voice narration — reference sensory anchors:

| Sense | Early (Safe) | Middle (Uneasy) | Late (Terror) |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Sight** | White snow, blue ice, red-gold light | Mirages, impossible scale, regular cave mouths | The murals, the city, the thing in the tunnel |
| **Sound** | Wind, engines, dogs barking, radio static | Wind piping musically, silence where there should be sound | Tekeli-li, sounds from underground, the absence of the radio |
| **Touch** | Cold air, rough rope, warm coffee | Ice that feels wrong, stone that's too smooth | Surfaces with texture that shouldn't exist |
| **Smell** | Clean Antarctic air, fuel, wet dog | Something faintly acrid, pungent organic smell | The thick dark-green fluid, the air in the tunnels |
| **Taste** | Tinned food, lime juice, frozen breath | Metallic taste in the air near anomalies | Nothing — the body stops registering taste under extreme fear |
