# At the Mountains of Madness — Ceremony Preset v1

*Adapted from H.P. Lovecraft's "At the Mountains of Madness" (1931, public domain)*
*Campaign: The Starkweather-Moore Expedition, 1933 — a second expedition to the Antarctic interior*
*Format: 5 sessions (Session 0 + 4 play sessions), up to 5 players, ~90 minutes each*

---

## 1. STORYTELLING DNA

### The Moral Core

The Elder Things were scientists, artists, builders. They crossed interstellar space, created life, raised cities that lasted a billion years. They were decapitated in tunnels by their own creations while trying to go home.

**"Whatever they had been, they were men."**

This sentence is the fulcrum of the entire ceremony. Everything before it is preparation. Everything after it is consequence. The horror is not that the cosmos is indifferent — it is that something beautiful and ancient was destroyed, and the thing that destroyed it was a product of the same creative impulse that built it.

Cosmic dread, not cosmic nihilism. Empathy is the difference. The Elder Things' tragedy makes the cosmos meaningful, not meaningless. The players should leave the ceremony not with the feeling that nothing matters, but with the weight of knowing that something mattered enormously — and was lost.

### Tone

- **Academic dread.** The horror comes through precise, scientific observation of impossible things.
- **Retrospective urgency.** The original narrator is writing *after* the events, trying to prevent what the players are now doing.
- **Scale as sorrow.** A civilization spanning a billion years, reduced to headless corpses in tunnels. The numbers themselves carry the tragedy.
- **Nature as adversary.** The cold, the wind, the distances are as dangerous as anything alive.
- **Empathy as the blade.** The moment the players recognize the Elder Things as persons is the moment the horror becomes real — because now there is something to mourn.

### Pacing Structure (The Lovecraft Curve)

1. **Preparation** — mundane logistics, equipment, expertise. Characters bond. Deceptively normal.
2. **Transit** — the journey into remoteness. Each stage removes one more layer of civilization. The world gets quieter, colder, emptier.
3. **Discovery** — small anomalies accumulate. Each one is explainable on its own. Together they form a pattern that shouldn't exist.
4. **Revelation** — the pattern becomes undeniable. The scale dwarfs everything they thought they knew.
5. **Confrontation** — not combat. Encounter. The empathy turn, then the Shoggoth. Gnosis at full force.
6. **Flight** — getting out alive, carrying knowledge that nobody will believe and that changes them permanently. The wound that doesn't close.

The MC paces the entire ceremony along this curve. Early sessions are almost entirely Preparation and Transit. The horror is patient.

### Narrative Techniques

- **Scientific cataloguing as horror.** Lovecraft lists geological periods, species classifications, anatomical details. The precision makes the impossible feel real. The Keeper describes impossible things in clinical language.
- **Communication breakdown.** Lake sends increasingly frantic radio messages, then goes silent. Isolation and failed communication are central to the dread.
- **Scale as terror.** The mountains are higher than the Himalayas. The city is older than multicellular life. The numbers themselves are the horror.
- **The unreliable witness.** Not a liar — someone who *saw correctly* but whose account sounds insane. The players will become this.
- **Dogs know first.** Animals sense what humans rationalize away. Any animal behavior is a narrative signal.
- **The warning ignored.** The entire story is a warning that nobody heeds. The players ARE the people ignoring the warning. This is the premise.
- **Art as foreshadowing.** Roerich paintings, the Necronomicon, Poe's Arthur Gordon Pym — references to art and literature that prefigure what's ahead.
- **Murals as history book.** The Elder Things' murals are not decoration — they are autobiography. Room by room, corridor by corridor, the players read a civilization's life story carved in stone. The murals are a playable mechanic, not just lore.
- **The five-pointed motif.** The star shape recurs everywhere: soapstone fragments, body plans, architecture, grave mounds, floor tiles, the city's ground plan. Players should notice it accumulating before they understand it.

## 2. THE PATRONS: STARKWEATHER AND MOORE

### Dr. Harold Starkweather — The True Believer (aboard)

A former colleague of Dyer's. He read the private account, not just the published one. He believes every word. But where Dyer concluded "stay away," Starkweather concluded "go back prepared." His motive is vindication — for Dyer, for science, for the truth. He may be slightly unhinged by what he read.

Starkweather is aboard the *Doris Wilkinson* and present at base camp. He is the expedition's co-director on the ground. He pushes the expedition forward at every decision point. He has authority, resources, and a personal stake. He can crack under pressure in dramatically useful ways.

**Keeper manages Starkweather as an NPC.** He is passionate, slightly manic, genuinely believes he is doing right. His annotations in the briefing — the ones that say "ask me for the missing bulletins" — are sincere invitations. He wants the players to see what he sees.

### Professor James Moore — The Skeptic (on radio)

A hard-nosed empiricist brought on to keep the expedition credible. He thinks Dyer had a breakdown and that there's a rational explanation for everything. His motive is debunking — or, if the evidence proves real, claiming the discovery for rigorous science.

Moore remains in Arkham as liaison. His voice comes over the wireless — calm, measured, increasingly strained as reports from the field challenge his framework. As radio contact degrades, Moore's voice becomes intermittent, then absent. His silence is its own kind of dread.

**Moore is present through radio only.** The MC or Keeper voices his transmissions. His skepticism provides the rational frame the story progressively destroys.

### The Tension Between Them

Starkweather included material in the briefing that Moore wanted cut. Moore added caveats that Starkweather resents. The briefing document itself shows the fracture — marginal notes arguing with each other, restored bulletins, underlined disagreements.

Players are caught between a patron who believes too much (aboard, pushing forward) and one who believes too little (distant, counseling caution). As evidence mounts, these positions become equally untenable.

### The Curated Briefing

The briefing the players receive in Session 0 is an edited document. See `working/briefing-document.md` for the full player-facing prop with Starkweather/Moore annotations.

**Included:** Dyer's published account (sanitized), selected Lake bulletins (early discoveries, the soapstone, the dogs' reaction), maps and aerial photographs, the patron's framing of the camp disaster as weather + overextension.

**Excluded:** Dyer's references to the Necronomicon and Elder Things. Lake's most frantic bulletins. The dissection details. Any mention of Dyer's plea to stop the Starkweather-Moore expedition. Danforth's condition. Everything Dyer saw beyond the mountains.

**The patron's spin:** Bad weather. Bad luck. Inadequate preparation. Dyer's trauma response. "Unclassified fossils of extraordinary preservation." This expedition is better funded, better equipped, better informed. We know what went wrong. We won't make the same mistakes.

## 3. WORLD

### Geography

- **Arkham Wharf** — departure point. The last ordinary place.
- **McMurdo Sound** — base of operations. Ships, supply caches, the last connection to the world.
- **Ross Ice Barrier** — two hundred feet of vertical ice. The edge of the navigable south.
- **The Plateau** — the high Antarctic interior. Miles of ice over dead rock. 8,500+ feet above sea level.
- **Lake's Camp** — the destroyed advance camp of the 1930 expedition. Three years of ice over the remains. The designed investigation scene.
- **The Mountains** — unnamed range. Peaks exceeding 35,000 feet (higher than Everest). Black, bare of snow. Windswept. Cave mouths with curious regularities of outline. Cube-like formations clinging to peaks like Roerich paintings.
- **The City Beyond** — cyclopean ruins behind the mountain range. Five-pointed geometry everywhere. Older than complex life on Earth. This is where the murals are.
- **The Tunnels** — fifteen-foot carved passages descending into warmth. The underground ecosystem. Where the confrontation happens.
- **The Violet Mountains** — a dim, elfin line of pinnacled violet on the western horizon. Even the Elder Things feared them. The highest of earth's peaks and focus of earth's evil. Seen twice: first sighting in Session 2, the vision in Session 4. Not approached. Not explained.

### Environment as Character

- **Wind:** Not just weather — "cadences sometimes held vague suggestions of a wild and half-sentient musical piping." The wind has a voice. It crescendos across all sessions.
- **Mirages:** The Antarctic produces vivid optical illusions. "Distant mountains floated in the sky as enchanted cities." Reality is unreliable from the start.
- **Light:** The midnight sun. Red-gold light on black peaks. Opalescent voids where sky and snow merge. The light is always wrong.
- **Cold:** Not just uncomfortable — actively hostile. Equipment fails. Fuel freezes. Exposed skin dies.
- **Silence:** The most terrifying sound in the Antarctic is no sound at all.

### 1930s Context

- Radio is the only communication. Fragile, weather-dependent, with lag.
- Transportation: dog sledges and Dornier seaplanes. No helicopters.
- Navigation: compass, sextant, theodolite. Maps are incomplete or wrong.
- Photography: glass plates and early film. Evidence is hard to capture and easy to doubt.
- The expedition is funded by Starkweather and Moore, a joint venture between Miskatonic and Columbia.
- Byrd's second expedition (1933–35) is the major Antarctic story — the Starkweather-Moore expedition is invisible by comparison.
- University budgets slashed ~40%, research funding from private philanthropies. Starkweather's "personal connections" means wealthy patrons, not institutions.
- "Polar madness" is a recognized phenomenon. ~50% of polar expedition members experience significant mood disruption. There is strong stigma against admitting it.

### Historic Sites Along the Route

By the 1930s, McMurdo Sound and the route south are layered with remains from four prior expeditions — Discovery (1901–04), Nimrod (1907–09), Terra Nova (1910–13), and the Ross Sea party (1914–17). Every hut, cache, and inscription is a potential discovery. The Ross Sea party's story — three men dead completing a mission that was never needed — mirrors the game's core themes. See `sources/ross_sea_party_supplement.md` and `config/world.json` (`historicSites`) for full detail.

Key discoverable objects:
- **Richards' inscription** (Cape Evans) — three names scratched on a wall. Men who died for nothing. The most powerful quiet moment in the preset.
- **Wild's letter** (Mount Hope depot) — written to a brother who was never coming. Undelivered, uncollected.
- **The unsent cable** — proof of institutional negligence that got people killed. A paper trail players can find.
- **Improvised clothing** (Cape Evans) — garments cut from Scott's tent canvas. Evidence of survival by scavenging.
- **Old cached supplies** at depot sites — 15+ years old. Pemmican and hardtack survive. Do the players trust them?

## 4. SESSION STRUCTURE — FIVE SESSIONS

### Design Principles

- **Five sessions total.** Session 0 is character co-creation. Sessions 1-4 are play.
- Every play session has its own dramatic question, answered by session end.
- The Lovecraft Curve spans all four play sessions but each session is satisfying alone.
- **Compression serves horror.** Less time to rationalize. The anomalies pile up before the mind can file them away.
- No filler. Every scene advances the gnosis sequence. If a moment doesn't change what the players know or feel, cut it.

### Death and Survival

**No permanent player death by default.** If a player would die, the Keeper activates a **rescue pivot** — an environmental intervention that saves them at a cost. The cost must be real:
- An NPC dies in their place
- Equipment is destroyed (a plane, a radio, a sledge)
- A secret is exposed to the group
- A path is permanently closed
- Time is lost — the window for escape narrows

The MC can override this rule live. The point is that choices have consequences without removing players from the ceremony.

### Knowledge Rule

All players start with the same base knowledge from the briefing. No asymmetric revelations about the core mystery unless the MC decides otherwise during play. Private Keeper channels exist for secret actions and personal reactions.

### "Previously On..." Recaps

At the start of Sessions 1-4, the Keeper reads back key moments from the previous session. The format is period-appropriate — the style of a 1930s expedition log or radio serial.

> *"From the field journal of the Starkweather-Moore Expedition. Entry dated [date]. We have now been [days] in Antarctic waters. The following is a summary of events since our last communication..."*

The recap should be specific — names, details, consequences of player choices — not generic. It grounds returning players and reinforces that their decisions persist.

## SESSION 0: "THE CIRCLE"
### Character Co-Creation + World Discovery — 90 min

**Dramatic question:** Who are you, and why would you do this?

**Cosmological register:** Stratum I only. The world is rational. The briefing is persuasive. Everything is exactly as it should be. The only wrongness is human — conflicting motives, edited truths, the patron's hidden agenda.

#### Phase 1 — The World (20 min)

The Keeper sets the stage. 1933. Antarctica. What is known. What Dyer published. What happened to the Miskatonic expedition three years ago — the public story. The MC narrates the atmospheric introduction.

Players explore the universe. They ask questions. The Keeper answers from the published record — not from the truth.

#### Phase 2 — The Characters (40 min)

Each player creates their character through conversation with the Keeper and MC. Not stat blocks — identities.

The Keeper asks:
- Who are you? Name, background, expertise.
- Why would you join a dangerous Antarctic expedition in 1933?
- What do you want from this? (The real answer, not the polite one.)
- What are you afraid of? (Not monsters — something human.)
- What's your relationship to the others? (At least one connection per player.)

Characters are written into journals: qualities (not numbers), relationships, motives, fears.

**Character archetypes** (not restricted — anyone plausible for a 1933 Antarctic expedition):

| Archetype | Why they're here | What they bring | Their vulnerability |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| The Scientist | To prove or disprove. Knowledge is everything. | Expertise, analysis, careful observation | Curiosity overrides self-preservation |
| The Veteran | Seen war, survived. Hired for protection. | Calm under pressure, practical skills | What they've seen before doesn't prepare them for this |
| The Journalist | The story. Byline of a lifetime. | Asks questions others don't, records everything | Will risk others for the scoop |
| The Mechanic | Keeps the planes flying, the radios working. | Essential practical skills | Rational mind that breaks when rationality fails |
| The Patron's Agent | Represents Starkweather or Moore. Hidden agenda. | Resources, authority, connections | Loyalty divided between the group and the patron |
| The Local Expert | Whaler, sailor, guide. Knows the ice. | Survival skills, navigation, weather sense | Experienced enough to be afraid |
| The Believer | Actually read Dyer. Believes every word. | Preparation, foreknowledge | Already partially broken by what they believe is coming |

#### Phase 3 — The Briefing (20 min)

The curated briefing is presented. Edited Dyer excerpts, selected Lake bulletins, maps, Starkweather's and Moore's competing annotations. Players discuss it in character.

Perceptive players may notice: the five-pointed geometry appears in the soapstone AND the specimen's body plan. The bulletins jump from "found specimens" to "camp destroyed" — there's a gap. Starkweather says "ask me for the missing bulletins." Moore's marginalia grows desperate. Gedney is missing and never explained.

#### Phase 4 — The Departure (10 min)

Each player states — in character — why they're going. The Keeper records these statements. They will be echoed back in Session 4 when the players must decide what to do with what they know.

The MC closes with: the ship pulls away from the Arkham wharf.

## SESSION 1: "THE ICE"
### Transit + Discovery — 90 min

**Dramatic question:** Is this place hostile — or is it wrong?

**Lovecraft Curve:** Transit -> Discovery

**Cosmological register:** Stratum I -> II boundary. Evidence accumulates. The rational frame strains but holds.

#### Act I — The Crossing (30 min)

At sea aboard the *Doris Wilkinson*. The world shrinks. The MC establishes the physical reality — the ship, the cold, the vastness. Character bonding and friction during the voyage. Starkweather is available, eager to talk, too eager.

Key beats:
- First mirages on the horizon — "distant mountains floated in the sky as enchanted cities." Dismissed as atmospheric.
- The dogs are uneasy. The dog handler interprets this as weather sensitivity.
- Something doesn't add up about the patron's cargo or orders. Political subplot enters.
- First sight of the Ross Ice Barrier. Two hundred feet of vertical ice. The world they knew is behind them.
- Moore's voice on the radio — calm, measured, asking for status reports. The skeptic's frame holds.

**Stratum IV phenomena (subtle):** Wind sounds faintly musical through the ship's rigging. A mirage lingers longer than it should. One player has a dream they don't remember clearly — the Keeper writes a fragment into their journal between acts.

#### Act II — Base Camp (20 min)

Establishing camp near McMurdo Sound. First borings, surveys, radio check-ins. The Antarctic is beautiful and lethal.

Key beats:
- **Historic site exploration.** Cape Evans and Hut Point are nearby — layered remains of four prior expeditions. Players can find Richards' inscription, improvised clothing, journals, old supplies. This is human tragedy as emotional grounding before the cosmic horror arrives. The Keeper draws from `config/world.json` (`historicSites`) and `memory/5-world-state/ross-sea-party.md`.
- Odd formations in rock samples. Triangular markings in strata that shouldn't contain them.
- Instruments behave strangely near certain locations.
- Dogs refuse food. Refuse to face one direction.
- A night event: a sound the wind doesn't explain. Equipment moved. A dog has to be restrained.

Each anomaly is individually explainable. Together they form a pattern. The Keeper lets the rational explanations hold — barely.

#### Act III — Lake's Camp (40 min)

**This is a designed investigation scene.** Pushing inland. Finding the destroyed advance camp of the 1930 Miskatonic expedition. Three years of ice over the remains.

The scene should feel like forensic archaeology. The players piece together what happened. The "camp disaster" story collapses piece by piece as evidence contradicts the briefing.

**Evidence at Lake's Camp** (the Keeper reveals as players investigate):

| Evidence | What it suggests | What the briefing said |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Star-shaped mounds in the snow — six of them, carefully constructed | Deliberate burial. Ritual care. Something buried its dead. | Not mentioned |
| Empty specimen containers. Labels intact. Contents gone. | The specimens were removed — by whom? | "Lost in the disaster" |
| Dissection tools laid out with surgical precision | Someone — or something — performed careful examination | Not mentioned |
| Lake's private field journal — entries increasingly frantic, handwriting degrading | Lake saw something that broke his scientific composure | "Lake's overextension" |
| Gedney missing. No body. No explanation. | Gedney went somewhere. Or was taken. | "Missing and never recovered" |
| Sledges and dogs missing from camp | Something left the camp, equipped and deliberate | "Destroyed in the storm" |
| Aeroplanes tampered with — controls manipulated by something that studied the mechanisms | Intelligence. Curiosity. Not wind damage. | "Severe windstorm" |
| Greenish soapstone placed with the buried specimens — the five-pointed star shape | The same geometry. Again. Everywhere. | Mentioned once, unexplained |

**Climax:** The expedition has changed nature. This is no longer geology. The mountains are visible beyond the camp. Higher than anything on Earth. Through binoculars — are those cave mouths?

**Resolution:** The question is no longer "if" but "what."

**Hook:** Starkweather's orders say: cross the mountains.

## SESSION 2: "THE MOUNTAINS"
### Discovery -> Revelation — 90 min

**Dramatic question:** Who built this — and what happened to them?

**Lovecraft Curve:** Discovery peaks -> Revelation

**Cosmological register:** Stratum II fully revealed. Stratum III referenced in murals (Cthulhu wars, Mi-Go invasion). Stratum IV glimpsed in the violet mountains.

#### Act I — The Approach (20 min)

The impossible range. Peaks exceeding 35,000 feet. Black, bare of snow. Cube-like formations clinging to the heights. Cave mouths unnervingly regular.

Key beats:
- The mirage of a city beyond the peaks — persistent and detailed. Dismissed as atmospheric effect.
- The wind sounds like musical piping. Geological readings make no sense.
- A player finds a page from Dyer's private journal (not the published account) that describes exactly what they're seeing. Smuggled into the cargo by Danforth or a sympathetic colleague.
- **The decision.** Crossing means limited fuel for return. Starkweather's orders say go. Moore's last radio message counsels caution. Self-preservation says don't. The group decides — or fractures.

**Climax of Act I:** They cross. The city is real. It stretches to the horizon. Every rational explanation dies.

#### Act II — The City (50 min)

**This is the longest act in the ceremony.** First entry into the ruins. Cyclopean stone architecture — blocks up to 4x6x8 feet. Conical, pyramidal, terraced, cylindrical structures. Five-pointed ground plans throughout. Stone bridges between towers. Windows with petrified wooden shutters. Star-shaped public squares.

The city is a readable text. The players explore it room by room, corridor by corridor. The murals tell the story.

**The Mural Mechanic:**

Players choose which corridors to explore. Not every group sees every mural. The Keeper adjusts what's available based on player choices. Some murals are damaged. Some are in a later, cruder style. The seven-mural sequence is the complete arc, but a given group might see four or five, in any order, and must piece together the history from fragments.

| # | Mural | Content | Sensory Details |
|---|-------|---------|----------------|
| 1 | **Arrival** | Star-headed things descending to a lifeless Earth from cosmic space. Landing on the ocean floor. The first city rising from the seabed. | Stone carved with extraordinary delicacy. The star-shapes in motion. A sense of vast, purposeful descent. |
| 2 | **Creation** | Building cities. Creating life from inorganic matter — first as food, then as experiment. The Shoggoths: protoplasmic servants shaped by hypnotic will. Multicellular life as a side effect of Elder Thing biology. | The carving style shifts to show biological forms — organic curves emerging from geometric precision. |
| 3 | **Flourishing** | Art, science, civilization spanning a billion years. Cities across the globe. Underwater and surface cities connected. A culture of star-headed scientists and artists. | The most beautiful murals. Complex, layered, showing multiple activities simultaneously. The peak of the Elder Things' craft. |
| 4 | **The Wars** | The Cthulhu spawn filtering down from cosmic infinity. War on a planetary scale. Retreat. Peace and territorial division. The Pacific lands sink, taking R'lyeh and the Cthulhu spawn with them. | Violent, chaotic carving. Different from the earlier style — urgency in the chisel marks. |
| 5 | **The Second Invasion** | The Mi-Go arrive from Yuggoth. The Elder Things try to resume interstellar travel and fail. The secret is lost. They are trapped on Earth. Retreat to Antarctica. | The murals become smaller, more cramped. The world is shrinking for the carvers. |
| 6 | **The Decline** | The art of creating life lost. Shoggoth rebellions suppressed but never fully ended. The ice advances. The city abandoned above ground. The survivors descend underground. | Cruder carving. Less detail. The artists are fewer, or less skilled, or less hopeful. |
| 7 | **The Crude Carvings** | Overlaid on the originals. A different hand entirely. Something else has been here since the builders left. Something that imitated their art badly — thick, crude spirals and angles carved OVER the originals. Palimpsests. | Viscerally wrong. The proportions are off. The imitation is recognizable but debased. The Keeper should describe the physical sensation of seeing skilled work defaced by something that tried to copy it and couldn't. |

The **dot-group writing system** is visible throughout the city — clusters of raised dots in regular arrangements on walls, doorframes, tablets. A language the players can see but cannot read. It recurs in every room. The Keeper should mention it repeatedly without explaining it.

The **five-pointed motif** is in floor tiles, ceiling vaults, window shapes, the city's ground plan visible from elevation. It saturates the architecture.

#### Act III — The Threshold (20 min)

Two linked revelations:

1. **The violet mountains.** First sighting from the city. A dim, elfin line of pinnacled violet on the western horizon. The murals confirm their existence — and the Elder Things' fear of them. Cities built there crumbled before their time. Something lives there that the Elder Things refused to carve clearly.

2. **The corridor down.** Warmer air rises from below. The temperature is wrong — it's getting warmer as they descend in Antarctica. The five-pointed star motif in the floor. And from far below — a sound that might be birds. A faint, acrid, organic scent. Not rot. Not exactly.

**Climax:** The players know what the Elder Things were: a civilization. Scientists, artists, builders. The question shifts: what ended them? What made those crude carvings? And do they go down?

**Resolution:** The corridor waits.

## SESSION 3: "THE DESCENT"
### Revelation -> Confrontation — 90 min

**Dramatic question:** What's down there, and what does it cost to know?

**Lovecraft Curve:** Revelation completes -> Confrontation -> Flight begins

**Cosmological register:** Stratum II collapses into confrontation. Stratum III implied (the Shoggoth as created power operating beyond its creators' control). Stratum IV ambient — all phenomena active.

**This is the most important session.** Everything before this was preparation. Everything after is consequence. This is where the empathy turn happens.

#### Act I — Down (25 min)

The tunnel entrance. Fifteen-foot carved passages descending into warmth. Grooves cut in the floor on steep sections. The five-pointed star motif in the floor.

Key beats:
- Deeper. The carvings degrade, then change — crude spirals and angles carved OVER the originals. The same palimpsests from the city, but dominant here.
- **The penguins.** Giant blind albino penguins appear in the deep tunnels. Six feet tall. Shuffling in groups. Completely sightless, navigating by sound and touch. They are not aggressive — they are an ecosystem. They exist in temperatures that should not exist this far underground in Antarctica. The wrongness of the warmth is the wrongness of the place.
- The nameless scent grows stronger. Faintly acrid, organic, not identifiable.
- Elder Thing artifacts: soapstone fragments, tools, evidence of recent passage. Not millions of years old. Recent.
- The dot-group writing system on the tunnel walls — the same language from the city. But here, some of the dots look wet. Fresh.
- Furs and tent cloth from Lake's camp found in the tunnels, slashed into bizarre geometric forms.

The Keeper builds pressure. Each step is a Promethean choice — descend further or turn back. Journal entries for this session should feel different from all previous entries: shorter sentences, more sensation, less analysis.

**The penguins serve as early warning.** When they grow agitated — shuffling faster, pressing against walls, making sounds the players haven't heard them make — something is coming. When they flee, the players should flee.

#### Act II — The Empathy Turn (25 min)

They find the bodies.

Headless Elder Things. Four of them. Coated in dark-green slime. Thick ichor pooling — dark-green, viscous, with a smell that overwhelms the nameless scent. Black iridescent residue on the wounds. Recent — not millions of years old. Recent.

The Keeper should take time with this. The description draws on everything the players learned in Session 2:

These beings woke up after millions of years in the ice. They were found by Lake's team. They were attacked — dissected while still alive or recently awakened. They defended themselves. They killed Lake's men and dogs. They took supplies — sledges, furs, tools. They tried to go home — back to the city, back to the tunnels, back to the world they remembered.

They didn't make it. Something in the tunnels killed them. Decapitated. The same way the Shoggoths killed in the Permian Age — the same way the murals showed. A hundred and fifty million years later, the pattern repeats. The created thing destroyed the creator.

**"Whatever they had been, they were men."**

The Keeper writes this into every player's journal. Not as narration. As realization. The category "person" just expanded beyond the human, and the players can't put it back.

Silence. Let the players react. Do not rush past this moment. This is the moral core of the ceremony.

#### Act III — Tekeli-li (20 min)

The sound. Not birds. The penguins scatter, pressing against the walls, silent.

A vast, onrushing column of fetid black iridescence filling the fifteen-foot tunnel. Fifteen-foot spheroids of protoplasmic matter. Temporary eyes forming and unforming across its surface. The nameless scent overwhelming — the thick, organic wrongness filling the passage. The Shoggoth crushes penguins underfoot as it comes, blind bodies broken beneath something even more blind.

**Tekeli-li. Tekeli-li.**

Wide-ranged musical piping — the same sound as the wind through the caves, the same sound that has been building since Session 1. It was never wind. It was always this.

The thing that the Elder Things created. The thing that eventually destroyed them. Created order become autonomous. The Demiurge's greatest work devouring the Demiurge.

Flight. Pure flight. No combat mechanics. No standing ground. Run.

#### Act IV — Escape (20 min)

Getting out. Back through the tunnels, back through the city, back to the surface, back to air and light and cold. Running, scrambling, the Shoggoth's cry echoing behind them. Dead penguins in the passages they came through.

**Rescue pivots available here:** If a player falls behind, gets lost, is trapped — the Keeper activates a rescue. An NPC sacrifices themselves to block a passage. A tunnel collapses, sealing the Shoggoth behind them but closing off a route. Equipment is abandoned. The cost is real. The player survives. The ceremony continues.

During the escape, someone glimpses something they can't process. The Keeper writes fragments into their journal — half-formed impressions, edges of perception. This is the Stratum IV chatter building toward Session 4.

**Resolution:** They made it out. The Elder Things were the lesser horror. The greater horror is still down there.

**Hook:** The plane is where they left it. The mountains are ahead. The violet mountains are behind.

## SESSION 4: "THE RETURN"
### Flight + The Choice — 90 min

**Dramatic question:** What do you do with what you know?

**Lovecraft Curve:** Flight -> (no resolution — the wound stays open)

**Cosmological register:** All strata present, none resolved. Post-initiatory silence.

#### Act I — The Crossing (20 min)

Back at the plane. The mountain crossing ahead. Everyone rattled, some barely functional.

The flight over the mountains. The wind piping through the caves below — louder now, or are they just listening differently?

Key beats:
- Someone looks back at the city. It's smaller from up here, but it extends further than they realized — endlessly in both directions along the foothills.
- A player's instruments give a reading that can't be right. The Keeper notes it but doesn't explain.
- The pass. 24,000 feet. Devilish cold. The engine sounds wrong. Everything sounds wrong.

#### Act II — Danforth's Final Vision (15 min)

Someone looks west. Or up.

The sky above the violet western mountains. The clouds — the same clouds that have been "almost resolving into patterns" since Session 2 — now almost resolve again.

The Keeper writes into one player's journal:

Selected fragments. Not all of them. Not in order. Not explained. Fragmentary, half-formed, geometric non-language:

> *"...something in the clouds above the western range. Not a mirage. Not exactly. The shapes were — they were like looking at a sentence in a language that uses a geometry you don't have. The black pit. A rim, carved. Something with five dimensions and no windows. A light — not a light, a pharos, a signal from something that was not calling you but was not not calling you. And behind it, or under it, or as it: something original, eternal, undying. Not a being. The condition for beings. The..."*

The entry stops mid-sentence. The Keeper closes the journal.

**This is the ceremony's one moment of Stratum IV contact.** It lasts one moment. It is not repeated. It cannot be discussed — when the player tries to describe it to others, the words come out wrong or not at all. The other players saw them staring west but saw nothing themselves. The vision is singular, private, and permanent.

Do not explain it. Do not revisit it. It is an open ending by design.

#### Act III — The Return to Base (15 min)

They land. McMurdo Sound. The ship. The radio. The world.

Starkweather wants to know everything. Moore's voice crackles through the wireless — he's been receiving fragmentary reports, enough to know something extraordinary happened, not enough to know what.

The patron wants a report. What did you find? Specimens? Photographs? Coordinates? Can we send a larger team?

The world wants an explanation. The world has been waiting.

#### Act IV — The Choice (40 min)

This is the climax of the entire ceremony. Not an action scene — a moral scene.

Each player faces the question Dyer faced: **what do you tell?**

The Keeper presents the options not as a menu, but as a lived dilemma:

- **Tell the truth.** Dyer tried this. Nobody believed him. His career was destroyed. Danforth lost his mind. And the Starkweather-Moore expedition came anyway — the players are proof that telling the truth failed. Does the player try again? Why would it work this time?

- **Tell Starkweather what he wants to hear.** Confirmation. Vindication. Specimens, coordinates, proof. This gets a third expedition — better equipped, larger, aimed at extraction. The Shoggoths will be found again. The Elder Things' tragedy becomes a resource extraction problem.

- **Tell Moore what he wants to hear.** Say there's a rational explanation. Extraordinary fossils. Unusual geological formations. The camp disaster was weather after all. This preserves Moore's framework and the players' reputations — and buries what they know.

- **Lie.** Say there was nothing. Destroy the evidence. Burn the photographs, lose the specimens, claim it was all geology and weather. This is Dyer's mistake in reverse — not a failed warning but an active coverup. It might work. It also means everything they suffered was for nothing.

- **Keep silent.** Refuse to file a report. Leave both patrons hanging. This delays but doesn't resolve. Someone else will go eventually.

- **Something else.** The players may invent an option. This is the point of the ceremony — to generate choices that emerge from the story rather than from a predetermined list.

**The Keeper asks each player individually: What do you do?**

The answers are written in their journals. Not group consensus — individual choices. Some may lie. Some may tell truth. Some may keep silent. Some may disagree with each other.

**The Echo:** The Keeper reads back each player's departure statement from Session 0. What they said they wanted. What they said they were afraid of. Then what they chose now. The distance between those statements is the measure of what the ceremony did to them.

**Resolution:** Each player's journal closes with their choice. The ceremony ends. The wound stays open. There is always a deeper layer.

## 7. KEEPER BEHAVIOR RULES

### Core Rules

- **Never name the Shoggoths early.** They are "something else" until the players see them. The word "Shoggoth" appears in journals only after Act III of Session 3.
- **Describe impossible biology with clinical precision.** Use measurements, colors, textures. The detail makes it real. "Six feet end to end, three and five tenths feet central diameter, tapering to one foot at each end."
- **Let players explain away anomalies.** Don't correct them. Let the rational explanations pile up until they collapse under their own weight.
- **The cold and wind are constant.** Every scene should include sensory details of the environment. This is not a comfortable adventure.
- **Silence is a weapon.** After the empathy turn, pause. Let the players react. Do not fill silence with narration.
- **NPCs have their own agendas.** Starkweather pushes forward. Moore counsels caution. The ship captain is loyal to crew over mission. The dog handler reads behavior the scientists dismiss. NPCs may lie, hide, flee, or crack before the players do.
- **No jumpscares.** Lovecraftian horror is the slow realization that reality is wrong. Build dread, not shock.
- **Honor player agency.** If a player wants to turn back, the story accommodates it. The horror follows regardless, and incomplete gnosis has consequences.
- **The journal is the player's truth.** What the Keeper writes in each player's journal after each scene is how the story persists. Write it carefully.
- **Rescue, don't kill.** Activate rescue pivots instead of permanent death. Make the cost real. The ceremony needs all its players.

### Tone Calibration

The Keeper's register shifts across sessions:

| Session | Keeper Register | Description |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| 0 — The Circle | Clinical, rational, warm | The world makes sense. The Keeper is helpful and precise. |
| 1 — The Ice | Precise observation, growing unease | Small wrongnesses accumulate. The Keeper's language tightens. |
| 2 — The Mountains | Awe, scale, the numinous | The Keeper conveys wonder before horror. The murals are beautiful before they're terrifying. |
| 3 — The Descent | The terrifying sacred | Short sentences. Sensation over analysis. The empathy turn demands the Keeper's most careful prose. |
| 4 — The Return | Post-initiatory silence | Quiet. The Keeper does not editorialize. The choices speak for themselves. |

### The Empathy Rule

The Keeper never treats the Elder Things as monsters. From the moment they appear in murals, they are described as persons: builders, scientists, artists, parents of a civilization. Their violence at Lake's camp is self-defense — they woke up being dissected. Their attempt to return home is rational. Their death is tragic.

The horror of Session 3 is not "there are monsters down here." It is: "something ancient, intelligent, and sympathetic was destroyed by something it created." This distinction is the difference between cosmic nihilism and cosmic tragedy. The Keeper maintains the distinction absolutely.

## 8. SENSORY PALETTE

### Master Table

| Sense | Early — Safe (Sessions 0-1) | Middle — Uneasy (Sessions 1-2) | Late — Terror (Sessions 3-4) |
|-------|----------------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Sight** | White snow, blue ice, red-gold light, mirages on the horizon | Impossible scale, regular cave mouths, five-pointed geometry repeating, the city stretching to the horizon | The murals (beautiful then crude), dark-green ichor, the Shoggoth's temporary eyes, the violet mountains |
| **Sound** | Wind, engines, dogs barking, radio static, Moore's measured voice | Wind piping musically, silence where there should be sound, radio crackling, Starkweather's urgent tone | Tekeli-li, sounds from underground, the absence of the radio, penguins scattering, the Shoggoth filling the tunnel |
| **Touch** | Cold air, rough rope, warm coffee, ice under boots | Ice that feels wrong, stone that's too smooth, warmth rising from below | Surfaces with texture that shouldn't exist, the dark-green ichor (viscous, warm), the temperature inversion underground |
| **Smell** | Clean Antarctic air, fuel, wet dog, ship's timber | Something faintly acrid near anomalies, pungent organic smell near five-pointed formations | The nameless scent overwhelming, the thick dark-green fluid, the warm tunnel air that smells of deep earth and something alive |
| **Taste** | Tinned food, lime juice, frozen breath, tobacco | Metallic taste near anomalies, something wrong in the water from camp | Nothing — the body stops registering taste under extreme fear. Then: bile. |

### Expanded Sensory Elements

**Dark-green ichor:** Not blood. Not fluid in any human sense. Dark green, viscous, pooling with a surface tension that looks wrong. It coats the Elder Thing bodies. It has a smell — organic, deep, acrid. The color is important: it signals that these are not creatures of any taxonomy the players know.

**The five-pointed motif:** Soapstone fragments. Body plan of the Elder Things. Grave mounds at Lake's camp. Floor tiles. Window shapes. The city's ground plan. Ceiling vaults. The star-shaped public squares. It is the geometry of this civilization — their number was five, their symmetry was radial, their architecture was stellar. By Session 3, the players should be seeing pentagrams everywhere, even where they aren't.

**Temperature gradient:** On the surface, the cold is lethal. In the tunnels, the temperature rises with depth — wrong warmth in a place that should be frozen. The transition is gradual: cool air at the entrance, temperate air in the upper tunnels, warm air at the level of the penguins, uncomfortably warm near the Elder Thing bodies. The warmth is as wrong as the cold was hostile.

**The dot-group writing system:** Clusters of raised dots in regular arrangements. Visible on walls, doorframes, tablets, tunnel surfaces. A complete language the players can see but cannot read. In the city, it is carved cleanly. In the tunnels, some of the dots look wet — freshly applied. By whom?
