# Lore Expansion Brief — At the Mountains of Madness

*Context document for collaborative world-building. Self-contained — everything you need is here.*

---

## 1. What This Is

We are building an interactive story adaptation of H.P. Lovecraft's *At the Mountains of Madness* (1931, public domain). The premise: a **second expedition** to the Antarctic interior in 1933 — the Starkweather-Moore Expedition — follows in the footsteps of the disastrous Miskatonic University Expedition of 1930-31.

The format is a live storytelling experience. A human MC narrates the story with voice. Up to five players interact through a text companion. An AI agent called the Keeper manages all game state — tracking what players know, what NPCs do, what the world reveals.

The story runs across **five sessions** (~90 minutes each): one character co-creation session and four play sessions. No dice. No stat blocks. Characters have qualities, relationships, motives, and fears — not numbers. There is no permanent player death; consequences take other forms.

**This conversation is about expanding the world.** We need to deepen two layers of lore: the real-world history that inspires our fiction, and the in-game knowledge that the 1933 characters carry. We're doing story design here — inventing, researching, making decisions about what's true in our world.

## 2. Moral Core and Tone

These are **locked.** Everything else in the story bends to serve them.

### The Fulcrum

The Elder Things were scientists, artists, builders. They crossed interstellar space, created life, raised cities that lasted a billion years. They were decapitated in tunnels by their own creations while trying to go home.

**"Whatever they had been, they were men."**

This sentence is the fulcrum of the entire story. Everything before it is preparation. Everything after it is consequence. The horror is not that the cosmos is indifferent — it is that something beautiful and ancient was destroyed, and the thing that destroyed it was a product of the same creative impulse that built it.

### The Tone

- **Cosmic dread, not cosmic nihilism.** Empathy is the difference. The Elder Things' tragedy makes the cosmos meaningful, not meaningless. Players should leave not with the feeling that nothing matters, but with the weight of knowing that something mattered enormously — and was lost.
- **Academic dread.** The horror comes through precise, scientific observation of impossible things. Clinical language makes the impossible feel real.
- **Scale as sorrow.** A civilization spanning a billion years, reduced to headless corpses in tunnels. The numbers themselves carry the tragedy.
- **Nature as adversary.** The cold, the wind, the distances are as dangerous as anything alive. The Antarctic is not a backdrop — it is a character.
- **Patient horror.** The anomalies accumulate slowly. Each one is individually explainable. Together they form a pattern that shouldn't exist. Less time to rationalize means the dread builds in the body, not just the intellect.
- **Compression serves horror.** No filler. Every scene advances the story. If a moment doesn't change what the players know or feel, cut it.

### What This Means for Lore

Every piece of world-building we create should serve this tone. Real-world details aren't window dressing — they're the rational frame that the story progressively destroys. The more precise and grounded the 1933 world feels, the more devastating the impossible becomes when it arrives.

## 3. What Is Established

Everything in this section is **locked** — decided, canonical, not open for revision. New lore must be consistent with all of it.

### 3a. The First Expedition (1930-31)

The Miskatonic University Expedition was funded as a geological survey. Its purpose was securing deep-level rock and soil specimens from the Antarctic continent, using a novel portable drilling apparatus designed by Professor Frank Pabodie.

**Personnel:** Professor William Dyer (geology, expedition leader), Professor Pabodie (engineering), Dr. Frank Lake (biology), Professor Atwood (physics and meteorology), seven graduate assistants, nine skilled mechanics. Total party of twenty.

**Equipment:** Four large Dornier aeroplanes modified for high-altitude polar flight. Fifty-five Alaskan sledge dogs. Portable wireless sets. Pabodie's drilling apparatus — lightweight, collapsible, capable of boring to one thousand feet through varying strata.

**Ships:** The brig *Arkham* (Captain Douglas) and barque *Miskatonic* (Captain Thorfinnssen).

**Key events:** During routine boring, anomalous triangular striated fossil impressions were found in slate — suggesting biological origin from strata predating all known complex life. This prompted Dr. Lake to establish a forward sub-expedition to the northwest, where a previously unknown mountain range had been sighted — peaks estimated to exceed 35,000 feet. Lake's party: eleven men, four students, and a complement of sledge dogs.

Lake's camp yielded extraordinary biological specimens — barrel-shaped, eight feet long, six-ridged, with seven-foot membranous wings, five-pointed starfish-shaped heads with prismatic cilia, and glassy red-irised eyes. The dogs reacted violently to the specimens. Lake's radio bulletins grew increasingly excited and frantic.

**The disaster:** Communication from the forward camp ceased. Dyer's relief party found the camp destroyed. Eleven men dead. One man — Gedney — missing, never recovered. Severe windstorm conditions were recorded. But the evidence did not match wind damage: aeroplanes tampered with, sledges missing, star-shaped mounds carefully constructed over six of the specimens.

**What Dyer saw:** Dyer and his graduate student Danforth crossed the mountains alone by air. They found a cyclopean city of impossible age, murals that told the history of Earth's true first civilization, tunnels descending into warmth, and evidence that the specimens were not ancient fossils but beings that had been frozen and revived. Dyer published an account warning against further Antarctic exploration. Nobody believed him. His career was effectively destroyed. Danforth suffered a breakdown and was committed to a sanitarium.

### 3b. The Second Expedition (1933)

The Starkweather-Moore Expedition. A joint venture between Miskatonic University and Columbia University — though in practice, Miskatonic has quietly distanced itself.

**The Patrons:**

- **Dr. Harold Starkweather** — The True Believer. A former colleague of Dyer's who read the *private* account, not just the published one. He believes every word. Where Dyer concluded "stay away," Starkweather concluded "go back prepared." His motive is vindication — for Dyer, for science, for the truth. He is slightly unhinged by what he read. **He is aboard the expedition**, serving as co-director on the ground.

- **Professor James Moore** — The Skeptic. A hard-nosed empiricist brought on to keep the expedition credible. He thinks Dyer had a breakdown and that there's a rational explanation for everything. His motive is debunking — or, if the evidence proves real, claiming the discovery for rigorous science. **He remains in Arkham**, communicating by radio. His voice grows increasingly strained as field reports challenge his framework. When radio degrades, he falls silent.

**The tension:** The briefing document the players receive was edited by both patrons — Starkweather restored bulletins Moore wanted cut, Moore added rationalizing caveats Starkweather resents. The document itself shows the fracture through competing handwritten annotations.

**Vessel:** Cargo steamer *Doris Wilkinson* (Captain R. Thorsen), 187 feet, steel-hulled, reinforced, ice-capable.

**Departure:** Arkham wharf, September 2, 1933. Route: Arkham → Auckland → Ross Sea → McMurdo Sound. Approximately six weeks at sea.

**Equipment:** Three Dornier aeroplanes (modified per Pabodie specifications, improved fuel-warming, increased cargo capacity, one with aerial camera). Forty Alaskan sledge dogs, acquired from the same breeder who supplied the first expedition. Three portable wireless sets with improved range. Two Pabodie-type drilling apparatus. Geological, biological, photographic, and surveying equipment.

**Personnel:** Starkweather (co-director, aboard), Moore (co-director, Arkham liaison), the players (up to 5), ship's crew of twelve, four mechanics, two wireless operators. The dog handler is an NPC. The radio operator is an NPC.

**Dyer's status:** Excluded from the expedition. Not consulted. His career is over — the scientific establishment considers him traumatized. He is writing an uncensored private account. He may attempt to contact the expedition through smuggled letters or intercepted radio messages.

**Danforth's status:** In a sanitarium. He saw something during the flight over the mountains that broke him. His whispered references — Leng, the Lake of Hali, Magellanic Clouds, Yog-Sothoth — are never fully explained. Players may not know he exists, or may only know he's "convalescing."

### 3c. Story Structure

**Five sessions:**
- **Session 0 — "The Circle":** Character co-creation. Players receive the curated briefing. Depart.
- **Session 1 — "The Ice":** Sea crossing, base camp, Lake's destroyed camp. *Dramatic question: Is this place hostile — or is it wrong?*
- **Session 2 — "The Mountains":** Mountain approach, crossing decision, the city, the murals. *Dramatic question: Who built this — and what happened to them?*
- **Session 3 — "The Descent":** Tunnel descent, the empathy turn, the Shoggoth. *Dramatic question: What's down there, and what does it cost to know?*
- **Session 4 — "The Return":** Mountain crossing, the vision, the moral choice. *Dramatic question: What do you do with what you know?*

**Mechanics:** No dice. Quality-based — characters have qualities, not stats. No permanent player death — if a player would die, the Keeper activates a *rescue pivot* (an NPC dies, equipment is lost, a path is closed, a secret is exposed). Choices have consequences without removing players from the story.

**The final question:** Each player faces the same dilemma Dyer faced: *What do you tell?* Tell the truth (Dyer tried — nobody believed him). Tell the patron what they want to hear (guarantees a third expedition). Lie (active coverup — might work, means everything was for nothing). Keep silent (delays but doesn't resolve). Or something else the story invents.

### 3d. Cosmology (Condensed)

Reality in this world has depth. Four strata, each containing intelligences incomprehensible to the layer below.

**Stratum I — The Material World.** Humans, known biology, consensus physics. The outermost shell. Not false — incomplete. This is where the story begins.

**Stratum II — The Demiurgic Order.** Elder Things and Shoggoths. Material but alien. Biological. Mortal. Tragic. The Elder Things are the master builders — interstellar travelers, city architects, life creators. The Shoggoths are their creation: protoplasmic servants shaped by hypnotic will, which eventually gained autonomy and destroyed their makers. *This is the stratum the players encounter directly.*

**Stratum III — The Spectral Powers.** Cthulhu and spawn, Mi-Go, and others. Matter unlike ours. Liminal. Transformative. Referenced in the Elder Things' murals as ancient wars — Cthulhu's spawn arrived from cosmic infinity, the Mi-Go from Yuggoth. The Elder Things lost interstellar travel fighting these wars and became trapped on Earth. *This stratum is historical, revealed through murals — not directly encountered.*

**Stratum IV — The Abyss / The Open Sky.** Azathoth, the violet mountains, the unnamed. Beyond form. Beyond comprehension. The sky itself becomes a medium — mirages that carry information, wind that sounds like musical piping, cloud formations that almost resolve into patterns. *Encountered once: the vision in Session 4, fragments written into one player's journal, mid-sentence, unexplained.*

**Key principle:** Higher strata don't appear directly. They *bleed* into lower strata as anomaly, wrongness, distortion. Wind that sounds musical. Equipment that fails near five-pointed structures. Dreams containing information the dreamer shouldn't have. The cold that is too organized to be weather.

### 3e. Key Decisions

All locked:

- **Patron configuration:** Option B (True Believer + Skeptic). Starkweather aboard, Moore on radio.
- **Session count:** Five (Session 0 + four play). Replaces an earlier eight-session design. Density over runway.
- **Names:** All names canon from Lovecraft — Dyer, Lake, Pabodie, Danforth, Gedney, Atwood, Douglas, Thorfinnssen. First names for Starkweather (Harold) and Moore (James) are our invention.
- **The dogs:** Forty Alaskan sledge dogs from the same breeder who supplied the first expedition. This detail is established. *The reason they're from the same breeder is open.*
- **No dice, no stats.** Quality-based system. Journal format.
- **No permanent death.** Rescue pivots instead.

## 4. Two Kinds of Lore We Need

We need to build out two distinct categories of world knowledge. They serve different purposes and follow different rules.

### [DESIGN] — Design-Inspiring Lore

Real-world facts, historical context, and period detail that we as story designers use to make the fiction feel grounded. This is our reference material — the texture of 1933.

Examples:
- What other Antarctic expeditions were active in 1933? (Byrd's second expedition departed the same year.)
- How did Depression-era funding work for scientific expeditions? Who paid?
- What were Dornier seaplanes actually like? Range, crew capacity, reliability in cold?
- What did transatlantic radio communication look like in 1933? What could you say, how fast, how far?
- What was Lovecraft himself doing in 1931-1933? (He was writing the novella. Does his real-world context feed our fiction?)
- What was 1930s academic culture at institutions like Miskatonic (modeled on Brown, Lovecraft's milieu)?

These facts may never appear directly in the story, but they inform our design decisions and keep us honest about what's plausible.

### [IN-GAME] — Diegetic Lore

Things the characters in the story know, could discover, or take for granted. This is the world as experienced from inside 1933.

Examples:
- What would an educated American in 1933 know about Antarctica? What did the newspapers report about the first expedition?
- What does Miskatonic University's administration say about Dyer now? Academic gossip.
- What survival knowledge would a 1933 Antarctic expedition member have? What did they know about frostbite, altitude, nutrition?
- What current events would be on the characters' minds during the sea crossing? (Roosevelt's first hundred days, the Reichstag fire, the Dust Bowl beginning.)
- What would the dog handler know about the breeder who supplied both expeditions?

These details directly enrich gameplay — they're the fabric of character conversation, period atmosphere, and the rational frame that the cosmic horror will destroy.

## 5. Identified Gaps

These are the specific areas where we need lore expansion. Each gap states what's LOCKED around it (don't contradict) and what's OPEN (free to invent or research).

### Design Gaps

**Gap D1: Other 1933 Antarctic Expeditions**
LOCKED: Byrd's second expedition (1933-35) is the major Antarctic story. Starkweather-Moore is too small to attract press attention.
OPEN: What was Byrd doing? Other national programs? Overlap in logistics, ports, or personnel?

**Gap D2: Depression-Era Expedition Funding**
LOCKED: Funded through Starkweather's personal connections and Moore's institutional contacts — not Miskatonic directly.
OPEN: How did 1933 expeditions get funded? What did it cost? Government or military interests in Antarctic exploration?

**Gap D3: Real Antarctic Logistics**
LOCKED: McMurdo Sound base. Dog sledges and Dornier seaplanes. Six-week crossing via Auckland. Pabodie drilling apparatus.
OPEN: Logistics chain, daily routines, food, fuel storage, medical supplies, costs in 1933 dollars.

**Gap D4: 1933 Communication Technology**
LOCKED: Three portable wireless sets. Relay through ship and Arkham. Degrades south of ~60 degrees. Beyond the mountains, functionally impossible.
OPEN: Frequencies, equipment brands, weight, actual range, transmission duration, what radio *sounded like*.

**Gap D5: Dornier Seaplane Specifications**
LOCKED: Three Dorniers, modified per Pabodie specs. First expedition used four "large Dornier aeroplanes."
OPEN: Which model? (The Wal? Do J?) Range, ceiling, crew capacity, fuel load, polar performance, fuel calculus for a 24,000-foot mountain crossing.

**Gap D6: Lovecraft's Own Context**
LOCKED: Novella written 1931, serialized in *Astounding Stories* 1936.
OPEN: What was Lovecraft reading and thinking in 1931-33? His correspondence, real expedition influences, relationship with Brown University.

**Gap D7: 1930s Academic Culture**
LOCKED: Miskatonic (based on Brown/Providence). Columbia as Moore's institution. Dyer dismissed as traumatized.
OPEN: Academic politics, expedition recruitment culture, how universities handled public embarrassment, Ivy vs non-Ivy dynamics.

**Gap D8: Period Media and Public Knowledge**
LOCKED: Lake's bulletins relayed through Arkham Advertiser and AP. Camp disaster was public. Dyer's account dismissed.
OPEN: What newspapers printed in 1931, how 1933 Americans consumed Antarctic news, sensationalist accounts, how quickly the story faded.

### In-Game Gaps

**Gap G1: Gedney's Fate**
LOCKED: Listed as missing, never recovered. No body. No explanation offered in the briefing.
OPEN: What happened to Gedney? Was he taken by the Elder Things? Did he flee? Is he still alive somewhere? This is a major narrative thread — the answer (or deliberate non-answer) carries weight.

**Gap G2: Dyer's Private Account**
LOCKED: Dyer wrote a private account beyond the published one. Starkweather read it. It describes what Dyer saw beyond the mountains. Dyer is writing an uncensored version in 1933.
OPEN: What specifically is in the private account that isn't in the published one? What details does Starkweather know that the players don't? How much of the truth did Dyer commit to paper?

**Gap G3: Lake's Final Hours**
LOCKED: Lake's later bulletins were frantic, increasingly excited, cut off by the "storm." Bulletins 6-9 were omitted from the briefing. Lake found and began dissecting a specimen. The specimens revived and killed his party.
OPEN: What specifically did Lake's final transmissions say? What was the sequence of events at the camp — hour by hour? What did dissection reveal? When exactly did the specimens revive? What did Lake understand in his last moments?

**Gap G4: Miskatonic's Response to Dyer**
LOCKED: Miskatonic distanced itself from Dyer's claims. His career is effectively over. The scientific establishment treats his account as trauma-induced confabulation.
OPEN: How did the distancing happen? Was there a formal inquiry? Did any colleagues believe him privately? What is Dyer's daily life like in 1933? Does he still teach? Does he have allies?

**Gap G5: Danforth's Condition**
LOCKED: In a sanitarium. Saw something during the flight that broke him. His whispered list includes real Lovecraft references (Leng, Lake of Hali, Magellanic Clouds, Yog-Sothoth). Players may not know he exists.
OPEN: What exactly is Danforth's condition? What does he say to his doctors? Has anyone visited him? Could someone reach him? What sanitarium — and what does 1933 mental health care look like?

**Gap G6: The Dog Handler and the Breeder**
LOCKED: Forty dogs from the same breeder who supplied the first expedition. The dog handler is an NPC aboard.
OPEN: Who is this breeder? Why would you go back to the same breeder whose dogs were all killed? What does the dog handler know about the first expedition's dogs? Is there a story here — superstition, loyalty, economy, something darker? What is the handler's name, background, personality?

**Gap G7: Starkweather-Moore Partnership Origin**
LOCKED: Starkweather is a former colleague of Dyer's. Moore is a hard-nosed empiricist from Columbia. They are co-patrons with complementary resources and a shared conviction.
OPEN: How did they find each other? What brought a True Believer and a Skeptic into partnership? Who approached whom? What was the negotiation like? Is there a specific incident that catalyzed the expedition?

**Gap G8: The Public Story of the First Expedition**
LOCKED: The camp disaster was attributed to a severe windstorm. Dyer published an account warning against further exploration. Nobody believed him. The specimens were described as "unclassified fossils of extraordinary preservation."
OPEN: What exactly does the public know? What did the newspapers print in 1931? Were there sensational theories? Conspiracy theories? Did the "eleven dead, one missing" get major coverage or was it buried? How does the general public in 1933 remember the expedition — if they remember it at all?

**Gap G9: The Geographic Error**
LOCKED: Mountains exceeding 35,000 feet. A city older than complex life. A range of violet mountains on the western horizon. The geography as presented in the story doesn't match real Antarctic geography.
OPEN: How do we handle this? Lovecraft's geography is physically impossible in the real Antarctic. Do we acknowledge this as part of the cosmic wrongness? Do we ground the early expedition in real coordinates and then let the geography become impossible once the mountains appear? What real Antarctic locations anchor the plausible portion of the route?

**Gap G10: Dyer's 1933 Daily Life**
LOCKED: Career destroyed. Writing uncensored account. Excluded from the new expedition.
OPEN: What does Dyer's life look like day to day? Does he still live in Arkham? Still affiliated with Miskatonic in any capacity? Married? Friends? How old is he? What does a disgraced 1933 professor do with his time? This matters because Dyer may try to contact the expedition — and because his private account may reach the players.

**Gap G11: Dogs from the Same Breeder**
LOCKED: The detail exists in the briefing. It is stated as fact.
OPEN: Why? This feels like it should mean something. Possibilities: economical (the breeder is the only reliable source in the region), sentimental (Starkweather's choice, a symbolic link to the first expedition), practical (proven Antarctic working dogs), ominous (the dogs carry something, remember something, react to something). We need to decide what this detail *means* — or consciously leave it as an unexplained resonance.

## 6. Prompts to Start

Three tiers, from broad to narrow. Pick any entry point.

### Tier 1 — Broad World-Building

- **The real Antarctic world of 1933.** What expeditions were active? What was the state of polar science? What did the international community know about the Antarctic interior? How did the real Byrd expedition (departing the same year) compare in scale, funding, and ambition to what we're building?

- **Depression-era science funding.** How did universities fund expeditions during the worst economic crisis in history? What role did wealthy patrons play? Military interests? Media deals? What would Moore's Columbia connections actually provide? What would "Starkweather's personal connections" look like in practice?

- **The public story of the Miskatonic disaster.** Eleven men dead, one missing, in Antarctica. This is a real news story in 1931. What would the AP coverage look like? How long does it stay in the papers? What theories does the public form? When a respected professor publishes an account that sounds insane, how does 1931 American media handle it?

### Tier 2 — Specific Research

- **Dornier seaplanes in polar service.** Which model fits our story? What are the real specs — range, ceiling, cold-weather performance? How did the real Byrd expedition use aircraft? What modifications would a polar Dornier need? What's the fuel arithmetic for crossing a mountain range at 24,000 feet?

- **Lake's final hours.** We need to build the hour-by-hour sequence at the forward camp. From the discovery of the specimens through the dissection through the revival through the slaughter. What does Lake understand as it happens? What are his final words?

- **Dyer in 1933.** A disgraced professor in Depression-era New England. What does his world look like? His daily routine, his living situation, his state of mind. He's writing the uncensored account — what drives him? Who, if anyone, stands by him?

- **Danforth's whispers.** Lovecraft gives us a list of fragments Danforth muttered — real Mythos references mixed with apparent nonsense. Which of these have meaning in our cosmology? What did Danforth *actually see* during the flight that broke him?

### Tier 3 — Hard Choices

- **What happened to Gedney?** This is the most loaded gap. The first expedition's one missing man. No body, no explanation. Was he taken by the Elder Things? Did he flee into the mountains alone? Is he still alive somewhere in the tunnels, three years later? Or did something else happen entirely? The answer (or the decision not to answer) shapes the entire mystery.

- **Why did Starkweather and Moore partner?** A True Believer and a Skeptic jointly funding an expedition to a place one of them thinks is cosmically dangerous and the other thinks is merely geologically interesting. What brings these two together? What's the deal — the actual negotiation? And what does each one *not* tell the other?

- **The dogs.** From the same breeder. Why does this matter? Is the breeder significant? Do the dogs carry genetic memory, behavioral conditioning, something stranger? Or is this a purely mundane detail that players will over-interpret — and should we let them?

## 7. A Note on Lovecraft

H.P. Lovecraft was a racist. His personal letters and some of his fiction contain virulent racism that was extreme even by the standards of his era. This is historical fact, not a contested interpretation.

Our adaptation acknowledges this without centering it. We are not adapting Lovecraft's worldview — we are adapting his best idea: that the universe is vast, ancient, and populated by intelligences that dwarf human comprehension, and that encountering this truth is transformative and irreversible.

The story's moral core — "Whatever they had been, they were men" — is an expansion of personhood, not a restriction. Our adaptation expands who belongs on a 1933 Antarctic expedition. Not anachronistically — the 1930s had its barriers and we don't pretend otherwise — but with awareness that a story about *recognizing the personhood of radically alien beings* should not be told by a cast that excludes most of humanity.

Empathy, not despair. Expansion, not contraction. The cosmos is vast, and so is the table.
