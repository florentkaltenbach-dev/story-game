# Starkweather-Moore — The Patrons

*Working document. Provisional.*

---

## What Lovecraft Gives Us

Nothing except the name. "The Starkweather-Moore Expedition" is mentioned four times. No first names, no institution, no physical descriptions, no motives beyond "they want to follow the same plan." They exist only as the unnamed future that Dyer is trying to prevent.

This is our blank canvas.

---

## The Premise

Starkweather and Moore are two people with complementary resources and a shared conviction: that Dyer found something real, and that it's worth going back for. They disagree on *why* it's worth going back.

They are the co-patrons of the expedition. They fund it, organize it, and recruit the players. They curate the briefing materials — selecting which parts of Dyer's account to include and which to suppress. They are not on the expedition themselves (or are they? — MC option below).

---

## Profile Options

The MC chooses one configuration. Each creates a different political tension.

### Option A: The Academic and the Opportunist

**Professor William Starkweather** — a geologist or paleontologist at a rival university (perhaps Columbia, Yale, or the University of Chicago). Respected, ambitious, approaching the end of his career without a defining discovery. He read Dyer's account as a scientist: if even half of it is true, this is the most significant find in the history of geology. He wants evidence, peer-reviewed and irrefutable. His motive is legacy.

**Philip Moore** — a wealthy industrialist or media magnate. Not a scientist himself but a collector of the extraordinary. He read Dyer's account as an investor: biological specimens of unprecedented nature, technology that defies known physics, a city older than complex life. His motive is acquisition — specimens, photographs, artifacts, exclusive rights to the story. He's funding most of the expedition. The money comes with strings.

**The tension:** Starkweather wants careful, methodical study. Moore wants results, trophies, headlines. The players are caught between thoroughness and speed, science and exploitation. As things get dangerous, these motives diverge violently.

### Option B: The True Believer and the Skeptic

**Dr. Harold Starkweather** — a former colleague of Dyer's. He read the private account, not just the published one. He believes every word. But where Dyer concluded "stay away," Starkweather concluded "go back prepared." His motive is vindication — for Dyer, for science, for the truth. He may be slightly unhinged by what he read.

**Professor James Moore** — a hard-nosed empiricist brought on to keep the expedition credible. He thinks Dyer had a breakdown and that there's a rational explanation for everything. His motive is debunking — or, if the evidence proves real, claiming the discovery for rigorous science. He's the skeptic who keeps the expedition grounded. Until the evidence overwhelms him.

**The tension:** One patron has too much faith, the other too little. The players receive a briefing shaped by two contradictory worldviews — Starkweather included material that Moore wanted cut, and Moore added caveats that Starkweather resents. The briefing document itself shows the fracture.

### Option C: The Institution

**Starkweather-Moore** is not two individuals but a committee or foundation — perhaps a joint venture between a university and a government agency (the Navy has Antarctic interests in the 1930s). The name represents institutional will, not personal ambition.

**The tension:** Bureaucratic. The expedition has multiple stakeholders with classified objectives. The players report to a structure, not a person. Orders arrive by radio and may contradict each other. The patron is faceless, which is its own kind of horror — nobody is personally responsible for sending these people into danger.

## The Curated Briefing

Regardless of which patron option is chosen, the briefing the players receive in Session 1 is an edited document. It contains:

### Included

- Dyer's published account — the sanitized version describing the expedition's goals, route, equipment, and the "camp disaster"
- Selected Lake bulletins — the early geological findings, the anomalous fossils, enough to establish scientific interest. The bulletins describing the full biological specimens may be included or withheld (MC choice)
- Maps and aerial photographs from the first expedition — the mountain range confirmed
- Newspaper coverage from 1931 — the public story
- The patron's own analysis framing the expedition as a follow-up geological survey with enhanced equipment

### Excluded (what the patron left out)

- Dyer's references to the Necronomicon, Elder Things, and Cthulhu cult — dismissed as evidence of his breakdown
- The most frantic of Lake's bulletins — the ones where his language becomes desperate and unscientific
- Any mention of Dyer's plea to stop the Starkweather-Moore expedition specifically
- Danforth's condition — the players may not know Danforth exists, or may only know he's "convalescing"

### The Patron's Spin

The briefing frames the first expedition's tragedy as:
- Bad weather and inadequate preparation (partly true)
- Lake's overextension without proper support (partly true)
- Dyer's subsequent psychological deterioration as trauma response (plausible but wrong)
- The anomalous specimens as "unclassified fossils of extraordinary preservation" (technically accurate, deeply misleading)

The briefing concludes: this expedition is better funded, better equipped, and better informed. We know what went wrong. We won't make the same mistakes.

This is, of course, exactly the kind of rational confidence the Lovecraft Curve is designed to destroy.

## Are They On the Ship?

Three options for the MC:

**Option 1: They stay behind.** Starkweather and/or Moore remain in the US. Communication is by radio. Orders arrive with delay. The players have autonomy that increases as radio contact degrades. The patrons become increasingly distant voices, then silence.

**Option 2: One of them comes.** One patron is aboard — probably the more driven one. This gives the MC a powerful NPC: someone with authority, resources, and a personal stake who can push the expedition forward when players hesitate. Also someone who can crack under pressure in a dramatically useful way.

**Option 3: Both come.** Maximum political tension on the ground. The disagreement between patrons plays out in real time, with players forced to choose sides. But this is complex to manage for a first ceremony with novice players.

**Recommendation for the first ceremony:** Option 1 or 2. Keep it manageable. One patron as NPC is probably the sweet spot — present enough to create friction, not so present that the patron overshadows the players.

## Starkweather-Moore and Dyer

The patron knows Dyer is alive. The patron has read his account (published version at minimum, possibly the private one depending on patron option). The patron has deliberately chosen not to involve Dyer. Reasons:

1. **Practical:** Dyer would undermine morale. He'd tell the team to turn back before they found anything.
2. **Reputational:** Dyer is considered unstable by the scientific establishment. His involvement would discredit the expedition.
3. **Strategic:** Dyer's detailed knowledge, if shared freely, might cause the team to approach with so much caution that they never reach the mountains. The patron wants boldness, not fear.
4. **Possibly darker:** The patron may have information from Dyer's private account and doesn't want the players to know how much the patron actually knows. The curated briefing protects the patron's information advantage, not just the players' morale.

Dyer may attempt to contact the expedition anyway — a letter smuggled aboard, a radio message intercepted during the crossing, pages from his private journal that someone (Danforth? a sympathetic colleague?) slipped into the cargo. These are narrative thread opportunities the Keeper can deploy.

## What Players Can Discover About the Patrons During Play

| Session | Possible revelation | Trigger |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| 1 | The briefing is edited — gaps, omissions, suspiciously clean narrative | A player with journalism or academic background might notice |
| 2 | Hidden cargo / sealed orders / patron's secondary objectives | Investigating the ship's hold, intercepted radio |
| 3-4 | The patron knew more than the briefing revealed — evidence at Lake's camp matches things the patron should have warned about | Comparing briefing to reality |
| 5-6 | The patron's true motive — what they actually want from the mountains | Orders received by radio, or found in sealed documents |
| 7-8 | The patron may be expendable — they expected losses, they planned for some people not coming back | Late-game revelation, possibly through NPC confession |
