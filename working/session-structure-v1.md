# Session Structure v1 — Provisional

*Working document. Not ground truth yet.*

---

## Design Principles

- Every session has its own dramatic question that gets answered by session end
- Every session has: setup, rising action, climax, resolution/hook
- The larger Lovecraft Curve spans all 8 sessions, but each session is satisfying alone
- Sessions add new scenes, locations, and revelations — no "travel filler"

## Death & Survival Rule

No permanent player death by default. If a player would die, the Keeper activates a **rescue pivot** — an environmental intervention that saves them at a cost:

- An NPC intervenes (and may die instead)
- An artifact activates unexpectedly (drawing unwanted attention)
- A penguin stampede disrupts the threat (and destroys something valuable)
- The environment shifts (ice collapse blocks the danger — and the way back)
- A fellow player's secret is forced into the open to justify a save

The rescue pivot must:
1. Feel organic, not like a save file reload
2. Have consequences — something is lost, revealed, or changed
3. Never feel like the player "got away with it"

The MC can override this rule during play and allow permanent death or group splitting if the story demands it. That's a live call, not a preset decision.

## Knowledge Rule

All players start with the same base knowledge. No player gets special Danforth-style exclusive visions unless the MC decides otherwise during play. Private Keeper channels exist for secret actions and personal reactions, not for asymmetric revelations about the core mystery.

## The Eight Sessions

### Session 1: "The Briefing"
**Dramatic question:** Who are these people, and should they be doing this?

**Setup:** Character creation via Keeper conversation, shaped by the preset. Players define who they are and why they'd join a dangerous Antarctic expedition in 1933.

**Rising action:** The group assembles. The patron's briefing. Dyer's published account is presented — players debate its credibility. Each character's motive surfaces. Conflicts between motives become visible.

**Climax:** Something concrete forces the group to confront the stakes — maybe a letter from Dyer himself warning them not to go, or a revelation about the patron's true interest. The cracks in the group are exposed before anyone touches ice.

**Resolution:** The departure decision. Knowing the risks, knowing each other's fault lines, they choose to go. The ceremony of leaving.

**Hook:** What the patron didn't tell them.

---

### Session 2: "The Crossing"
**Dramatic question:** What is this expedition actually about?

**Setup:** At sea, heading south. The world shrinks. The MC establishes the physical reality — the ship, the cold, the distances.

**Rising action:** Character bonding and friction during the voyage. First mirages on the horizon. The dogs are uneasy. Meanwhile, something doesn't add up — a sealed crate from the patron, intercepted radio chatter, a crew member who knows more than they should. The political mystery enters.

**Climax:** A dangerous moment during the crossing — storm, equipment failure, or a confrontation over the hidden cargo — forces characters to act under pressure and reveal their true priorities.

**Resolution:** First sight of the Ross Ice Barrier. Two hundred feet of vertical ice. The world they knew is behind them.

**Hook:** The dogs won't stop looking south.

---

### Session 3: "The Camp"
**Dramatic question:** Is this place just hostile, or is it wrong?

**Setup:** Establishing base camp near McMurdo Sound. First borings, surveys, radio check-ins with the ship. The Antarctic is beautiful and lethal.

**Rising action:** Accumulating small anomalies. Odd formations in rock samples. Instruments behaving strangely near certain locations. Triangular markings in strata that shouldn't contain them. Dogs refusing food, refusing to face one direction. Each anomaly individually explainable.

**Climax:** A night event. Not dramatic — but wrong. A sound the wind doesn't explain. Equipment moved. A dog that has to be restrained from running toward the mountains. Or: a player on watch sees something in the mirage-light that they can't quite describe, and the Keeper writes it into their journal as uncertain.

**Resolution:** The rational explanations hold — barely. The expedition is still scientific. But the players aren't sure anymore.

**Hook:** The surveys point toward something specific inland. The same direction the dogs fear.

---

### Session 4: "Lake's Camp"
**Dramatic question:** What happened to the first expedition?

**Setup:** Pushing inland. Finding traces of the 1930 Miskatonic expedition earlier than expected — a supply cache, a trail marker, a frozen dog harness.

**Rising action:** The destroyed advance camp. Three years of ice over the remains. Star-shaped mounds that look deliberate. Empty specimen containers. Dissection tools laid out with care — by whom? Lake's private field journal (not the published account) with increasingly frantic entries. The official "camp disaster" story collapses piece by piece.

**Climax:** The definitive evidence. Could be: Gedney's body still missing (everyone else accounted for). Specimens that were there and are now gone — or one that remains, frozen, that shouldn't exist. The star-shaped graves — built with care, with ritual. Something intelligent was here, and it buried its dead.

**Resolution:** The expedition has changed nature. This is no longer geology. The question is no longer "if" but "what."

**Hook:** Beyond the camp, the mountains are visible. Higher than anything on Earth. And through binoculars — are those cave mouths?

---

### Session 5: "The Mountains"
**Dramatic question:** Do we cross the point of no return?

**Setup:** Approaching the impossible range. Peaks exceeding 35,000 feet. Black, bare of snow. Cube-like formations clinging to the heights. Cave mouths unnervingly regular.

**Rising action:** The mirage of a city beyond the peaks — dismissed as atmospheric effect, but remarkably persistent and detailed. The wind sounds like musical piping. Geological readings make no sense. A player finds a page from Dyer's private journal (not the published account) that describes exactly what they're seeing.

**Climax:** The decision. Crossing means limited fuel for return. The patron's orders say go. The evidence says go. Self-preservation says don't. The group must decide together — or fracture. Maybe someone refuses. Maybe someone has been ordered to ensure the crossing happens regardless.

**Resolution:** They cross. The city is real. It stretches to the horizon. Every rational explanation just died.

**Hook:** The architecture has a pattern. Five-pointed. Everywhere.

---

### Session 6: "The City"
**Dramatic question:** Who built this — and are they gone?

**Setup:** First entry into the ruins. Cyclopean architecture older than complex life on Earth. Stone bridges, towers, domes — weathered but intact. The five-pointed geometry is inescapable.

**Rising action:** The murals. Room by room, corridor by corridor, the history of Earth's true first civilization unfolds. The Elder Things' arrival from space. Their mastery of biology. Their cities across the ancient world. Their science, their art, their wars — with the Mi-Go, with the Cthulhu spawn. Players choose which corridors to explore, which murals to study. Some are damaged. Some are in a later, cruder style.

**Climax:** Two linked revelations. First: the decline. The murals show retreat, loss, the city abandoned as the ice advanced. The Elder Things didn't die here — they went underground. Second: the crude carvings. Overlaid on some of the original murals. A different hand. Something else has been here since the builders left. Something that imitated their art badly.

**Resolution:** They know what the Elder Things were — a civilization. Not monsters. Scientists, artists, builders. The question shifts: what ended them? And what made those crude carvings?

**Hook:** A corridor leads down. Warmer air rises from it. And from far below — a sound that might be birds.

---

### Session 7: "The Descent"
**Dramatic question:** What's down there, and what does it cost to know?

**Setup:** The tunnel entrance. Fifteen-foot carved passages descending into warmth. The temperature is wrong — it's getting warmer in Antarctica. The Keeper notes the five-pointed star motif in the floor.

**Rising action:** Deeper. The carvings degrade, then change — crude spirals and angles carved OVER the originals. Palimpsests. Giant blind albino penguins appear, living in the deep tunnels. An entire underground ecosystem. The nameless scent grows stronger. Players find Elder Thing artifacts — soapstone fragments, tools, evidence of recent passage.

**Climax (two beats):**
1. THE EMPATHY TURN — They find the bodies. Headless Elder Things, coated in dark-green slime, thick ichor pooling. Recent. And the realization: these beings woke up after millions of years, were attacked by Lake's team and their dogs, defended themselves, fled here — tried to go home. "Whatever they had been, they were men." The players mourn something they would have feared an hour ago.
2. THE SHOGGOTH — Tekeli-li. A vast, onrushing column of fetid black iridescence filling the fifteen-foot tunnel. Temporary eyes forming and unforming. Crushing penguins. The thing that the Elder Things created and that eventually destroyed them. Flight. Pure flight.

**Resolution:** Survival. Getting back to the city, to the surface, to air and light. The knowledge that the Elder Things were the lesser horror — and that the greater horror is still down there.

**Hook:** During the escape, someone glimpses something they can't process. The Keeper writes fragments into their journal.

---

### Session 8: "The Return"
**Dramatic question:** What do you do with what you know?

**Setup:** Back at the plane. The mountain crossing ahead. The wind piping through the caves. Everyone rattled, some barely functional.

**Rising action:** The flight over the mountains. Someone looks back — or up — and sees something in the cloud formations above the violet western mountains. The ones the Elder Things themselves feared. The Keeper writes it into journals as fragments: half-formed impressions that resist language. The knowledge that this was not the bottom — there are deeper layers, and they are not meant for human comprehension.

**Climax:** Back at base. The patron's representative (or radio contact) wants a report. The world wants an explanation. And each player faces the choice: what do you tell? Dyer chose silence, and it didn't work — that's why they're here. The players know this. Do they repeat his mistake? Do they tell the truth nobody will believe? Do they lie and say they found nothing? Do they give the patron what they want — specimens, coordinates, proof?

**Resolution:** Each player's decision, written in their journal. Not a group consensus — individual choices. Some may lie. Some may tell truth. Some may keep silent. Some may plan to go back. The Keeper records it all.

The ceremony closes.

## Campaign Arc Mapped to Lovecraft Curve

| Lovecraft Phase | Sessions | Core Experience |
|----------------|----------|-----------------|
| Preparation | 1-2 | Social tension, human mystery |
| Transit | 2-3 | Environmental dread, small anomalies |
| Discovery | 3-4 | Evidence accumulation, rational collapse |
| Revelation | 5-6 | The city, the murals, the history |
| Confrontation | 7 | The empathy turn + the Shoggoth |
| Flight | 7-8 | Survival, the vision, the moral choice |
