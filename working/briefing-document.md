# The Briefing Document

*Player-facing prop for Session 1. This is what players receive before departure. It is the last thing they read before the world breaks.*

*Design notes are in [brackets]. The document itself begins after the separator.*

---

## Design Principles

- The briefing is a **contested document**. Starkweather and Moore disagree about what to include. The document shows their fracture through annotations, struck-through lines, and marginal notes.
- Starkweather's annotations are marked **[HS]** (his handwriting). They push toward belief, urgency, and the importance of the specimens.
- Moore's annotations are marked **[JM]** (his handwriting). They rationalize, qualify, and caution against credulity.
- Players should feel they are reading something real — and something edited. The gaps should be visible. A perceptive player will notice the omissions.
- The briefing ends with a confident summary: *we know what went wrong, we won't make the same mistakes.* This is the promise the story will break.

---

## Format

The briefing is a **folio** — a folder of loose typed pages with handwritten marginalia. When delivered to players (digitally or as a physical prop), it contains:

1. Cover letter from the expedition committee
2. Summary of the first expedition
3. Selected Lake bulletins (edited)
4. Maps and route plan
5. Expedition roster and equipment
6. The patron's concluding assessment

---

# STARKWEATHER-MOORE ANTARCTIC EXPEDITION
## Confidential Briefing Materials
### For Expedition Members Only

*Prepared by the Office of Dr. Harold Starkweather and Prof. James Moore*
*Miskatonic University / Columbia University Joint Venture*
*August 1933*

---

### I. COVER LETTER

Dear Colleague,

You have been selected for participation in the Starkweather-Moore Antarctic Expedition, departing from Arkham, Massachusetts in September 1933. This briefing package contains materials necessary for your preparation.

Our expedition follows the route established by the Miskatonic University Expedition of 1930-31, led by Professor William Dyer. That expedition made geological and biological discoveries of extraordinary significance. A camp disaster, attributed to severe weather and logistical overextension, curtailed their work before its full potential could be realized.

We intend to complete what Professor Dyer's team began.

You will find enclosed: a summary of the first expedition's published findings, selected field communications from the expedition's biologist, Dr. Frank Lake, a route plan, and an overview of our own expedition's resources. Please study these materials carefully. Questions may be directed to Dr. Starkweather aboard the vessel during transit.

> *[HS, in ink:] I will be available at any hour. Come find me. This work matters more than you yet understand.*

We depart at dawn on September 2nd from the Arkham wharf. The cargo steamer *Doris Wilkinson* will carry our party, equipment, and provisions to McMurdo Sound via Auckland and the Ross Sea. Expect approximately six weeks at sea.

Yours faithfully,

Dr. Harold Starkweather
Prof. James Moore

---

### II. THE FIRST EXPEDITION: SUMMARY

The Miskatonic University Expedition (1930-31) was funded as a geological survey. Its principal objective was securing deep-level rock and soil specimens from the antarctic continent, using a novel portable drilling apparatus designed by Professor Frank Pabodie of Miskatonic's engineering department.

**Personnel:** Professor William Dyer (geology, expedition leader), Professor Pabodie (engineering), Dr. Frank Lake (biology), Professor Atwood (physics and meteorology), seven graduate assistants, nine skilled mechanics. Total party of twenty.

**Equipment:** Four large Dornier aeroplanes modified for high-altitude polar flight. Fifty-five Alaskan sledge dogs. Portable wireless sets. Pabodie's drilling apparatus — lightweight, collapsible, capable of boring to one thousand feet through varying strata.

**Ships:** The brig *Arkham* (Captain Douglas) and barque *Miskatonic* (Captain Thorfinnssen).

**Base of operations:** Ross Island, McMurdo Sound — the traditional staging ground for Antarctic operations.

**Key events:**

The expedition established a southern base camp and conducted successful drilling operations through the austral summer of 1930-31. Geological specimens were obtained from deep strata. During routine boring operations, anomalous fossil impressions were discovered in slate fragments: triangular striated markings of extraordinary regularity, suggesting biological origin from strata predating all known complex life.

These discoveries prompted Dr. Lake to establish a forward sub-expedition to the northwest, where a previously unknown mountain range had been sighted — peaks of remarkable height, estimated to exceed 35,000 feet. Lake's party of eleven men, four students, and a complement of sledge dogs departed the main camp on January 22nd, 1931.

> *[JM, pencil:] Note: Lake's decision to push northwest was made against Dyer's recommendation. Relevant to understanding the subsequent disaster.*

Lake's forward camp yielded discoveries described in the wireless bulletins reproduced in Section III below.

**The camp disaster:**

On the night following Lake's most significant biological discoveries, communication from the forward camp ceased. When Dyer's relief party reached the site, they found the camp destroyed. Eleven men were confirmed dead. One man — Gedney — was listed as missing and never recovered. Severe windstorm conditions were recorded in the region at the time.

> *[HS:] "Severe windstorm." Read the bulletins in Section III and judge for yourself whether wind did this.*

> *[JM:] Harold — the evidence is consistent with a polar katabatic event of unusual severity. The Ross Ice Shelf region has recorded wind speeds exceeding 200 mph. We should not encourage speculative thinking among expedition members.*

The surviving members of the expedition, led by Dyer, conducted a brief aerial survey of the mountain range before withdrawing. Dyer subsequently published an account warning against further Antarctic exploration. His warnings were not supported by the physical evidence and have been attributed by colleagues to the severe psychological strain of discovering his camp destroyed and his colleagues dead.

> *[HS:] Dyer is not deranged. I have read his correspondence. He saw something beyond those mountains that he cannot bring himself to describe publicly. That is not the behavior of a man who suffered a weather event.*

> *[JM, heavily underlined:] Starkweather — remove this note before the briefing is distributed. This is exactly the sort of editorial that undermines our credibility.*

> *[HS:] No.*

---

### III. SELECTED FIELD COMMUNICATIONS — DR. FRANK LAKE

*The following are excerpts from Lake's wireless bulletins, transmitted from the forward camp and relayed through the Arkham Advertiser and Associated Press. They are reproduced from the published record.*

> *[JM:] I have edited these for relevance and clarity. Some of Lake's later transmissions became increasingly informal and speculative. The excerpts below represent the substantive scientific content.*

> *[HS:] Moore has cut the most important bulletins. I have restored two of them, marked with an asterisk. You deserve to read what Lake actually reported.*

**Bulletin 1 — Discovery of anomalous fossils**

"Fowler makes discovery of highest importance in sandstone and limestone fragments from blasts. Several distinct triangular striated prints like those in archean slate, proving that source survived from over six hundred million years ago to Comanchean times without more than moderate morphological changes and decrease in average size. Comanchean prints apparently more primitive or decadent, if anything, than older ones. Emphasize importance of discovery in press. Will mean to biology what Einstein has meant to mathematics and physics."

**Bulletin 2 — Soapstone fragment**

"Have found peculiar soapstone fragment about six inches across and an inch and a half thick, wholly unlike any visible local formation — greenish, but no evidences to place its period. Has curious smoothness and regularity. Shaped like five-pointed star with tips broken off, and signs of other cleavage at inward angles and in center of surface. Small, smooth depression in center of unbroken surface. Arouses much curiosity as to source and weathering. Probably some freak of water action."

> *[HS:] Note the five-pointed geometry. Note the "curious smoothness and regularity." Note that Lake says "probably some freak of water action" — this is a scientist trying to explain away something that does not fit his framework. Keep this geometry in mind when you reach the mountains.*

**Bulletin 3 — The specimens**

"Important discovery. Orrendorf and Watkins, working underground at 9:45 with light, found monstrous barrel-shaped fossil of wholly unknown nature; probably vegetable unless overgrown specimen of unknown marine radiata. Tissue evidently preserved by mineral salts. Tough as leather, but astonishing flexibility retained in places. Marks of broken-off parts at ends and around sides. Six feet end to end, three and five tenths feet central diameter, tapering to one foot at each end. Like a barrel with five bulging ridges in place of staves."

> *[JM:] Lake's subsequent descriptions become increasingly detailed but also increasingly speculative. The biological classification of these specimens remains unresolved. They may represent a novel form of preserved colonial organism or an unusually large radiate. Further analysis is precisely the purpose of our expedition.*

**Bulletin 4 — Specimen details** *

*[restored by HS]*

"Of organic specimens, eight apparently perfect, with all appendages. Objects are eight feet long all over. Six-foot, five-ridged barrel torso three and five tenths feet central diameter. Dark gray, flexible, and infinitely tough. Seven-foot membranous wings of same color, found folded, spread out of furrows between ridges."

"At top of torso blunt, bulbous neck of lighter gray holds yellowish five-pointed starfish-shaped apparent head covered with three-inch wiry cilia of various prismatic colors. Head thick and puffy, about two feet point to point, with three-inch flexible yellowish tubes projecting from each point. Slit in exact center of top probably breathing aperture. At end of each tube is spherical expansion where yellowish membrane rolls back on handling to reveal glassy, red-irised globe, evidently an eye."

> *[JM:] I specifically excluded this bulletin. The anatomical description, while vivid, is unverified and has contributed disproportionately to sensational press coverage. Our expedition will conduct proper analysis under controlled conditions.*

> *[HS:] Moore excluded it because it makes the specimens sound like animals. Not fossils. Not colonial organisms. Animals with eyes, wings, and a symmetry that matches no known phylum. You should know what you are going to find out there.*

**Bulletin 5 — Dogs' reaction**

"Dogs growing uneasy as we work, and seem to hate this soapstone. Must see if it has any peculiar odor."

*[Later:]* "Having trouble with dogs. They can't endure the new specimen, and would probably tear it to pieces if we didn't keep it at a distance from them."

> *[HS:] This is not in the briefing by accident. Animals sense what instruments do not.*

> *[JM:] Animals react to unfamiliar stimuli. This is unremarkable.*

**[BULLETINS 6-9 — OMITTED]**

> *[JM:] Lake's final transmissions were fragmentary, increasingly excited, and cut off by the storm. They do not contain verified scientific data and would serve only to alarm. They will be available to senior expedition staff upon request.*

> *[HS, in the margin, underlined twice:] Ask me for the missing bulletins.*

### IV. ROUTE AND OBJECTIVES

**Outward voyage:** Arkham, Mass. — Auckland, NZ — Ross Sea — McMurdo Sound. Approximately six weeks.

**Phase 1: Base camp establishment.** McMurdo Sound. Unload equipment, establish supply depot, test equipment, acclimatize.

**Phase 2: Forward operations.** Fly northwest to Lake's camp coordinates. Assess the site. Recover specimens and equipment. Establish a forward operating base.

**Phase 3: Mountain survey.** Aerial reconnaissance of the mountain range. Map passes. Photograph geological formations. Assess feasibility of ground-level exploration beyond the range.

> *[HS:] "Beyond the range." Everything depends on what is beyond the range.*

**Phase 4: Deep exploration.** Contingent on Phase 3 findings. Ground teams may cross the mountains if conditions permit.

> *[JM:] Phase 4 is provisional. Safety assessments will determine whether any crossing is attempted. No member of this expedition will be ordered into conditions that cannot be evaluated in advance.*

---

### V. EXPEDITION RESOURCES

**Vessel:** Cargo steamer *Doris Wilkinson* (Capt. R. Thorsen), reinforced hull, ice-capable.

**Aircraft:** Three Dornier aeroplanes, modified per Pabodie specifications. Improved fuel-warming systems. Increased cargo capacity. One fitted with aerial camera.

**Sledge dogs:** Forty Alaskan dogs, acquired from the same breeder who supplied the first expedition.

**Communications:** Three portable wireless sets, improved range. Direct relay capability to the *Doris Wilkinson* anchored in McMurdo Sound. Secondary relay through Arkham Advertiser station.

**Scientific equipment:** Pabodie-type drilling apparatus (two units). Geological sample cases. Biological preservation equipment. Photographic equipment. Surveying instruments.

**Personnel:** Dr. Starkweather (expedition co-director, aboard). Prof. Moore (co-director, Arkham liaison via wireless). [Players to be listed here.] Ship's crew of twelve. Four mechanics. Two wireless operators.

> *[HS:] We are better equipped, better funded, and better informed than Dyer's party. We have read everything he published. We know what the weather can do. We will not make Lake's mistake of pushing forward without adequate support.*

---

### VI. ASSESSMENT AND EXPECTATIONS

The first Miskatonic expedition was curtailed by a preventable disaster. Lake's decision to establish an unsupported forward camp in extreme conditions, combined with a catastrophic weather event, resulted in the loss of eleven lives and the abandonment of the most significant biological discovery of the century.

Professor Dyer's subsequent warnings against further exploration are understandable as the response of a man who lost colleagues under his command. They do not reflect the scientific evidence, which points overwhelmingly toward discoveries of extraordinary importance awaiting recovery and proper study.

This expedition is better funded, better equipped, and better informed. We know what went wrong. We will not make the same mistakes.

> *[HS:] Read that last sentence again when you reach the mountains.*

> *[JM:] Harold, for God's sake.*

---

*END OF BRIEFING MATERIALS*

## Design Notes (for MC/Keeper, not shown to players)

### What the briefing reveals
- The first expedition found something unprecedented
- Lake found biological specimens that defy classification
- The camp was destroyed, officially by weather
- Dyer warns against going back, is dismissed as traumatized
- The patrons disagree — Starkweather believes Dyer, Moore doesn't
- There's material being withheld (bulletins 6-9, Dyer's full account)

### What the briefing conceals
- Lake's references to the Necronomicon and Elder Things (cut by Moore from bulletins)
- Lake's references to the Cthulhu cult (cut)
- The dissection bulletins — Lake cut one open and described internal anatomy
- Lake's final transmission: "Matter of highest — I might say transcendent — importance"
- That the specimens appeared "no more than ten years dead" despite geological age
- That things at the camp were not consistent with wind damage (aeroplanes tampered, sledges taken)
- Everything Dyer saw beyond the mountains

### What perceptive players might notice
- The five-pointed geometry appears in the soapstone AND the specimen's body plan — no one in the briefing connects these
- The bulletins jump from "found specimens" to "camp destroyed" — there's a gap
- Starkweather says "ask me for the missing bulletins" — a direct invitation to dig deeper
- Moore's increasingly desperate marginalia suggests he knows there's more to this than he admits
- The briefing never explains what happened to Gedney. Missing and never recovered. No theory offered.
- Dogs react to the specimens. This detail is included but never explained.

### How the briefing breaks
Session by session, reality contradicts the briefing:
- **Session 3-4 (Lake's Camp):** The damage is not consistent with wind. The five-pointed mounds. The missing sledges. The players see what the briefing glossed over.
- **Session 5-6 (The City):** The scale of what exists beyond the mountains dwarfs anything the briefing imagined. Starkweather's "beyond the range" takes on a terrible meaning.
- **Session 7 (Underground):** The specimens were not fossils. The briefing's careful rationalism becomes absurd.
- **Session 8:** The players now know more than either patron. The briefing was not just incomplete — it was a map to a place neither Starkweather nor Moore truly understood.
