# ROERICH AND THE ORKHON VALLEY — PROP MATERIAL

## Briefing Document Insert & Keeper Reference

*A document or annotation that can appear in the Session 0 briefing materials or aboard the ship.*
*Plants the steppe/Mi-Go connection early. Pays off when players encounter Mural 5.*
*The Keeper generates the prop; the MC decides when and how to deploy it.*

---

## CONTEXT FOR THE KEEPER

Nicholas Roerich is already referenced in Lovecraft's text. Dyer compares the Antarctic mountains to "the strange and disturbing Asian paintings of Nicholas Roerich." The comparison is not casual — Roerich was a genuine mystic who believed in hidden cities and lost civilizations in the mountains of Central Asia, and who led a real expedition through Mongolia, Tibet, and the Himalayas in the 1920s searching for Shambhala.

This prop extends Roerich's role from a passing literary comparison to a planted clue. It connects the painter's Central Asian experiences to the Mi-Go's territorial presence in the Himalayas and to the Tengrist sky-theology that underlies the ceremony's treatment of the open sky as sacred medium.

The Orkhon Valley — site of the Kül Tigin inscription and the Old Turkic runic steles — lies in central Mongolia. Roerich's expedition passed through this region. The inscription's theology (Tengri as the Eternal Blue Sky, humans created in order to die) resonates with the Mi-Go's relationship to the sky as element rather than backdrop. The prop makes this connection available to players without explaining it.

## PROP OPTION A — STARKWEATHER'S ANNOTATION

*A marginal note in the briefing document, in Starkweather's handwriting, attached to the section that quotes Dyer's Roerich comparison.*

### Where It Appears

In the briefing, wherever Dyer's comparison of the Antarctic mountains to Roerich's paintings is quoted. Starkweather has underlined the Roerich reference and written in the margin:

---

*Roerich's Central Asia expedition — 1925-28. Crossed Mongolia, the Altai, Tibet. Searching for Shambhala — the hidden city. His paintings from this period show mountains that shouldn't exist: violet light, impossible peaks, geometry in the rock. He wrote that the local people — Mongol herders, Tibetan monks — told him about things in the high places. Not human. Not animal. Things that had been there before people came. "The old tenants," he called them.*

*I have three of his Himalayan paintings. They look like Antarctica.*

*— S.*

---

### Keeper Notes on This Prop

Starkweather is an obsessive. His annotation reveals that he has already made the connection between Roerich's Central Asian experiences and the Antarctic. He does not spell out what "the old tenants" means. A player who knows Lovecraft's lore will recognize the Mi-Go. A player who doesn't will register the phrase as ominous without understanding it. Both responses are correct.

The phrase "They look like Antarctica" is Starkweather at his most dangerous — the moment where his pattern-matching (which is accurate) drives him toward the mountains rather than away from them. He sees the resemblance and reads it as invitation. Dyer saw the same resemblance and read it as warning.

## PROP OPTION B — THE ROERICH LETTER

*A separate document in the briefing package — a letter or excerpt that Starkweather included as supporting material. Attributed to Roerich or to a member of his expedition.*

### The Document

A typed excerpt, presented as a translation from Russian, from a journal or letter written during Roerich's Central Asian expedition. The source attribution reads: *"From the expedition journal of Prof. N.K. Roerich, translated by G.Z. Blumenfeld. Excerpt covers the party's passage through the Orkhon Valley, Mongolia, July 1927."*

---

We made camp near the stone monuments the Mongols call the Orkhon steles. Old Turkic script — eighth century, perhaps earlier. Our Mongolian guide, Tserendorj, would not approach the larger stele after dark. He said the inscriptions were not written *for* people. They were written *to* Tengri. To the sky. He said Tengri does not read the way people read. Tengri reads by seeing the marks from above — from very far above — and that certain marks, if carved correctly, are visible from distances no human eye could manage.

I asked what else might see the marks from such distances. He did not answer directly. He said that in his grandfather's time, herders in the high Altai found tunnels in the rock that were not natural and not old in the way that geological formations are old. The tunnels were smooth. They went down at angles that made no sense for water drainage or mineral veins. His grandfather's generation sealed the entrances with cairns and did not speak of them except to say that the mountain's tenants did not like light and preferred the high places where the sky was closest to the earth.

I pressed him on the word "tenants." He used the Mongolian *ezed* — a word that means something between "owner" and "spirit of a place." Not human. Not divine in the Buddhist sense. Something that was there before the land had a name, and that remained because it had no reason to leave.

The paintings I made the next morning — the Altai series — came out wrong. The violet in the sky was not a color I mixed. It was in the light itself.

---

### Keeper Notes on This Prop

This is a fabricated document — Roerich did not write this. The Keeper and MC should understand that it is invented for the ceremony. It uses Roerich's real expedition timeline (1925-28, crossing Mongolia) and real local concepts (*ezed* is a genuine Mongolian term for place-spirits) to create a document that feels plausible within the ceremony's world.

The key plants:

- **Tunnels in the rock that were smooth and at wrong angles.** This matches the Mi-Go's architectural signature: mining operations, tunnels, functional infrastructure. A player who remembers this detail will recognize it when they see Mural 5's depiction of Mi-Go tunnels beneath Elder Thing cities.

- **"The mountain's tenants did not like light and preferred the high places where the sky was closest to the earth."** The Mi-Go operate in northern mountain regions — the Himalayas, the Vermont hills, anywhere high, cold, and remote. The detail about preferring proximity to the sky connects to the Tengrist axis: the sky as medium, not backdrop.

- **The violet light.** Connects forward to the violet western mountains in Sessions 2-4. Roerich's violet is the same violet. The color is a signature of Stratum IV — it appears wherever the deeper strata bleed through into the visible world.

- **Tserendorj's refusal to approach the stele after dark.** The stele inscriptions address Tengri — the sky. Something that reads from above. The Mi-Go traverse interstellar space. The implication is available but not stated.

## PROP OPTION C — THE ORKHON PHOTOGRAPH

*A photograph or sketch in the briefing materials, showing one of the Orkhon steles. Annotated.*

### The Document

A black-and-white photograph (or a sketch, if no photograph is available as a prop) showing a tall stone stele on the Mongolian steppe. Flat grassland extending to the horizon in all directions. An enormous sky.

Starkweather's annotation on the back:

---

*Kül Tigin monument, Orkhon Valley, Mongolia. Photographed during the Roerich expedition, 1927. Old Turkic runic script. The inscription addresses "Tengri" — the Eternal Blue Sky. Not a god who lives in the sky. The sky itself.*

*The Nepalese call them Mi-Go. The Mongols call them ezed. Roerich called them "the old tenants." The Vermonters don't call them anything — they just avoid the hills after dark.*

*Different names. Same tenants.*

*— S.*

---

### Keeper Notes on This Prop

This is the most direct version. Starkweather has connected the dots: Mi-Go, ezed, old tenants, Vermont hill legends. He has written it down. He is not afraid — he is excited. This is a man who sees a pattern and wants to follow it to its source.

This prop should only be used if the MC wants the Mi-Go connection to be relatively legible early. If the MC prefers a slower burn, use Prop Option A (the marginal annotation) instead. Prop Option B (the Roerich letter) is the middle ground — it plants the seeds without Starkweather's explicit synthesis.

## DEPLOYMENT GUIDANCE

### Session 0 — The Briefing

Any of the three prop options can be included in the briefing package the players receive. The MC chooses which version based on how legible they want the Mi-Go connection to be at the start of play.

### Session 1 — The Crossing

If the prop was not included in the briefing, it can surface aboard the ship. A Roerich painting hanging in the ship's wardroom (real — Roerich was famous enough in the 1930s for a print to be plausible). Or a book in someone's cabin: Roerich's *Altai-Himalaya* (published 1929), with a passage marked. The ship crossing is the ideal time for players to encounter background material — they have time to read, to speculate, to build connections.

### Session 2 — The Mountains

The Roerich comparison becomes visceral when the players see the Antarctic mountains for the first time. The Keeper should describe them in terms that echo Roerich's Central Asian paintings — the violet light, the impossible peaks, the geometry in the rock — without naming Roerich directly. A player who has read the prop will feel the echo. The mountains look like the Altai. The Altai looked like Antarctica. The pattern is the same because the tenants are the same.

### Session 2, Act II — Mural 5

The payoff. The player sees the Mi-Go invasion depicted in the Elder Things' murals. The tunnels. The mining. The creatures that came through the sky. If the player remembers Tserendorj's grandfather's tunnels in the Altai — smooth, at wrong angles, sealed with cairns — the recognition is a private horror. The Mongol herder's oral tradition and the Elder Things' carved history are describing the same thing, separated by millions of years and thousands of miles, and they match.

## WHAT THE MC SHOULD KNOW

These props are fabricated for the ceremony. Roerich's Central Asian expedition was real. The Orkhon inscriptions are real. The Tengrist theology is real. The Mongolian concept of *ezed* is real. The connection to the Mi-Go is Lovecraft's — he placed the Mi-Go in the Himalayas and identified them with the Nepalese legend of the Abominable Snow Men. Everything in these props follows logically from Lovecraft's own world-building and from the real history of the regions he referenced.

The fabrication is: Roerich did not describe tunnels or "old tenants" in his actual journals. Starkweather's annotations are invented. The Roerich letter is invented. These are ceremony props — they exist to deepen the world, not to misrepresent history. If a player knows enough about Roerich to recognize the fabrication, that is a feature, not a bug. They are playing the game at a level where the boundary between the ceremony's world and the real world has become porous. That is exactly the condition the ceremony is designed to produce.
