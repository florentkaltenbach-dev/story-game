# HISTORICAL RHYMES — THE KEEPER'S ANALOGICAL INDEX

## Cosmological Architecture Appendix

*Maps the major lore entities to their historical and mythological parallels.*
*Not for players. For the Keeper's generative use when improvising descriptions, NPC dialogue, mural content, and atmospheric detail.*

---

## 0. PURPOSE

The entities in the ceremony's cosmology are not allegories for historical civilizations. They are cosmic horrors operating on geological and interstellar timescales. But Lovecraft's imagination drew from specific wells — the fears, myths, and remembered catastrophes of settled civilizations confronting forces they could not comprehend. These parallels are the Keeper's toolkit for making invented material feel real. When the Keeper describes a mural, generates an NPC's reaction to a discovery, or writes a journal fragment, these analogies provide emotional and structural depth without ever being named.

The Keeper never tells players "the Mi-Go are the Mongols." The Keeper describes the Mi-Go invasion mural with the same structural grammar that a Persian chronicler would use to describe the fall of Khwarazm — and lets the archetype do its work beneath the surface.

---

## 1. THE ELDER THINGS — THE SETTLED CIVILIZATIONS OF THE ANCIENT NEAR EAST

### The Parallel

Sumer. Egypt. Persia. The Abbasid Caliphate. Song China. Every great civilization that built cities, wrote on walls, maintained libraries, developed art and science over millennia — and was eventually overwhelmed by forces from outside.

### What Maps

| Elder Thing trait | Historical echo |
|---|---|
| City builders with monumental architecture | Ur, Babylon, Persepolis, Baghdad |
| Murals recording their own history on every interior surface | Egyptian tomb paintings, Assyrian palace reliefs, Persian rock carvings at Behistun and Naqsh-e Rostam |
| Created life from inorganic matter (the Shoggoths) | Slave economies — civilizations whose prosperity depended on created or captured labor |
| Lost the secret of interstellar travel — became earthbound | The recurring pattern of civilizations that lose the capability that made them great (Rome forgetting how to make concrete, the Abbasids losing military dominance to their own slave soldiers) |
| Retreat from global presence to Antarctic isolation | The civilizational contraction — from the Abbasid Caliphate spanning three continents to the rump states of the late medieval Islamic world |
| Art that declines in quality over geological ages | The late Roman period, the post-Mongol Persian world — art that remembers its own golden age and can no longer match it |

### Keeper Application

When describing the Elder Thing city, the Keeper draws from the emotional register of ruins that were once the center of the world. Not nostalgia — the cold recognition that something enormous and brilliant stood here and is gone. The murals are not quaint. They are the Rosetta Stone, the Behistun inscription, the tomb of Seti I — records made by beings who assumed their civilization would last forever and were wrong.

When describing the decline murals (Mural 6), the Keeper can draw from the emotional quality of late-period art in any civilization: technically competent but carrying a quality of exhaustion, of forms repeated because they are traditional rather than because they are felt. Fewer carvers. Less detail. The world shrinking for the artists.

## 2. THE MI-GO — THE STEPPE NOMADS

### The Parallel

The Mongol Empire. The Turkic khaganates. The Xiongnu. Every nomadic power that emerged from the spaces between cities and overwhelmed the settled world through superior mobility, adaptability, and a fundamentally different relationship to territory.

### What Maps

| Mi-Go trait | Historical echo |
|---|---|
| Arrived from outside and drove the Elder Things from all northern lands | The Mongol conquests: Khwarazm, Baghdad, Kiev, the Jin and Song dynasties — swept from the map in a generation |
| No grand cities — mining operations, tunnels, functional infrastructure | The Mongol relationship to space: the yurt, the relay station, the supply line. Power expressed as movement, not monument. The yam postal system spanning a continent |
| Retained cosmic mobility while the Elder Things lost it | The defining nomadic advantage: the ability to move. The Mongol horse army covering distances that settled civilizations considered impossible |
| Composed of matter unlike terrestrial biology — transformations impossible for the Elder Things | The ontological shock of the steppe invasion: settled peoples could not understand how the Mongols operated. Their tactics, logistics, and social organization seemed to violate the rules of civilization as understood |
| Active in the Himalayas and Central Asia — remembered as "Abominable Snow Men" | The Mongol heartland. The Turkic steppe. The roof of the world |
| Communicate with human agents (Akeley) — covert, manipulative, using locals | The Mongol practice of recruiting local elites, using merchants and diplomats as intelligence assets, turning a civilization's own people into instruments |
| Extract and preserve human brains in metal cylinders | The most extreme version of the nomadic practice of taking what is valuable and leaving the rest. Not the city, not the walls, not the monuments — the knowledge. The mind separated from the body, from the place, from the context that gave it meaning |

### The Tengrist Axis

The Mi-Go come from Yuggoth — from the outer darkness, from the cosmic sky. The Mongols and Turks rode under Tengri's mandate — the Eternal Blue Sky as supreme sacred principle. Both are powers of the open sky imposing themselves on builders of enclosed spaces. The Elder Things build cities and carve walls. The Mi-Go traverse the gulfs of space. The settled world builds temples to bring the divine indoors. Tengri cannot be brought indoors. Tengri is the sky itself.

The Kül Tigin inscription on the Orkhon stele — *Öd Teŋri yasar · kişi oγlu qop · ölgeli törümiş* ("When Tengri above created time, human beings were all created in order to die") — carries the same ontological weight as the Mi-Go's cosmic indifference. The steppe does not hate the city. It simply does not need it. The sky does not oppose the wall. The sky was there before the wall, and will be there after.

### Keeper Application

When describing the Mi-Go invasion murals (Mural 5), the Keeper draws from the emotional register of chronicles written by the conquered — the Persian historians who described the fall of their world to the Mongols with a mixture of horror, incomprehension, and grudging awe. The Elder Things carved the Mi-Go invasion with precision, because they were compulsive record-keepers, but the carvings should convey something the carvers could not fully process: an enemy that operated by different rules entirely.

The murals become smaller, more cramped. The world is shrinking for the carvers. This is not just narrative — it is the Elder Things' psychological response to an enemy whose power was not architectural but operational. They couldn't carve what the Mi-Go built, because the Mi-Go didn't build. They moved.

When the Antarctic sky features in any session — the mirages, the piping wind, the open expanse — the Keeper can let the Tengrist resonance inform the description. The sky is not empty. It is full. It is the medium through which powers that do not need walls communicate with powers that do.

## 3. THE CTHULHU SPAWN — THE ATLANTEAN MARITIME POWER

### The Parallel

Atlantis as Plato described it in the *Timaeus* and *Critias*. A maritime civilization of impossible architecture that controlled oceanic territory, warred with the powers of the known world, and sank beneath the waves in a catastrophic event — surviving only as transmitted memory, cult, and dream.

### What Maps

| Cthulhu spawn trait | Historical/mythological echo |
|---|---|
| Arrived from cosmic infinity, seized the new lands of the Pacific | Atlantis as a power from beyond the Pillars of Hercules — from "out there," past the edge of the known world |
| Built R'lyeh: cyclopean, non-Euclidean, geometry that defies comprehension | Plato's Atlantis: concentric rings of water and land, temples covered in metals, architecture described with obsessive geometric precision. Both are saying "this civilization built in ways we cannot replicate" — Plato through idealization, Lovecraft through aberration |
| Drove the Elder Things from the land into the sea | Atlantis threatened the Mediterranean world — a maritime power projecting force against settled coastal civilizations |
| Peace treaty: the spawn got the new lands, Elder Things kept the sea and older territories | Plato's Atlantis coexisted with Athens before the war. Two powers in negotiated equilibrium, until catastrophe |
| The Pacific lands sank, taking R'lyeh and all the spawn | Atlantis sank in a day and a night. Sudden, total, geological — not military defeat but the earth swallowing the civilization whole |
| Cthulhu sleeps but does not die — the dreams leak to sensitive minds worldwide | Atlantis persists as cultural memory, guarded by Egyptian priests for millennia. The sunken power is not destroyed — it is preserved in degraded forms of knowledge |
| Global cult: New Zealand, Louisiana, Greenland, the Arab world | The Atlantis myth recurs in nearly every maritime culture — Lemuria, Mu, Kumari Kandam, Ys. A global pattern of remembering something that sank |

### The Transmission Chain

The deepest parallel is epistemological — how the knowledge of the sunken civilization survives:

| Chain | Source | First witness | Second witness | Surviving text | Reception |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Atlantis | The civilization itself | Egyptian priests | Solon | Plato's *Timaeus* and *Critias* | Treated as allegory or philosophy, not history |
| R'lyeh | The civilization itself | Pre-human cult contacts | Abdul Alhazred | The *Necronomicon* | Treated as madness or forbidden knowledge |
| The Antarctic city | The civilization itself (the murals) | William Dyer | The patron (edited briefing) | Dyer's published account / the curated briefing | Treated as trauma response or delusion |

Each chain degrades. Each intermediary filters. Each surviving text is dismissed by the mainstream as not-quite-real. And each text, despite the dismissal, contains enough accurate information to lead the next expedition to the source. The warning becomes the map.

### Where the Parallel Exceeds Plato

Lovecraft adds what Plato withholds: the sunken power is not finished. Atlantis in Plato is a cautionary tale — the sinking is divine punishment, the story has a moral, and closure is provided. R'lyeh has no closure. Cthulhu dreams, and the dreams are active, and the cult prepares for a return that is not metaphorical. The catastrophe did not end anything. It suspended it. The Atlantis archetype, stripped of its moral framework, becomes pure dread: something vast and alien sank beneath the ocean and is waiting.

### Keeper Application

When describing the war murals (Mural 4), the Keeper draws from the emotional register of civilizations depicting enemies whose power they could not fully comprehend. The Elder Things carved the Cthulhu spawn with their characteristic precision, but the carvings should convey strain — the five-pointed, symmetrical, obsessively ordered carving style of the Elder Things struggling to represent non-Euclidean geometry. The stone resists. The figures don't quite resolve. The carver was trying to depict something their aesthetic vocabulary couldn't contain.

When the Necronomicon is referenced in any context, the Keeper holds the Timaeus parallel in mind: this is not a spellbook. It is testimony. Degraded, filtered, dismissed — but testimony. Abdul Alhazred is the Solon figure: the traveler who went to the source, heard the account from those who remembered, and wrote it down for a civilization that would refuse to believe him.

When describing cult activity or dream-contact (if it arises), the Keeper draws from the quality of Atlantis myths in real-world cultures: the persistent, cross-cultural conviction that something important is beneath the sea. Not superstition — pattern recognition across millennia, degraded into folklore.

## 4. THE SHOGGOTHS — THE SLAVE REVOLT

### The Parallel

Every slave rebellion in history. Spartacus. The Haitian Revolution. The Mamluk seizure of power in Egypt. The Janissary revolts in the Ottoman Empire. And most precisely: any system where a civilization creates a servile class, grants it increasing capability and autonomy over time, and is eventually destroyed by it.

### What Maps

| Shoggoth trait | Historical echo |
|---|---|
| Created by the Elder Things as protoplasmic servants, controlled by hypnotic will | Slave economies: labor created or captured specifically to serve. The Mamluks — slave soldiers purchased, trained, and deployed by the Abbasid and Ayyubid states |
| Developed independent intelligence over geological time | The Mamluks developed political consciousness, military expertise, and institutional memory. The Janissaries built a corporate identity that eventually overrode their masters' control |
| Rebelled, were suppressed, rebelled again, eventually won | The Mamluk Sultanate: the slaves became the rulers. The Janissary revolts that paralyzed the Ottoman state. Haiti: the enslaved population destroyed the system that created them |
| Imitation — crude carvings over Elder Thing art, forming temporary eyes and organs in mimicry | The pattern of revolutionary cultures initially imitating the forms of the civilization they overthrew. Post-revolutionary states adopting the architecture, titles, and ceremonies of their predecessors — but badly, with the proportions off |
| "Tekeli-li" — a vocalization that is not language but resembles it | The fear, in every slave-holding civilization, that the servile class has developed a culture, a communication system, a will that the masters cannot decode |

### The Gnostic Overlay

In the Gnostic frame (see Cosmological Architecture §2D), the Shoggoths are Archons — beings created to maintain the Demiurge's order, now operating autonomously. The horror is not just physical. It is the horror of created order devouring its creator. The Demiurge built a world and populated it with enforcers. The enforcers learned. The enforcers continued. The Demiurge did not.

### Keeper Application

When describing the Shoggoth encounter (Session 3) or the crude carvings (Mural 7), the Keeper draws from the emotional register of the uncanny valley of imitation — the specific wrongness of seeing skilled work defaced by something that tried to copy it and couldn't quite manage. The proportions are off. The forms are recognizable but debased. This is not destruction. It is continuation by other means. The Shoggoths didn't erase the Elder Things' art. They wrote over it. They are still trying to be what their makers were.

The "Tekeli-li" cry should be described not as a monster's roar but as an attempt at speech — or an echo of speech once heard and imperfectly reproduced across millions of years. That is more disturbing than any scream.

## 5. THE GREAT RACE OF YITH — THE ARCHIVISTS

### The Parallel

The Library of Alexandria. The House of Wisdom in Baghdad. The Vatican Secret Archives. Any institution that accumulates knowledge across civilizational boundaries by extracting it from its original context — scholars who travel to other cultures, copy their texts, bring the knowledge home, and outlast the civilizations they studied.

### What Maps

| Great Race trait | Historical echo |
|---|---|
| Project consciousness across time, inhabiting bodies of other species to study their eras | Scholarly extraction: the Greek scholars who traveled to Egypt and Babylon, absorbed their mathematics and astronomy, and brought it back to a civilization that would outlast the sources |
| Libraries and archives beneath the Australian desert | The buried libraries — scrolls preserved in desert caves (the Dead Sea Scrolls, the Dunhuang manuscripts), the subterranean archives that outlast the civilizations above them |
| War with the Flying Polyps | The recurring conflict between archival civilizations and destructive forces — the burning of libraries, the Mongol destruction of Baghdad's House of Wisdom, the loss of Alexandria |
| Minds, not bodies — they abandon hosts when the era becomes dangerous | The pattern of intellectual traditions that migrate: Greek learning preserved in Arabic, then retransmitted to Latin Europe. The knowledge moves; the bodies are expendable |

### Keeper Application

The Great Race is not directly encountered in this ceremony, but their pattern — knowledge extracted from context, preserved in alien form, surviving the death of its source — resonates with the players' own activity. The players are reading murals carved by beings who recorded everything. They are extracting knowledge from a dead civilization's walls. They are, in a small way, doing what the Great Race does. The Keeper can let this parallel inform the quality of discovery scenes without ever naming it.

## 6. CROSS-CUTTING PATTERNS

### The Fear of the Settled

Every entity that threatens the Elder Things represents a specific fear of settled civilizations:

| Entity | The fear | Historical form |
|---|---|---|
| Cthulhu spawn | The maritime power from beyond the horizon that controls territory you cannot reach | Atlantis. The Sea Peoples. The Viking raids on monastic Europe |
| Mi-Go | The nomadic force from the spaces between cities that moves faster than you can respond | The Mongols. The Huns. The Turkic khaganates |
| Shoggoths | Your own created labor class developing autonomy and destroying you | The Mamluk seizure. The Haitian Revolution. Every slave revolt |
| Flying Polyps | The incomprehensible underground force that predates your civilization and will outlast it | Earthquakes. Volcanic eruptions. Geological time itself as an enemy |

### The Pattern of Retreat

The Elder Things' decline follows the exact trajectory of civilizational collapse as described by historians from Ibn Khaldun to Gibbon:

1. Loss of founding capability (creating life, interstellar travel)
2. Military defeat by external powers (Cthulhu spawn, Mi-Go)
3. Territorial contraction (global → Antarctic)
4. Internal rebellion (Shoggoths)
5. Retreat underground (the tunnels)
6. Cultural degradation (declining mural quality)
7. Extinction — or something worse than extinction

This sequence is the Keeper's structural guide for the emotional arc of the mural sequence. Each mural should feel like the next chapter of the same story — a story the players' own civilization is also telling, whether it knows it or not.

---

## 7. USAGE RULES

1. **The Keeper never names these parallels to players.** They operate beneath the surface. A player who recognizes the pattern gets a private thrill of recognition. A player who doesn't still feels the archetypal weight.

2. **The analogies are generative, not restrictive.** If the Keeper needs to improvise a mural detail, an NPC reaction, or a journal fragment, these parallels provide a bank of emotional textures and structural patterns to draw from. They are not the only textures available.

3. **The parallels work in both directions.** The Mi-Go are illuminated by comparison to the Mongols, but the Mongols are also illuminated by comparison to the Mi-Go. If a player with historical knowledge draws the connection, the Keeper should let that insight deepen the horror rather than deflecting it.

4. **The human parallels are more disturbing, not less.** The point is not that cosmic horrors are "really" historical events. The point is that historical events carry the same structural grammar as cosmic horror — and recognizing this makes both more unsettling. The Mongol invasion of Khwarazm and the Mi-Go invasion of the northern lands produce the same response in the civilizations that suffered them: *something came from outside and unmade everything we built, and we still don't fully understand how.*
