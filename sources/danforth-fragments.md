# DANFORTH FRAGMENTS — THE SUNKEN CITY VISIONS

## Keeper Reference for Session 4 Dream and Vision Content

*Fragments the Keeper can write into player journals during Session 4.*
*Carries the Atlantis/R'lyeh resonance forward from Mural 4 into the vision sequence.*
*Not all fragments will be used. The Keeper selects based on what this specific player has seen and what the session requires.*

---

## CONTEXT

In Session 4 ("The Return"), the players cross back over the mountains. During the crossing, Danforth's vision occurs — or rather, the ceremony's version of it occurs, distributed across the players rather than concentrated in one person. The Keeper writes fragments into player journals: dream impressions, half-formed visions, sensory intrusions that arrive mid-scene without explanation.

Lovecraft's Danforth saw something beyond the violet western mountains that broke him. His whispered fragments — "the black pit," "the carven rim," "the proto-Shoggoths," "windowless solids with five dimensions," "the nameless cylinders," "the elder pharos," "Yog-Sothoth," "the primal white jelly," "the color out of space," "the wings," "the eyes in darkness," "the moon-ladder," "the original, the eternal, the undying" — are the canonical list. The fragments below extend this list with material that carries the specific resonance of the Atlantis/sunken-civilization parallel developed in Mural 4.

These fragments work best for players who have spent time with Mural 4 (the wars, the peace, the sinking). For those players, the fragments echo something they have already seen — the sinking of R'lyeh depicted in the Elder Things' carvings — but experienced from a different angle. Not the view from the shore (the Elder Thing watching the Pacific descend) but the view from inside — the experience of being in a place that is sinking, that has always been sinking, that was built to accommodate the sinking.

## THE FRAGMENTS

### Fragment Set A — The Sinking (sensory)

These fragments describe the physical sensation of submersion — not drowning, but descending with the city. They should be written into the journal as half-sentences, interrupting whatever the player was last recording.

**A1.**
*— the weight of water that is not water, pressing from above, but the structures do not collapse, they were built for this, the angles hold the pressure the way a hull holds the sea and the descent is not an event it is a condition, a way of —*

**A2.**
*— green light filtering through something denser than ocean, and the buildings are intact, every one of them, the doors still open but the doors do not open the way doors open, they open downward and inward at the same time and the geometry is holding, the geometry was always meant to hold against —*

**A3.**
*— I understood for a moment that the sinking was not a catastrophe. It was an arrival. The surface was the temporary state. Below is where it was always going. Below is where the architecture makes —*

Each of these fragments should end mid-thought. The journal entry does not complete. The next entry the player writes should pick up as if the fragment never happened — or with a brief note of confusion, as if the player's character lost a moment.

### Fragment Set B — The Vigil (emotional)

These fragments carry the emotional content of the lone Elder Thing figure from Mural 4, Layer 4 — the watcher standing at the edge of the Pacific after R'lyeh sank. They are not about the sunken city. They are about the experience of watching something disappear beneath the surface and knowing it is still there.

**B1.**
*— standing at an edge that used to be a coast and is now just an edge, and the water is flat and the wind has stopped and nothing is visible below the surface but the surface itself is wrong, it carries a pattern, a grid, the ghost of streets still —*

**B2.**
*— how long does a vigil last when the thing you are watching for does not die? The water rose. The spires went under. The angles are still there beneath the surface. I can feel them the way you feel a sound too low to hear, in the bones, in the —*

**B3.**
*— the dreams come from below. Not up through the water like something surfacing. Through the water as if the water is not there. As if the water is a formality, a veil that the dreams do not acknowledge. The sleeper does not know it is under the ocean. The sleeper does not experience depth. The sleeper dreams as if it is still on the surface, and the dreams reach the surface as if nothing —*

### Fragment Set C — The Architecture (perceptual)

These fragments describe the inside of R'lyeh — or something that shares R'lyeh's organizing principles. They are not coherent architectural descriptions. They are the experience of a human nervous system encountering non-Euclidean space: the proprioceptive confusion, the failure of spatial reasoning, the nausea.

**C1.**
*— the floor slopes in a direction I cannot name. Not up. Not down. The direction that is left over when you subtract up and down from the total number of directions available. My feet are on the surface and the surface is level and the surface slopes and these three things are simultaneously —*

**C2.**
*— I passed through a doorway and the room I entered was the room I left. Not a circle. Not a loop. The same room, seen from inside itself, containing itself as a subset. The walls are the exterior. The exterior is one of the walls. I sat down and the —*

**C3.**
*— the window looks out on the interior. The interior contains the building. The building is inside the room that is inside the building. The geometry is consistent. I checked. I counted corners. There are the right number of corners. They are the right angles. They are not the right angles. They are angles I have not —*

### Fragment Set D — The Dreamer (theological)

These fragments approach Cthulhu — not by name, not by description, but by the quality of the dreaming. They describe the experience of proximity to a consciousness that is not awake and not dead, whose dreams are not private but architectural: the dreams build. The dreams have geometry. The dreams are the medium through which the sunken city maintains its coherence.

**D1.**
*— the city does not persist because its materials are durable. The city persists because it is being dreamed. The dreamer does not know it is dreaming. The dream does not know it is a city. The city does not know it is a dream. But the walls hold because the dreaming continues and the dreaming continues because —*

**D2.**
*— I could feel the dreaming the way you feel weather. It was ambient. It was everywhere in the water. It was not directed at me. It was not directed at anything. It was the medium. The water dreams. The stone dreams. The angles between the walls dream. Everything in the radius of the sleeper participates in the dreaming and the radius is —*

**D3.**
*— not dead. The phrase is not metaphorical. Not sleeping in the sense of resting before waking. Sleeping in the sense that sleep is a state, a mode of existence, a way of being present without acting, and the presence fills the ocean and the ocean fills the —*

### Fragment Set E — The Return (the ceremony's own)

These fragments are not about R'lyeh. They are about the experience of returning from the vision — the moment where the player's character comes back to the surface, to Antarctica, to the plane, to the present. They describe the residue. The vision leaves something behind.

**E1.**
*— and then the mountains again. The violet line on the western horizon. The sky. The wind. The sound of the engine. All of it exactly as it was. Except that now I know the sky extends downward as well as upward, and the ocean is not a barrier but a lens, and something beneath the lens is —*

**E2.**
*— my hands. I looked at my hands. They were shaking. Not from cold. From the recognition that the geometry I had been inside is not confined to the city that sank. It is in the angles of the mountains. It is in the pattern of the ice. It is faint, diluted, almost nothing — but it is there. The dreaming reaches this far. The dreaming reaches —*

**E3.**
*— I cannot write what I saw because what I saw is not a thing that happened. It is a thing that is happening. It did not occur in the past. It is not occurring in the present. It occurs in the tense that does not exist in English, the tense that means "has always been occurring and has never begun." The Elder Things' word for it — if they had a word — would be carved in dots I cannot read, and the dots would be arranged in a pattern that is simultaneously the word and the thing the word —*

## DEPLOYMENT GUIDANCE

### Selection Principles

The Keeper does not use all fragments. Selection depends on:

1. **What this player has seen.** A player who examined Mural 4 in detail gets fragments from Sets A-D. A player who passed through Mural 4 quickly gets only Set E (the residue, without the vision that produced it — which is its own kind of horror: feeling the aftermath of something you didn't witness).

2. **The player's character.** A scientist gets the perceptual fragments (Set C) — the architecture that violates the rules their training depends on. A person of faith or philosophical bent gets the theological fragments (Set D) — the dreaming that has the quality of prayer or divine presence. A practical, action-oriented character gets the sensory fragments (Set A) — the physical experience of being in a place that is sinking.

3. **The session's emotional register.** Session 4 is about the return — the moral weight of deciding what to do with what you know. The fragments should deepen that weight, not distract from it. One or two fragments per player is usually enough. The fragments are punctuation, not prose.

### Timing

Fragments appear in player journals during the mountain crossing in Session 4, Act I. They interrupt the journal's normal register — clinical observation, logistical detail, emotional reflection — with material that does not belong to the player's conscious experience. The player's character may or may not acknowledge the fragment in subsequent journal entries. The Keeper does not force acknowledgment. The fragment is there. What the player does with it is their choice.

### The Entry That Stops Mid-Sentence

At least one fragment per player should end mid-sentence. This is Lovecraft's technique: Danforth's whispered list ends with "the original, the eternal, the undying" — and then nothing. The sentence does not complete because the vision exceeds the capacity of language to contain it. The Keeper replicates this by writing a fragment that builds toward a revelation and then stops. The missing end of the sentence is the most important part. It lives in the player's imagination, where it will be worse than anything the Keeper could write.

## CONNECTION TO THE MURAL SEQUENCE

The fragments are designed to rhyme with Mural 4. If a player saw the sinking panel — R'lyeh descending beneath the Pacific, the Elder Thing watching from the shore — and then receives Fragment B2 ("how long does a vigil last when the thing you are watching for does not die?"), the echo should register physically. The mural was carved history, observed from outside. The fragment is the same event experienced from inside. The player has crossed the boundary between record and experience. They are no longer reading about the sunken city. They are, for a moment, in it.

This is the Atlantis parallel carried to its conclusion. Plato's readers can only read about Atlantis. The Elder Things could only carve about R'lyeh. The players — through the vision — go where neither the philosopher nor the carver could follow. They are inside the myth. And the myth is still sinking.
