# MURAL EXTENSIONS — ENRICHED CONTENT FOR MURALS 4 AND 5

## Keeper Reference for Expanded Mural Descriptions

*Supplements the seven-mural sequence in the session structure and preset.*
*Provides the Keeper with layered descriptive material for the two murals that carry the heaviest cosmological and historical weight.*
*The Keeper draws from this material when players explore the relevant corridors. Not all of it will surface in a single playthrough.*

---

## MURAL 4 — THE WARS AND THE PEACE

### Current Scope (from preset)

Mural 4 covers: the Cthulhu spawn filtering down from cosmic infinity, war on a planetary scale, retreat, peace and territorial division, and the sinking of the Pacific lands taking R'lyeh and the spawn with them.

This is too much narrative for a single encounter. The material below splits it into discoverable layers. A player who lingers in this corridor sees more. A player who passes through quickly gets the compressed version. The Keeper adjusts depth based on player engagement.

### Layer 1 — The Arrival of the Spawn (the compressed version)

**What the player sees:** A section of wall where the carving style changes abruptly. The earlier murals — Arrival, Creation, Flourishing — were carved with extraordinary delicacy, complex layering, multiple activities depicted simultaneously. Here the chisel marks carry urgency. The composition is less balanced. Figures crowd the frame.

**What the Keeper describes:** Shapes descending. Not from the sky — from somewhere the sky leads to. The Elder Thing carvers depicted the Cthulhu spawn as massive, octopoid, winged — but the proportions feel wrong. Not badly carved. Wrong in a different way. As if the carver was rendering something they could see clearly but whose geometry did not translate into their aesthetic vocabulary. The five-pointed symmetry that governs every other mural is absent from these figures. The spawn do not fit the grid. The stone seems to resist them.

**Sensory note for the Keeper:** The player should feel what the Elder Thing carver felt — the frustration and unease of trying to represent something whose organizing principles are incommensurable with your own. If the player has seen Murals 1-3, they know what this civilization's art looks like at its best. This is not worse art. It is art straining against the limits of its own system.

### Layer 2 — The War (if the player examines closely)

**What the player sees:** A long horizontal panel — the widest single carving in this corridor. Battle scenes spanning what appears to be an ocean, with land masses visible on both sides. The scale is planetary. Cities on one side (five-pointed, recognizable as Elder Thing architecture). On the other side — structures that the eye cannot settle on. The buildings, if they are buildings, do not resolve.

**What the Keeper describes:** The Elder Things fought the Cthulhu spawn in the oceans they had always controlled. The murals show the Elder Things driven from the land into the sea — and then driven from parts of the sea as well. The spawn's territory is depicted as the new Pacific lands, but the architecture the spawn raised there is rendered in a way that makes the viewer's eyes slide off it. The angles are present in the stone but they do not connect the way angles should. The Elder Thing carvers — master artists who could depict their own billion-year civilization with crystalline precision — could not carve R'lyeh. What appears on the wall is their best attempt, and it is not enough.

**The key detail:** In one section of the battle mural, the Elder Thing carvers depicted their enemies' city from above — as if seen in flight or from a great height. The ground plan should be visible. It is not. The viewer can see walls, can see structures, can see spaces between structures — but the relationship between inside and outside does not resolve. Doors that appear horizontal also appear vertical. The plan simultaneously depicts a contained space and an open one. The carver did not make an error. The carver faithfully reproduced what they saw, and what they saw does not obey the rules that govern Elder Thing architecture — or human perception.

**Keeper note:** This is the Atlantis resonance at its most direct. Plato described Atlantis with obsessive geometric precision — concentric rings, exact measurements, specific metals. The precision was the point: Atlantis was comprehensible, just grander. Lovecraft inverts this. R'lyeh is depicted with the Elder Things' characteristic precision, but the precision reveals incomprehensibility rather than concealing it. The more carefully the carvers rendered R'lyeh, the less sense it made. Both Plato and the Elder Thing carvers are saying "this civilization built in ways that exceed us" — Plato through idealized order, the Elder Things through irreducible strangeness.

### Layer 3 — The Peace Treaty (if the player spends significant time)

**What the player sees:** A quieter panel after the battle sequence. Two groups of figures facing each other across a carved line that might represent an ocean, a boundary, or something more abstract. The Elder Thing figures are rendered in full detail — the star-shaped heads, the barrel bodies, the wings folded. The Cthulhu spawn figures across the line are rendered with the same straining quality as before, but here there is something new: the two groups' carving styles bleed into each other at the boundary line. Where the Elder Thing figures approach the line, they become slightly less symmetrical. Where the spawn figures approach it, they become slightly more ordered. As if proximity to the other forced a partial accommodation.

**What the Keeper describes:** This is the most politically complex carving in the entire city. Two civilizations — both alien, both immeasurably old, both wielding power on a planetary scale — negotiated a territorial boundary across the surface of the Earth. The Elder Things kept the sea and the older lands. The Cthulhu spawn took the new Pacific territories. The carving shows this as a moment of contact between two incompatible systems of order. Neither side is depicted as victorious. Neither is depicted as defeated. The boundary is a compromise — and the carvers recorded it with the same care they gave to their greatest achievements and their worst catastrophes.

**The key detail:** Along the boundary line itself, the dot-group writing system appears — the same raised-dot clusters visible throughout the city. But here, the dots are arranged differently than anywhere else. The clusters are larger. The spacing is irregular. And on the spawn's side of the line, there are marks that might be a different writing system entirely — or the same system distorted by proximity to non-Euclidean geometry. The treaty may have been written. The languages may have met on the wall. The player cannot read either one.

**Keeper note:** The peace treaty is the detail that gives the Cthulhu-Atlantis parallel its tragic shape. Plato's Atlantis coexisted with Athens before the war. There was a period of contact, exchange, even mutual recognition. The catastrophe that destroyed Atlantis also destroyed that equilibrium. Similarly, the Elder Things and Cthulhu spawn achieved a stable geopolitical arrangement — two great powers on one planet, each acknowledging the other's domain. The sinking didn't just destroy the spawn's territory. It destroyed the arrangement. It removed one party from a treaty that had kept the world in balance. What the Elder Things lost when the Pacific sank was not just an enemy but a counterpart.

### Layer 4 — The Sinking (a single devastating panel)

**What the player sees:** The corridor's final panel. Simpler than what came before. A vast area of carved ocean — the Pacific rendered as a textured surface taking up most of the wall. And in the center, sinking into it, the shapes of R'lyeh. Not destroyed. Descending. The water closing over structures that are still intact, still geometrically impossible, still present — just going down.

**What the Keeper describes:** The Pacific lands sank. The carving shows this with a restraint that is more disturbing than any depiction of violence. No catastrophic waves. No broken structures. The land simply descended, and the ocean simply rose, and the city that the Elder Things could never quite carve went with it. The spawn are not depicted dying. They are depicted going. And beneath the carved ocean surface, very faintly — as if the carver was recording something they knew but could not verify — the outlines of R'lyeh continue. Below the waterline. Still there.

**The key detail:** In the lower corner of the sinking panel, separate from the main composition, there is a small carved figure. An Elder Thing. Alone. Looking toward the place where the Pacific was. The posture — wings partially spread, tentacles oriented toward the vanished land — could be read as vigilance, grief, or fear. The Keeper does not resolve which. The Elder Things' enemies sank beneath the ocean — and the Elder Things carved a figure watching the place where they used to be. The vigil has lasted since the Cretaceous.

## MURAL 5 — THE SECOND INVASION

### Current Scope (from preset)

Mural 5 covers: the Mi-Go arriving from Yuggoth, the Elder Things attempting to resume interstellar travel and failing, being trapped on Earth, and retreating to Antarctica.

### Layer 1 — The Mi-Go Arrive (the compressed version)

**What the player sees:** The murals become noticeably smaller. After the planetary-scale battle panels of Mural 4, this corridor's carvings are cramped, as if the available wall space contracted — or as if the carvers were working in a world that had grown smaller. The figures are rendered with precision but less ambition. The compositions are tighter. The cosmic scope of the earlier murals has narrowed.

**What the Keeper describes:** The Mi-Go did not arrive the way the Cthulhu spawn arrived. The spawn descended from cosmic infinity — a phrase the Elder Thing carvers rendered as shapes emerging from an undefined upper region of the wall. The Mi-Go are depicted differently. They come through the sky. Not from above it — through it. The carving shows the sky itself as a medium, and the Mi-Go as things that move within it the way the Elder Things once moved through ocean. The sky is not empty space the Mi-Go cross. The sky is their element.

**Sensory note for the Keeper:** This is the Tengrist resonance made visual. The Elder Things are builders of enclosed spaces — every surface they touch becomes a wall to carve on, a ceiling to vault, a floor to tile with five-pointed geometry. The Mi-Go are depicted as creatures of openness. The carvers had no visual vocabulary for this. Their art is an art of surfaces, of interiors, of contained space. The Mi-Go exist in the space their art cannot contain. The murals show this as a kind of formal crisis — the carvers depicting creatures that refuse to be bounded by the frame.

### Layer 2 — The Invasion (if the player examines closely)

**What the player sees:** A sequence of panels showing the Elder Things' northern territories emptying. Cities depicted in earlier murals as thriving — recognizable by their five-pointed ground plans, their conical towers, their tubular bridges — shown in successive panels with fewer Elder Thing figures. Then with none. Then with shapes moving through them that are not Elder Things. The Mi-Go are depicted as half-fungous, half-crustacean — but what strikes the viewer is not their biology. It is what they do to the cities. They do not destroy them. They do not occupy them in any recognizable way. They mine them.

**What the Keeper describes:** The Mi-Go invasion was not a conquest in the way the Cthulhu war was a conquest. The Cthulhu spawn built their own cities, claimed their own territory, established a parallel civilization. The Mi-Go did something the Elder Thing carvers found harder to represent: they dismantled. The northern cities were not razed. They were extracted from. The murals show Mi-Go figures in and around Elder Thing structures, but the structures are being modified — tunnels driven through foundations, mineral deposits beneath the cities accessed, the infrastructure repurposed for extraction rather than habitation. The Mi-Go did not want the cities. They wanted what was under the cities.

**The key detail:** In one panel, an Elder Thing city is shown in cross-section — a cutaway view revealing the geological strata beneath the foundations. The Mi-Go are depicted in the tunnels below. The Elder Things are depicted on the surface above. The two groups never face each other directly in this panel. The Mi-Go operate beneath. The Elder Things stand on a surface that has been hollowed out from below. The carver understood what was happening: the ground itself was being taken from under them.

**Keeper note:** This is the steppe-invasion pattern at its most precise. The Mongols did not want Khwarazm's cities for their own sake. They wanted the trade routes, the resources, the strategic geography. The cities were obstacles to be neutralized, not prizes to be won. The Mi-Go's relationship to Elder Thing architecture is identical: indifference to the cultural achievement, interest only in the extractable resource. The Elder Thing carvers — for whom architecture was identity, was memory, was civilization itself — could not fully comprehend an enemy that looked at their greatest cities and saw raw material.

### Layer 3 — The Lost Secret (if the player spends significant time)

**What the player sees:** The most emotionally charged panel in Mural 5. A group of Elder Things depicted in a posture the player has not seen before — wings fully extended, bodies oriented upward, tentacles reaching toward the upper edge of the wall. Above them: nothing. Open space. The sky rendered as blank stone — uncarved, unmarked, empty.

**What the Keeper describes:** The Elder Things tried to leave. When the Mi-Go drove them from the north, the Elder Things attempted to resume interstellar travel — the capability that had brought them to Earth in the first place, depicted with such confidence in Mural 1. They made the traditional preparations. They assumed the posture of departure. They reached for the sky that had once been their highway between stars.

It did not work. The secret was lost. The method — whatever biological, technological, or ontological capacity had once allowed them to traverse cosmic space — was gone. The panel shows them reaching and not arriving. The empty sky above them is not a carving of emptiness. It is the absence of the carving that should be there — the transit, the departure, the stars. The carver left the stone blank because there was nothing to depict. The journey did not happen.

**The key detail:** Below this panel, in smaller and more careful carving than the main composition, there is a single line of dot-group writing. It is the longest continuous line of the Elder Things' script visible anywhere in the corridors the players have explored. The Keeper should describe it as deliberate, dense, and unhurried — as if whoever carved it took great care to get every cluster exactly right. The player cannot read it. But the placement — directly beneath the panel showing the loss of interstellar travel — suggests that this line of text records something the Elder Things considered important enough to write with extraordinary precision at the moment they realized they were trapped.

**Keeper note:** This is the hinge of the Elder Things' tragedy. Every loss that follows — territorial retreat, the decline of art, the Shoggoth rebellion, the ice, the tunnels, the end — flows from this moment. They could have left. They had always been able to leave. The Mi-Go retained that ability. The Cthulhu spawn had arrived from cosmic infinity and could presumably return to it. The Elder Things alone, among all the great powers that had touched Earth, became earthbound. They joined humanity's condition — trapped on a single planet, in a single biosphere, with no way out. The empathy the players will feel in Session 3 ("they were men") begins here, in this mural, whether the players know it or not.

### Layer 4 — The Retreat South (a transitional panel)

**What the player sees:** A map. The Elder Things' territory depicted in successive panels, shrinking. The northern hemisphere empties. The equatorial regions thin. The southern lands contract toward a single point. Antarctica.

**What the Keeper describes:** The retreat was slow. Geological time slow. The murals compress it, but the intervals between panels represent millions of years. The Elder Things did not flee. They withdrew — methodically, with the discipline of a civilization that had existed for a billion years and knew how to contract without collapsing. Cities were abandoned in order. Populations consolidated. The sacred first landing site — Antarctica — became not just the capital but the last refuge.

The final panel in this sequence shows the Antarctic city from above. The five-pointed ground plan fills the wall. It is the most detailed single architectural rendering in the entire mural sequence. The carvers lavished attention on it — every tower, every bridge, every public square with its star-shaped floor. This is not the city as it was at its height. This is the city as it was when it became the only city. The detail is an act of defiance: we have lost everything else, but this we will record perfectly.

## KEEPER INTEGRATION NOTES

### How These Layers Surface in Play

The Keeper does not dump all four layers on a player who enters a mural corridor. The layers are gated by engagement:

- **Passing through:** Layer 1 only. Quick, compressed, atmospheric. The player gets the narrative beat and moves on.
- **Stopping to look:** Layers 1-2. The player sees the main composition and one or two key details. Enough to build the picture.
- **Examining carefully:** Layers 1-3. The player discovers the details that carry the heaviest emotional and thematic weight. This is the reward for patience.
- **Lingering (rare):** All four layers. The player has chosen to spend significant time in this corridor. The Keeper provides the full depth.

### Connecting Murals 4 and 5

If a player sees both murals, the Keeper should ensure the contrast registers: Mural 4 is big, violent, planetary in scope — an enemy that challenged the Elder Things' power. Mural 5 is quiet, methodical, suffocating — an enemy that made their power irrelevant. The Cthulhu spawn were a rival civilization. The Mi-Go were something worse: a force that did not recognize civilization as a category worth contesting.

### The Emotional Arc

Murals 1-3 build admiration. Mural 4 introduces catastrophe on a scale that dwarfs the admiration. Mural 5 introduces a different kind of catastrophe — not dramatic but entropic. By the time the player reaches Mural 6 (the decline), they should already feel the weight of inevitability. Mural 7 (the crude carvings) completes the sequence by showing what came after the carvers stopped carving. The arc is: brilliance → war → entrapment → decline → replacement. The players are walking through the life cycle of a civilization in reverse, reading the autobiography of the dead.
