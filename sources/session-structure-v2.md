# Session Structure v2 — Five Sessions

*Session 0 (character co-creation) + 4 play sessions.*
*Replaces v1 (8 sessions). Designed for density over runway.*

---

## Design Principles

- **Five sessions total.** Session 0 is character co-creation and world discovery. Sessions 1–4 are play.
- Every play session has its own dramatic question, answered by session end.
- The Lovecraft Curve spans all four play sessions but each session is satisfying alone.
- **Compression serves horror.** Less time to rationalize. The anomalies pile up before the mind can file them away. The descent is felt in the body, not just the intellect.
- No filler. Every scene advances the gnosis sequence. If a moment doesn't change what the players know or feel, cut it.

## Death & Survival Rule

No permanent player death by default. If a player would die, the Keeper activates a **rescue pivot** — an environmental intervention that saves them at a cost. The cost must be real: an NPC dies, equipment is lost, a secret is exposed, a path is closed. The MC can override this rule live.

## Knowledge Rule

All players start with the same base knowledge from the briefing. No asymmetric revelations about the core mystery unless the MC decides otherwise during play. Private Keeper channels exist for secret actions and personal reactions.

## SESSION 0: "THE CIRCLE"
### Character Co-Creation + World Discovery

**Purpose:** Build the characters together. Receive the briefing. Establish relationships and tensions. Depart.

**This is not a briefing session — it is an initiation.** The players don't just learn about the expedition; they discover the world they're entering and the people they're entering it with. The Keeper guides character creation as conversation, shaped by the preset.

### Structure

**Phase 1 — The World (20 min)**
The Keeper sets the stage. 1933. Antarctica. What is known. What Dyer published. What happened to the Miskatonic expedition three years ago — the public story. The MC narrates the atmospheric introduction: the era, the geography, the state of polar exploration, the mood of the world.

Players explore the universe. They can ask questions. The Keeper answers from the published record — not from the truth.

**Phase 2 — The Characters (40 min)**
Each player creates their character through conversation with the Keeper and MC. Not stat blocks — identities.

The Keeper asks:
- Who are you? What's your name, your background, your expertise?
- Why would you join a dangerous Antarctic expedition in 1933?
- What do you want from this? (The real answer, not the polite one.)
- What are you afraid of? (Not monsters — something human.)
- What's your relationship to the others? (At least one connection per player.)

Characters are written into journals by the Keeper: qualities (not numbers), relationships, motives, fears. These journals are the player's identity for the rest of the ceremony.

**Phase 3 — The Briefing (20 min)**
The patron's curated briefing is presented. Edited Dyer excerpts, selected Lake bulletins, maps, the patron's framing. Players discuss it in character. The cracks in the briefing may or may not be noticed.

The patron's spin: the first expedition's tragedy was bad weather and bad luck. The anomalous specimens are "unclassified fossils of extraordinary preservation." This expedition is better prepared.

**Phase 4 — The Departure (10 min)**
The ceremony of leaving. Each player states — in character — why they're going. The Keeper records it. These statements will be echoed back in Session 4 when the players must decide what to do with what they know.

The MC closes with: the ship pulls away from the dock.

**Cosmological register:** Stratum I only. The world is rational. The briefing is persuasive. Everything is exactly as it should be. The only wrongness is human — conflicting motives, edited truths, the patron's hidden agenda.

## SESSION 1: "THE ICE"
### Transit + Discovery

**Dramatic question:** Is this place hostile — or is it wrong?

**Lovecraft Curve phases:** Transit → Discovery

**Covers:** The sea crossing, base camp, first anomalies, Lake's camp.

### Structure

**Act I — The Crossing (30 min)**

At sea. The world shrinks. The MC establishes the physical reality — the ship, the cold, the vastness. Character bonding and friction during the voyage.

Key beats:
- First mirages on the horizon — "distant mountains floated in the sky as enchanted cities." Dismissed as atmospheric.
- The dogs are uneasy. The dog handler interprets this as weather sensitivity.
- Something doesn't add up about the patron's cargo or orders. Political subplot enters.
- First sight of the Ross Ice Barrier. Two hundred feet of vertical ice. The world they knew is behind them.

**Stratum IV phenomena (subtle):** Wind sounds faintly musical through the ship's rigging. A mirage lingers longer than it should. One player has a dream they don't remember clearly — the Keeper writes a fragment into their journal between acts.

**Act II — Base Camp (20 min)**

Establishing camp near McMurdo Sound. First borings, surveys, radio check-ins. The Antarctic is beautiful and lethal.

Key beats:
- Odd formations in rock samples. Triangular markings in strata that shouldn't contain them.
- Instruments behave strangely near certain locations.
- Dogs refuse food. Refuse to face one direction.
- A night event: a sound the wind doesn't explain. Equipment moved. A dog has to be restrained.

Each anomaly is individually explainable. Together they form a pattern. The Keeper lets the rational explanations hold — barely.

**Act III — Lake's Camp (40 min)**

Pushing inland. Finding traces of the 1930 Miskatonic expedition.

Key beats:
- The destroyed advance camp. Three years of ice over the remains. Star-shaped mounds that look deliberate.
- Empty specimen containers. Dissection tools laid out with care — by whom?
- Lake's private field journal with increasingly frantic entries.
- The official "camp disaster" story collapses piece by piece.
- The definitive evidence: Gedney still missing. Specimens gone — or one that remains, frozen, that shouldn't exist. The star-shaped graves — built with care, with ritual.

**Climax:** The expedition has changed nature. This is no longer geology. The mountains are visible beyond the camp. Higher than anything on Earth. Through binoculars — are those cave mouths?

**Resolution:** The question is no longer "if" but "what."

**Hook:** The patron's orders say: cross the mountains.

**Cosmological register:** Stratum I → II boundary. Evidence accumulates. The rational frame strains but holds. The Demiurge's traces are present but unrecognized.

**Phenomena:** Wind piping (faint). Equipment anomalies near five-pointed formations. Dogs as early warning. Radio begins to degrade.

## SESSION 2: "THE MOUNTAINS"
### Discovery → Revelation

**Dramatic question:** Who built this — and what happened to them?

**Lovecraft Curve phases:** Discovery peaks → Revelation

**Covers:** The mountain approach, the crossing decision, first entry into the city, the murals.

### Structure

**Act I — The Approach (20 min)**

The impossible range. Peaks exceeding 35,000 feet. Black, bare of snow. Cube-like formations clinging to the heights. Cave mouths unnervingly regular.

Key beats:
- The mirage of a city beyond the peaks — persistent and detailed. Dismissed as atmospheric effect.
- The wind sounds like musical piping. Geological readings make no sense.
- A player finds a page from Dyer's private journal (not the published account) that describes exactly what they're seeing.
- The decision. Crossing means limited fuel for return. The patron's orders say go. Self-preservation says don't. The group decides — or fractures.

**Climax of Act I:** They cross. The city is real. It stretches to the horizon. Every rational explanation dies.

**Act II — The City (50 min)**

First entry into the ruins. Cyclopean architecture older than complex life on Earth. Five-pointed geometry everywhere.

This is the longest act in the ceremony. The players explore the city. The murals tell the story:

**Mural sequence (Keeper reveals in layers as players explore):**

1. **Arrival** — Star-headed things descending to a lifeless Earth from cosmic space.
2. **Creation** — Building cities. Creating life from inorganic matter. The Shoggoths — protoplasmic servants shaped by hypnotic will.
3. **Flourishing** — Art, science, civilization spanning a billion years. Cities across the globe.
4. **The Wars** — The Cthulhu spawn filtering down from cosmic infinity. War. Retreat. Peace and territorial division. The Pacific lands sink, taking R'lyeh and the Cthulhu spawn.
5. **The Second Invasion** — The Mi-Go arrive from Yuggoth. The Elder Things try to resume interstellar travel and fail. The secret is lost. They are trapped on Earth. Retreat to Antarctica.
6. **The Decline** — The art of creating life lost. Shoggoth rebellions suppressed but never fully ended. The ice advances. The city abandoned above ground. The survivors go underground.
7. **The Crude Carvings** — Overlaid on the originals. A different hand. Something else has been here since the builders left. Something that imitated their art badly.

Players choose which corridors to explore. Some murals are damaged. Some are in a later, cruder style. The Keeper adjusts what's available based on player choices — not every group sees every mural.

**Act III — The Threshold (20 min)**

Two linked revelations:

1. **The violet mountains.** First sighting from the city. A dim, elfin line of pinnacled violet on the western horizon. The murals confirm their existence — and the Elder Things' fear of them. Cities built there crumbled before their time. Something lives there that the Elder Things refused to carve clearly.

2. **The corridor down.** Warmer air rises from below. The temperature is wrong — it's getting warmer in Antarctica. The five-pointed star motif in the floor. And from far below — a sound that might be birds.

**Climax:** The players know what the Elder Things were: a civilization. Not monsters. Scientists, artists, builders. The question shifts: what ended them? And what made those crude carvings? And do they go down?

**Resolution:** The corridor waits.

**Cosmological register:** Stratum II fully revealed. Stratum III referenced in murals (Cthulhu wars, Mi-Go invasion). Stratum IV glimpsed in the violet mountains.

**Phenomena:** Cloud formations almost resolve into patterns. Dreams intensify. The nameless scent begins — faint, acrid, organic. Sublingual chatter: the sense of being observed. Intrusive geometry — a player catches themselves thinking in patterns that aren't their own.

## SESSION 3: "THE DESCENT"
### Revelation → Confrontation

**Dramatic question:** What's down there, and what does it cost to know?

**Lovecraft Curve phases:** Revelation completes → Confrontation → Flight begins

**Covers:** The tunnel descent, the underground ecosystem, the empathy turn, the Shoggoth.

**This is the most important session.** Everything before this was preparation. Everything after this is consequence.

### Structure

**Act I — Down (25 min)**

The tunnel entrance. Fifteen-foot carved passages descending into warmth. The five-pointed star motif in the floor.

Key beats:
- Deeper. The carvings degrade, then change — crude spirals and angles carved OVER the originals. Palimpsests.
- Giant blind albino penguins appear, living in the deep tunnels. An entire underground ecosystem. The temperature is wrong.
- The nameless scent grows stronger.
- Elder Thing artifacts: soapstone fragments, tools, evidence of recent passage.
- The dot-group writing system — a language they can see but cannot read.

The Keeper builds pressure. Each step is a Promethean choice — descend further or turn back. The journal entries for this session should feel different from all previous entries: shorter sentences, more sensation, less analysis.

**Act II — The Empathy Turn (25 min)**

They find the bodies.

Headless Elder Things. Coated in dark-green slime. Thick ichor pooling. Recent — not millions of years old. Recent.

The Keeper should take time with this. The description draws on everything the players learned in Session 2:

These beings woke up after millions of years in the ice. They were found by Lake's team. They were attacked — dissected while still alive or recently awakened. They defended themselves. They killed Lake's men and dogs. They took supplies. They tried to go home — back to the city, back to the tunnels, back to the world they remembered.

They didn't make it. Something in the tunnels killed them. Decapitated. The same way the Shoggoths killed in the Permian Age — the same way the murals showed. A hundred and fifty million years later, the pattern repeats.

**"Whatever they had been, they were men."**

The Keeper writes this into every player's journal. Not as narration. As realization. The category "person" just expanded beyond the human, and the players can't put it back.

Silence. Let the players react.

**Act III — Tekeli-li (20 min)**

The sound. Not birds.

A vast, onrushing column of fetid black iridescence filling the fifteen-foot tunnel. Temporary eyes forming and unforming. The nameless scent overwhelming. Crushing penguins underfoot. The thing that the Elder Things created and that eventually destroyed them.

**Tekeli-li. Tekeli-li.**

The Ahrimanic principle in action. Created order become autonomous. The Demiurge's greatest work devouring the Demiurge.

Flight. Pure flight.

**Act IV — Escape (20 min)**

Getting out. Back through the tunnels, back through the city, back to the surface, back to air and light. Running, scrambling, the Shoggoth's cry echoing behind them.

During the escape, someone glimpses something they can't process. The Keeper writes fragments into their journal — half-formed impressions, edges of perception, the sublingual chatter at maximum intensity.

**Resolution:** They made it out. The knowledge that the Elder Things were the lesser horror — and that the greater horror is still down there.

**Hook:** The plane is where they left it. The mountains are ahead. The violet mountains are behind.

**Cosmological register:** Stratum II collapses into confrontation. Stratum III implied (the Shoggoth as created power operating beyond its creators' control). Stratum IV ambient — all phenomena active: the nameless scent, temperature inversions, intrusive thoughts, peripheral movement, the sense of being observed by something without eyes.

## SESSION 4: "THE RETURN"
### Flight + The Choice

**Dramatic question:** What do you do with what you know?

**Lovecraft Curve phases:** Flight → (no resolution — the wound stays open)

**Covers:** The mountain crossing, the violet mountain vision, the return to base, the moral choice.

### Structure

**Act I — The Crossing (20 min)**

Back at the plane. The mountain crossing ahead. Everyone rattled, some barely functional.

The flight over the mountains. The wind piping through the caves below — louder now, or are they just listening differently?

Key beats:
- Someone looks back at the city. It's smaller from up here, but it extends further than they realized — endlessly in both directions along the foothills.
- A player's instruments give a reading that can't be right. The Keeper notes it but doesn't explain.
- The pass. 24,000 feet. Devilish cold. The engine sounds wrong. Everything sounds wrong.

**Act II — The Vision (15 min)**

Someone looks west. Or up.

The sky above the violet western mountains. The clouds — the same clouds that have been "almost resolving into patterns" since Session 2 — now almost resolve again.

The Keeper writes into one player's journal:

Selected fragments from Danforth's list. Not all of them. Not in order. Not explained.

> "...something in the clouds above the western range. Not a mirage. Not exactly. The shapes were — they were like looking at a sentence in a language that uses a geometry you don't have. The black pit. A rim, carved. Something with five dimensions and no windows. A light — not a light, a pharos, a signal from something that was not calling you but was not not calling you. And behind it, or under it, or as it: something original, eternal, undying. Not a being. The condition for beings. The..."

The entry stops mid-sentence. The Keeper closes the journal.

This is the ceremony's contact with Stratum IV. It lasts one moment. It is not repeated. It cannot be discussed — when the player tries to describe it to others, the words come out wrong or not at all.

**Act III — The Return to Base (15 min)**

They land. McMurdo Sound. The ship. The radio. The world.

The patron's representative (or radio contact) wants a report. What did you find? Do you have specimens? Photographs? Coordinates? Can we send a larger team?

The world wants an explanation. The world has been waiting.

**Act IV — The Choice (40 min)**

This is the climax of the entire ceremony. Not an action scene — a moral scene.

Each player faces the question Dyer faced: **what do you tell?**

The Keeper presents the options — not as a menu, but as a lived dilemma:

- **Tell the truth.** Dyer tried this. Nobody believed him. His career was destroyed. His student lost his mind. And the Starkweather-Moore expedition came anyway — the players are proof that telling the truth failed. Does the player try again? Why would it work this time?

- **Tell the patron what they want to hear.** Specimens, coordinates, proof of discovery. This gets funding, resources, institutional support — and guarantees a third expedition, better equipped, larger, aimed at extraction. Whatever is in those tunnels will be disturbed again. The Shoggoths will be found again.

- **Lie.** Say there was nothing. Destroy the evidence. Burn the photographs, lose the specimens, claim it was all geology and weather. This is Dyer's mistake in reverse — not a failed warning but an active coverup. It might work. It also means everything they suffered was for nothing.

- **Keep silent.** Say nothing. Refuse to file a report. Leave the patron hanging. This delays but doesn't resolve. Someone else will go eventually.

- **Something else.** The players may invent an option the MC didn't anticipate. This is the point of the ceremony — to generate choices that emerge from the story rather than from a predetermined list.

The Keeper asks each player individually: **What do you do?**

The answers are written in their journals. Not a group consensus — individual choices. Some may lie. Some may tell truth. Some may keep silent. Some may plan to go back. Some may disagree with each other. The Keeper records it all.

**The Echo:** The Keeper reads back each player's departure statement from Session 0. What they said they wanted. What they said they were afraid of. Then what they chose now. The distance between those statements is the measure of what the ceremony did to them.

**Resolution:** Each player's journal closes with their choice. The ceremony ends. The wound stays open. There is always a deeper layer.

## Campaign Arc — Lovecraft Curve (Compressed)

| Lovecraft Phase | Session | Core Experience |
|----------------|---------|-----------------|
| Preparation | 0 | Character creation, briefing, departure — the rational frame |
| Transit | 1 (Act I–II) | Sea crossing, base camp — the world narrows, first anomalies |
| Discovery | 1 (Act III) – 2 (Act I) | Lake's camp, the mountains — evidence overwhelms the frame |
| Revelation | 2 (Act II–III) | The city, the murals — the Demiurge's autobiography |
| Confrontation | 3 | The empathy turn + the Shoggoth — gnosis at full force |
| Flight | 3 (Act IV) – 4 | Escape, the vision, the choice — the wound that doesn't close |

## Timing Guide (approximate per session)

| Session | Target Duration | Notes |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| 0 — The Circle | 90 min | Can be shorter if players create characters quickly. Can be done as a separate pre-session. |
| 1 — The Ice | 90 min | Three acts. The crossing is brisk; Lake's camp gets the most time. |
| 2 — The Mountains | 90 min | The city/murals act is the longest — allow exploration. Don't rush the murals. |
| 3 — The Descent | 90 min | The empathy turn needs silence. The Shoggoth needs speed. Contrast is everything. |
| 4 — The Return | 90 min | The vision is brief. The choice is long. Give every player time. |

## Decision Points (Player Agency)

| Session | Decision | Stakes | If They Choose No |
|---------|----------|--------|-------------------|
| 0 | Join the expedition | Personal — what they're willing to risk | The ceremony doesn't begin (out of scope — all players join) |
| 1 | Press inland toward the mountains | Safety vs. discovery | The patron's orders override. An NPC pushes forward. The choice is noted but the expedition continues. |
| 2 | Cross the mountains | Point of no return — fuel limits the return | Someone in the party or the patron's orders force the crossing. But the player's reluctance is recorded and pays off later. |
| 2 | Enter the city | Curiosity vs. self-preservation | Genuinely optional. A player can stay at the plane. They miss the murals but are present for the crossing back. |
| 3 | Descend into the tunnels | The deepest Promethean choice | Genuinely optional. A player can refuse. They stay in the city. They hear the Shoggoth's cry from above. They don't see the dead Elder Things. Their gnosis is incomplete — and that has consequences in Session 4. |
| 4 | What to tell | The entire moral weight of the ceremony | There is no wrong answer. Every choice has consequences the ceremony doesn't show — they live in the player's imagination after the ceremony closes. |
