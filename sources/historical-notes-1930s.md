## ASTRONOMY & SPACE

- **1929** — Edwin Hubble publishes evidence that the universe is expanding. Einstein had previously inserted a mathematical constant into his equations to avoid this conclusion; he later called it his greatest blunder.
- **1930** — Pluto discovered by Clyde Tombaugh at Lowell Observatory. Tombaugh was 23, self-taught, from a Kansas farm family. The name was suggested by Venetia Burney, an 11-year-old from Oxford, passed through her grandfather to Oxford astronomer Herbert Hall Turner, who cabled it to the United States. Staff at Lowell voted unanimously in favor on May 1, 1930.
- **1932** — Karl Jansky at Bell Telephone Labs discovers radio waves emanating from the center of the Milky Way while trying to identify sources of telephone interference. This is the founding moment of radio astronomy.
- **1932** — James Chadwick discovers the neutron. Carl Anderson detects antimatter, confirming a theoretical prediction that for every particle an opposite exists.
- **1937** — Grote Reber builds the first purpose-built radio telescope in his backyard in Wheaton, Illinois, and operates it alone for years.

## ANTARCTIC EXPEDITIONS 1928–1937

### Byrd's First Antarctic Expedition (1928–30)
- Established **Little America I** on the Ross Ice Shelf near the Bay of Whales — the first American base on the continent.
- November 29, 1929: Byrd makes the first flight over the South Pole.
- A documentary, *With Byrd at the South Pole*, won an Academy Award in 1930.
- Byrd returned to the United States in June 1930 to ticker-tape parades. It was the start of the Great Depression.

### Byrd's Second Antarctic Expedition (1933–35)
- Funded through a combination of small public donations, corporate product donations (food, fuel, supplies), and the National Geographic Society.
- Two ships: the **Jacob Ruppert** (metal freighter) and the **Bear of Oakland** (wooden barkentine, formerly an Arctic icebreaker). Four airplanes, four tractors.
- Base: **Little America II**, Ross Ice Shelf, Bay of Whales.
- Winter 1934–35: Byrd occupied an advance weather station alone, 123 miles south of Little America. He suffered acute carbon monoxide poisoning from a faulty stove. A rescue team reached him after a difficult overland journey.
- Byrd cultivated relationships with President Roosevelt, Henry Ford, and John D. Rockefeller to finance and gain political support for his expeditions.
- Byrd published a memoir of the experience: *Discovery: The Story of the Second Byrd Antarctic Expedition* (1935).

### British Graham Land Expedition (1934–37)
- Small, austere British expedition. Spent two winters on the Antarctic Peninsula with minimal resources.

### Lincoln Ellsworth's Trans-Antarctic Flight (1935)
- November 23, 1935: Ellsworth departed Dundee Island near the tip of the Antarctic Peninsula.
- First flight across the entire continent, landing frequently to wait out storms.
- The flight terminated December 5, approximately 16 miles short of Little America.

### German Antarctic Expedition (1938–39)
- Ship: **Schwabenland**, a catapult ship carrying two seaplanes.
- Mission was explicitly political: to photomap a portion of the continent and establish territorial claims for Germany.
- Mapped Princess Martha Coast and Princess Astrid Coast.

### Territorial politics
By 1933, Britain, Norway, Argentina, France, New Zealand, Australia, and Chile were all making formal territorial claims in Antarctica, drawing sector lines from the South Pole outward. The continent was a politically contested space with no governing framework.

## EXPEDITION PSYCHOLOGY & MEDICINE

### "Polar Madness"
- The term was in common use among expedition circles by the early 20th century. The first ship to overwinter in Antarctica, the Belgian *Belgica* (1897–99), had several crew members go insane during the polar night. Ship's surgeon Frederick Cook documented the phenomenon and prescribed fresh meat, bright light, exercise, and sustained hope as the treatment regimen.
- Cook wrote in his expedition notes: *"The human system accommodates itself sluggishly and poorly to the strange conditions of the polar seasons."*

### Documented historical cases of psychological breakdown
- **Professor Edgeworth David**, British Antarctic Expedition (1907–09): became mentally unstable during the expedition. Fellow members Douglas Mawson and Alistair Mackay forced him to resign leadership. The official account, published by Shackleton, described the resignation as voluntary. The gap between the private diaries and the public record is documented by historians.
- **Sidney Jeffryes**, Mawson's Australasian Antarctic Expedition (1911–14): arrived mid-expedition as wireless telegraph operator — the only man capable of operating the radio. At Cape Denison, which experienced winds estimated at over 260 km/h, Jeffryes developed severe paranoia. He began transmitting messages to Australia stating he was the only sane man on the expedition and that the others were plotting to kill him. The crew placed him under constant surveillance for the safety of the group.

### Clinical profile
- Documented symptoms include depression, anxiety, irritability, sleep disruption, memory loss, hormonal changes from prolonged cold and darkness, and paranoia.
- Studies across multiple expeditions indicate approximately half of polar expedition members experience significant mood disruption. Around 5% develop symptoms requiring clinical intervention.
- Modern research identifies the condition as overlapping: Seasonal Affective Disorder, isolation psychosis, and what is now termed **"winter-over syndrome."**
- Groups with low social cohesion show markedly higher rates of depression and interpersonal conflict than cohesive groups.
- There was significant stigma around admitting psychological symptoms on expeditions. Expedition members had strong practical incentives to conceal distress.

### Documented incidents of polar violence
- 1959: A man at Russia's Vostok station allegedly stabbed a colleague over a chess game.
- 2017: A team member at South Africa's Marion Island station destroyed a colleague's room with an ax.
- 2018: Sergey Savitsky stabbed Oleg Beloguzov at the Russian Bellingshausen station. The criminal charge was later dropped.

### Morale practices
- Ernest Shackleton's crew selection involved informal personality interviews. He looked for optimism, selflessness, and social adaptability over technical skill.
- When the *Endurance* sank in 1915, Shackleton ordered his men to take no more than two pounds of personal possessions each. The single exception was the expedition meteorologist's banjo — retained specifically to maintain morale during the long polar night.

## ACADEMIC AND FUNDING CULTURE, 1933

### Universities in the Depression
- State university budgets were cut by an average of 40% in the early 1930s, with reductions steepest in manufacturing-heavy regions.
- Private college endowments shrank by more than 26%. Alumni and benefactor gifts, which had supplied nearly a quarter of revenues, fell by over 70%.
- Faculty salaries at many institutions dropped 30–40% by 1933. Some faculty were dismissed entirely.

### Science funding structure
- In 1933, the federal government was largely not in the science funding business. Most research funding came from private philanthropies: the Carnegie Corporation and the Rockefeller Foundation were the primary institutional sources.
- Universities actively resisted federal funding on principle, fearing political interference in the research process.
- Individual wealthy patrons — industrialists, financiers — were the primary source of funding for large expeditions.
- New Deal programs (from 1935 onward) introduced federal money into academic infrastructure through the Works Progress Administration, primarily for construction and employment rather than directed research.

## NAZI AND SOVIET FRINGE SCIENCE, 1933

### Nazi occult and pseudoscientific currents
- January 30, 1933: Hitler became Chancellor. Himmler, already head of the SS since 1929, immediately began planning what would become a formal pseudoscientific research institute.
- **Ahnenerbe** (formally established 1935): Himmler's institute for research into Aryan ancestral heritage. Initially focused on runes, symbols, and Germanic folk history. Expanded to archaeology, anthropology, and expeditions. By 1937 it was an official branch of the SS.
- In 1933, Himmler brought occultist **Karl Maria Wiligut** into the SS. Wiligut's prophecies guided Himmler's selection of Wewelsburg Castle as an SS base. One room was designated the "Grail Room" with a rock crystal representing the Holy Grail.
- **Herman Wirth**, co-founder of the Ahnenerbe and its first president (until 1937): Dutch occult historian obsessed with Atlantis and the idea that Aryans were descended from Atlantean survivors. His primary focus was ancient Nordic runic script.

### Welteislehre (Cosmic Ice Theory)
- A cosmological theory developed by Austrian engineer Hanns Hörbiger, holding that the universe was formed by cosmic ice. Contradicted mainstream physics entirely.
- Hörbiger framed conventional science as "Jewish-Liberal science" hostile to Germanic truth.
- After Hörbiger's death in 1931, followers merged the theory into Nazi scientific culture.
- Himmler created a dedicated meteorological division within the Ahnenerbe specifically to research and validate it.

### Hohlweltlehre (Hollow Earth / Concave Earth Theory)
- The Nazi variant of hollow earth theory held that the Earth's surface was on the *interior* of a concave sphere — that humans lived on the inside of a hollow ball, looking inward.
- Promoted by Peter Bender, a German prisoner of war who encountered the theory during WWI captivity and converted Nazi official Hermann Göring to it.
- Between 1933 and 1942, the theory was treated as credible within certain Nazi scientific and naval circles.
- An actual expedition was dispatched to the Baltic island of Rügen with powerful telescopic cameras to spy on the British fleet — by pointing the cameras upward through the atmosphere across the concave sky. The expedition failed entirely.
- The theory fell out of favor after this failure. Bender was sent to a concentration camp and the theory was officially banned.

### The Ahnenerbe's Tibet Expedition (1938–39)
- Led by Ernst Schäfer (zoologist, SS officer) and Bruno Beger (racial anthropologist).
- Conducted zoological and botanical fieldwork alongside physical measurements of local Tibetan people.
- Operating theory: Tibet was a possible Aryan homeland; the expedition sought physical evidence of an ancestral Aryan presence.

### Edmund Kiss and Atlantis archaeology
- Before the Ahnenerbe's formal establishment, SS archaeologist Edmund Kiss promoted the theory that Aryans were descended from survivors of Atlantis who had spread across the globe and built ancient civilizations. He excavated at Tiwanaku, Bolivia, in search of Atlantean evidence.

### Soviet polar activity
- The USSR was conducting extensive Arctic and polar research throughout the 1930s, driven by both ideological motives (demonstrating Soviet scientific capability) and practical resource mapping.
- American-Soviet scientific relations in 1933 were not yet defined by Cold War hostility — formal US-USSR diplomatic relations were established in November 1933.
