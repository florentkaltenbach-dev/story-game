# The Ross Sea Party — Design Supplement

*Reference document for Mountains of Madness preset. Source: Wikipedia article on the Ross Sea party, cross-referenced with project preset and ceremony architecture.*

---

## Why This Matters for the Ceremony

The Ross Sea party (1914–1917) operated at the exact locations your players will visit: Cape Evans, Hut Point, the Great Ice Barrier, and the route to the Beardmore Glacier. By the time a 1930s expedition arrives, these sites are layered with the physical remains of at least four prior expeditions — Discovery (1901–04), Nimrod (1907–09), Terra Nova (1910–13), and the Ross Sea party itself. Every hut, every cache, every inscription is a potential discovery for your players. More importantly, the Ross Sea party's story is a mirror of your game's central themes: missions pursued past the point of reason, institutional betrayal, cosmic futility, and the question of whether human endurance means anything when the universe doesn't care.

---

## Historical Summary

The Ross Sea party was the support arm of Shackleton's Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition. Their job was simple on paper: land at McMurdo Sound, lay supply depots across the Ice Barrier from the Ross Sea to the Beardmore Glacier, so Shackleton's main party (crossing from the Weddell Sea via the South Pole) could survive the final quarter of their journey. The main party's survival literally depended on those depots being there.

Everything went wrong. The party was underfunded, underequipped, and rushed. Their ship, the SY Aurora, was ripped from its moorings by sea ice during a winter gale and drifted for months, stranding ten men ashore with almost no supplies — most of their gear, food, and fuel was still aboard. Despite this, the stranded men improvised clothing and equipment from the remains of earlier expeditions, and over a grueling second season completed their depot-laying mission in full, all the way to Mount Hope at the foot of the Beardmore Glacier. The effort took 198 continuous days of sledging — the longest in Antarctic history to that point.

Three men died. Arnold Spencer-Smith collapsed from exhaustion and scurvy on the return march and died on 9 March 1916. Aeneas Mackintosh (the party leader) and Victor Hayward walked out onto thin sea ice on 8 May 1916 against the strenuous objections of their companions, vanished into a blizzard within the hour, and were never seen again.

The depots were never used. Shackleton's Endurance was crushed in the Weddell Sea ice. The transcontinental march never happened. The Ross Sea party's entire mission — and the three lives lost completing it — was for nothing. They didn't learn this until January 1917, when the repaired Aurora finally returned to rescue them and Shackleton himself stepped ashore at Cape Evans to deliver the news.

## The Richards Inscription

Dick Richards — the last surviving member of the Ross Sea party, who lived until 1985 at age 91 — carved an inscription on the wall near his bunk inside the Cape Evans hut. It lists the names of the three men who died: Spencer-Smith, Mackintosh, and Hayward.

This inscription is still there. It can still be read. The Cape Evans hut is maintained by the Antarctic Heritage Trust and the New Zealand government, though its condition has deteriorated over the decades.

### Design Value

This is one of the most powerful discoverable objects in the entire preset. A player exploring the Cape Evans hut finds scratched names on a wall — names of men who died completing a mission that turned out to be pointless. This is not ancient Elder Thing history. This is recent. This is human. And it rhymes with everything your players might be walking into.

### How to Deploy It

- The Keeper can describe it during exploration of the Cape Evans hut, if players examine the living quarters closely.
- A player with a historical or academic background might recognize the names or know the story.
- It can function as a quiet moment of foreshadowing — no horror, just the weight of what Antarctica does to people who come here with purpose.
- If a player character dies later in the campaign, the MC could mirror the gesture: an inscription, a record, a name scratched somewhere. The echo makes both moments stronger.

### Companion Details

Richards was without regrets near the end of his life. He didn't regard the struggle as futile. He believed it was something the human spirit had accomplished, and that no undertaking carried through to conclusion was entirely for nothing. This philosophy could surface as a thematic counterpoint to Lovecraftian nihilism — the game's "empathy turn" operates in exactly this space.

## The Aurora

The SY Aurora has one of the stranger fates of any expedition ship. Here is the full arc:

### The Drift (1915–1916)

On the night of 7 May 1915, a severe gale tore the Aurora from its moorings at Cape Evans, carrying it out to sea attached to a large ice floe. First Officer Joseph Stenhouse and the remaining crew were trapped aboard, unable to return. The ship's engines were out of commission. Wireless attempts to contact the shore party failed. The Aurora drifted north — out of McMurdo Sound, into the Ross Sea, and eventually into the Southern Ocean — locked in ice for over nine months. It finally broke free on 12 February 1916 and limped to New Zealand on 2 April with a damaged rudder.

### The Rescue (1916–1917)

The ship was repaired and refitted in New Zealand, funded jointly by the governments of Australia, New Zealand, and Great Britain. Shackleton had no money left and no control — the joint committee dismissed Stenhouse and the original officers, appointing veteran navigator John King Davis to command the rescue. Shackleton was permitted to sail only as a supernumerary officer. The Aurora departed 20 December 1916 and reached Cape Evans on 10 January 1917, rescuing the seven survivors.

### The Disappearance (1917–1918)

After the rescue, Shackleton sold the Aurora for £10,000. She was repurposed as a coal-carrier between Australia and South America. On 20 June 1917, she departed Newcastle, New South Wales — and was never seen again. On 2 January 1918, Lloyd's of London listed her as missing, presumed lost. She either foundered in a storm or was sunk by an enemy raider during the war. Aboard her was James Paton, the Ross Sea party's boatswain, still serving as crew. He went down with the ship.

### Design Value

The Aurora is atmospheric dynamite. A ship torn from its moorings by the ice, drifting helplessly for months, then returning against the odds to rescue the men it abandoned — only to vanish entirely a year later, swallowed by the ocean. This is the kind of story that gives a setting texture.

### How to Deploy It

- **Radio ghost:** Your 1930s expedition might pick up garbled radio transmissions during the sea crossing. Old frequencies, fragments of Morse. Someone suggests it could be the Aurora — still out there, still drifting. This is atmosphere, not plot, but it sets a tone.
- **Ship lore as foreshadowing:** If your players are arriving by ship, the Keeper can have NPCs or briefing materials reference the Aurora's fate as a cautionary tale. Ships don't always come back for you.
- **The Paton detail:** A loyal crewman who survived the Antarctic ordeal only to die on the same ship during a mundane coal run. That's the kind of detail that makes a world feel real and unjust. An NPC could mention this in passing.
- **Structural parallel:** If your players' ship encounters trouble — ice damage, mechanical failure, a decision about whether to wait or leave — the Aurora precedent hangs over every choice. The MC doesn't need to reference it explicitly. Players who've heard the story will feel it.

## The Cape Evans Hut — Loot and Discovery Catalog

By the 1930s, the Cape Evans hut contains accumulated material from multiple expeditions. The Ross Sea party specifically added to this by scavenging earlier expedition supplies to survive. Here is what your players might find, organized by type.

### Improvised Survival Gear

When the Aurora was torn away, the ten stranded men were left with "only the clothes on their backs." They improvised everything from earlier expedition remains:

- **Canvas clothing** from "Joyce's Famous Tailoring Shop" — garments fashioned from a large tent abandoned by Scott's Terra Nova expedition. These would still be in the hut, bizarre handmade coats and trousers cut from tent canvas.
- **"Hut Point Mixture"** — a homemade tobacco substitute concocted by Ernest Wild from sawdust, tea, coffee, and dried herbs. A tin of this could be found, labeled in Wild's handwriting. A player who opens it finds something that smells terrible and has clearly never been good.
- **Improvised sledging equipment** — patched and rebuilt from parts of multiple expeditions. Runners, harnesses, lashings that show the marks of desperate repair.

### Documents and Letters

- **Undelivered letters.** Ernest Wild left a letter at the Mount Hope depot for his brother Frank, assuming Frank was crossing from the Weddell Sea with Shackleton. Frank never came. That letter may still be at Mount Hope, or a copy could have been left at Cape Evans. Either way, it's a letter written to someone who was never going to arrive — a perfect object for your game.
- **Conflicting orders.** Shackleton sent Mackintosh contradictory instructions — on one hand calling the depot-laying of "supreme importance," on the other saying he'd carry enough supplies to cross Antarctica without the depots. These documents, if found, reveal the institutional dysfunction that got people killed.
- **The unsent cable.** Shackleton wrote from South Georgia on 5 December 1914 that he had "no chance of crossing that season." A cable was supposed to be sent to Mackintosh to prevent the first season's depot-laying — the one that destroyed the dogs and nearly killed the men. The cable was never sent. This is a paper trail of negligence.
- **Journals and diaries.** Mackintosh, Joyce, Richards, and others kept records. Fragments would remain. Joyce described conditions during the return march. Mackintosh recorded the winter's survival work. These are primary sources your players could read.

### Equipment and Relics

- **Broken Primus stoves.** The failure of a Primus stove during the second season's march forced three men to turn back. Primus stoves from multiple expeditions would be scattered through the hut — some functional, some not. A player might need one and have to determine which still works.
- **Sledging rations.** Depot supplies were landed before the Aurora blew out. Some may remain cached at Cape Evans or at depot sites along the Barrier route. These are 15+ years old by the 1930s. Pemmican and hardtack survive, but the question is whether players trust them.
- **Scientific instruments.** The Ross Sea party included a small scientific team (physicist, biologist, geologist, chemist) conducting magnetic, meteorological, and biological research. Their instruments — or the remains of them — would be in the hut.
- **Dog harnesses and equipment.** Most of the sled dogs died during the first season. Six survived the winter, four completed the second season's march. Dog gear — harnesses, leads, feeding bowls — would remain, some sized for animals that didn't make it.

### Layers from Earlier Expeditions

The Cape Evans hut was Scott's Terra Nova headquarters. Hut Point was Scott's Discovery base. Your players are walking through a palimpsest:

- **Scott's supplies** (1910–13) — the Ross Sea party scavenged these. Some remain.
- **Nimrod expedition remains** (1907–09) — Shackleton's earlier base was nearby. Equipment overlap between expeditions.
- **Discovery expedition remains** (1901–04) — Hut Point was established as a shelter and storage depot during Scott's first expedition. The oldest layer.

## People — NPC Seeds and Archetypes

The Ross Sea party members map onto archetypes your Keeper can use for NPC creation or for flavoring historical references.

### Aeneas Mackintosh (The Driven Leader)

Former Merchant Navy officer, lost his right eye on the Nimrod expedition. Appointed by Shackleton to lead the Ross Sea party. Pushed forward recklessly despite Joyce's objections, prioritized urgency over safety, made decisions that cost dogs and nearly cost lives. His sense of duty overrode his judgment. Ultimately walked out onto thin ice against all advice and died. Archetype: the leader whose commitment to the mission becomes indistinguishable from self-destruction.

### Ernest Joyce (The Pragmatist)

Antarctic veteran whose experience began with Scott's Discovery expedition. Described by Shackleton's biographer as "a strange mixture of fraud, flamboyance and ability." Clashed constantly with Mackintosh over methods — Joyce favored caution and using dogs, Mackintosh favored urgency and man-hauling. Joyce was right about the methods. He and Richards effectively took command during the second season's return march when Mackintosh collapsed. Awarded the Albert Medal. Archetype: the experienced subordinate who knows better than the boss but lacks the authority to overrule him.

### Dick Richards (The Survivor Who Remembers)

Physicist, signed up for a nominal wage of £1 per week. Youngest scientific member. Along with Joyce, he was one of the two men who held the party together during the worst of the return march — hauling invalids on sledges through blizzards with no food. Awarded the Albert Medal. Lived to 91, the last survivor of the party. Left the inscription on the wall. Said near the end of his life that he had no regrets and didn't regard the struggle as futile — "it was something that the human spirit had accomplished." Archetype: the witness. The one who lives to tell the story and decides what it meant.

### Arnold Spencer-Smith (The Man Who Broke)

Scottish Episcopal priest and former schoolmaster. Joined as a last-minute replacement when original members left for war service. Collapsed on the return march from Mount Hope, physically unable to continue. Was left alone in a tent while the others pushed forward to lay the final depot, then loaded onto a sledge for the return. Died on 9 March 1916, worn out by exhaustion and scurvy, and was buried in the ice. Archetype: the volunteer who had no idea what he was signing up for.

### Victor Hayward (The Adventurer)

London finance clerk "with a taste for adventure," recruited because he'd worked on a ranch in Canada. Collapsed during the return march alongside Mackintosh and Spencer-Smith. Survived the march. Then walked out onto the ice with Mackintosh on 8 May 1916 and vanished. Awarded the Albert Medal posthumously. Archetype: the civilian who came for excitement and found something else entirely.

### Ernest Wild (The Loyal One)

Royal Navy petty officer, possibly recruited through his brother Frank Wild (Shackleton's second-in-command on Endurance). Stayed with the invalids when Joyce and Richards went for supplies at the Bluff depot. Concocted the "Hut Point Mixture" tobacco. Awarded the Albert Medal posthumously — not for the Antarctic, but because he died of typhoid in Malta in 1918 while serving in the war. Archetype: the steady hand who does his duty everywhere and dies somewhere unremarkable.

### Joseph Stenhouse (The Officer Left Behind)

Young officer from the British India Steam Navigation Company. Given command of the Aurora when Mackintosh went ashore. Made the fateful decision to winter the ship at Cape Evans rather than Hut Point. When the ship was torn free, he managed to keep it together during nine months of drift and brought it to New Zealand. Was then dismissed by the joint rescue committee and replaced by Davis. Archetype: the competent man who gets no credit.

### Lionel Hooke (The Kid)

18-year-old electrical apprentice, recruited as wireless operator as a last-minute replacement. Went on to become managing director and then chairman of AWA, knighted in 1957 for services to industry. Archetype: proof that surviving Antarctica young can forge a person into something remarkable.

## Thematic Resonances with the Preset

### Futility and Purpose

The Ross Sea party completed their mission. Every depot was laid. Three men died to make it happen. The depots were never used. This is the game's core question made literal: does effort matter when the outcome is void? Richards said yes. Lovecraft's cosmos says no. Your players will have to decide for themselves.

### The Patron Problem

Shackleton sent contradictory instructions, underfunded the expedition, failed to communicate critical information (the unsent cable), and was beyond reach when things fell apart. This maps directly onto the Starkweather-Moore dynamic. Your patron knows more than they're telling. Your patron's planning may be inadequate. Your patron may not be coming to save anyone. The historical precedent makes these beats feel inevitable rather than contrived.

### Leadership Under Pressure

The Mackintosh-Joyce conflict is a template for party dynamics. One leader prioritizes the mission above all else, the other prioritizes survival. Both are partially right. The tension nearly destroyed the group. If your player party develops a similar split — push forward vs. turn back — the Keeper has historical precedent to draw on for how that plays out.

### The Ice Takes What It Wants

The Aurora being ripped from its moorings is Antarctica asserting its indifference. You can prepare, you can anchor, you can plan — and the ice will do what it does. This is Lovecraftian without needing any supernatural element. The environment itself is the antagonist. For your 1930s expedition, the question is whether the mountains will let them leave.

## The Dogs

Four dogs survived the full ordeal: Oscar, Con, Gunner, and Towser.

Oscar was described as normally lazy, but during the crisis on the return march — when the party was starving and trying to reach the Bluff depot — he lowered his head and pulled harder than he ever had. Richards credited Oscar with providing the extra strength that got them to the depot alive.

Con was killed by the other dogs in a fight before the rescue ship arrived. Oscar, Gunner, and Towser returned to New Zealand and were placed in Wellington Zoo. Oscar reportedly lived to the age of 25.

### Design Value

Dogs are emotionally powerful in game. If your 1930s expedition brings dogs, the Keeper has a template for how Antarctic conditions affect them — and for how a dog's loyalty or stubbornness can become a plot-relevant moment. The detail about Oscar rising to the occasion when everything was desperate is exactly the kind of beat that makes players care about NPCs, even animal ones.

## Specific Locations — What Players Find Where

### Cape Evans (Scott's Terra Nova Hut)

Primary shore base for the Ross Sea party. Contains: Richards' inscription, improvised clothing, remnants of scientific equipment, journals, cached supplies from multiple expeditions, "Joyce's Famous Tailoring Shop" remnants, evidence of the long winter of improvisation.

### Hut Point (Scott's Discovery Base)

Edge of the Barrier, natural starting point for southern journeys. The party was stranded here from late March to 1 June 1915, living on seal meat and blubber. Contains: minimal supplies, evidence of desperate habitation, possible cached sledging gear.

### Minna Bluff Depot (79°S)

First major depot site. Laid during both the first and second seasons. Contains: depot stores (or their remains), depot flags/markers, cached fuel and rations.

### 80°S Depot

Second depot site. The first season's push to reach this point killed all ten dogs taken on the journey. Contains: cached supplies, possible dog graves or remains.

### Mount Hope Depot (83°30'S, foot of the Beardmore Glacier)

Final depot. The furthest point reached. Ernest Wild's letter to his brother Frank was left here. This is also the location closest to the city in the mountains. Contains: Wild's letter (undelivered, uncollected), depot supplies, the southernmost evidence of the Ross Sea party's reach.

## Integration Notes

**Where this fits in the project structure:** This document supplements `mountains_of_madness_preset_v0.md` and feeds into the config files (particularly `world.json` for locations and discoverable objects, `characters.json` for NPC archetypes, and `techniques.json` for foreshadowing patterns). It also informs the briefing document design — the patron's curated briefing might deliberately omit or downplay the Ross Sea party's fate.

**Keeper usage:** The Keeper should have access to this material as part of the world state (Memory Level 5). It's background knowledge — not every detail needs to surface in play, but the Keeper should be able to draw on any of it when players explore the right locations or ask the right questions.

**Tone calibration:** The Ross Sea party material is tragic, not horrific. It operates in the human register — duty, loyalty, stubbornness, institutional failure, the weight of endurance. This is the emotional grounding that makes the cosmic horror land harder when it arrives. Players who've felt the human cost at Cape Evans will react differently when they encounter something inhuman in the mountains.
