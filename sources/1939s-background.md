Great question. Lovecraft packs *At the Mountains of Madness* with period-specific technology, cultural references, and assumptions that a modern reader might gloss over or misunderstand. Here's a rundown of everything that matters for the game:

---

**Aviation — the Dornier seaplanes.** Lovecraft specifies "four large Dornier aëroplanes, designed especially for the tremendous altitude flying necessary on the antarctic plateau." Modern players hear "plane" and think reliable transport. In 1930, Antarctic aviation was barely a thing. Byrd had just made the first flight over the South Pole in November 1929 — one year before Dyer's expedition. These planes are open-cockpit or minimally enclosed, propeller-driven, mounted on landing skis, and powered by engines that can freeze solid if not warmed. They have no radar, no instruments for blind flying, no pressurized cabins. Flying at altitude over the Antarctic plateau means thin air, brutal cold, and zero margin for error. A whiteout or sudden fog means you're dead — you can't see the ground and the horizon disappears. Lovecraft uses the planes as a way to cover vast distances quickly, but every flight is a gamble. Your players need to understand that choosing to fly somewhere is a genuine risk decision, not a taxi ride.

**Photography — evidence that can be doubted.** Lovecraft makes a big deal of Dyer photographing everything in the city. Modern players assume photographs are irrefutable. In the early 1930s, they were anything but. Dyer and Danforth are using hand cameras with film — likely large-format cameras that produce individual negatives. Each shot is deliberate (no motor drive, no rapid shooting). Development happens later, back at camp or back in civilization. The photographs can be blurry, damaged by cold, double-exposed, or simply unconvincing — a photo of a massive ruined city in Antarctica looks like a mirage or a hoax to anyone who wasn't there. This is why Dyer's account is dismissed despite having physical evidence. Photography in this era *supports* claims but doesn't *prove* them the way we'd expect today. For the game, this means your players' evidence is inherently fragile. Whatever they document, people back home can and will doubt.

**Pabodie's drill — what it means to bore into rock.** Lovecraft describes the drill in loving detail: steel head, jointed rods, gasoline motor, collapsible wooden derrick, dynamiting paraphernalia. This isn't a modern diamond-core drill rig. It's a hybrid between an artesian well drill and a rock-boring tool, light enough to be carried on three dog sledges (which is remarkable and is presented as an engineering achievement). The blasting is dynamite — literal explosives to break through harder strata. Lake's team uses a blast and that's what opens the cave containing the specimens. Your players would have a similar or improved version. The key thing for modern players: drilling is slow, loud, violent work. You're not scanning the ground with sensors. You're punching holes in rock with steel and gunpowder, and you have no idea what's underneath until you break through.

**Dog sledges — more than transport.** Lovecraft uses dogs as both logistics and narrative instrument. The dogs react to the Elder Thing specimens before any human recognizes the danger. In the 1930s, dogs were the primary surface transport in Antarctica — motorized vehicles existed but were unreliable in extreme cold (Scott's motor sledges failed catastrophically). Dogs are alive, they have personalities, they get injured and die, and they know things their handlers don't. Lovecraft understood this. For your players, dogs aren't equipment — they're NPCs with instincts that function as an early warning system. A player who bonds with their dog team and pays attention to behavior gets information the others miss.

**Navigation — you can get lost.** No GPS, no satellite imagery, no detailed maps. Antarctic navigation in the 1930s relies on magnetic compass (unreliable near the magnetic pole), sun compass (useless in overcast or at night), sextant (requires visible horizon and celestial bodies), and theodolite for surveying. Maps of the interior are sketch-based, often drawn from aerial observation at speed, and frequently wrong. Lovecraft has Dyer and Danforth navigating the city using a trail of torn paper dropped behind them — "the ancient principle of hare and hounds." That's not whimsy; that's a real navigation method for enclosed spaces where compasses don't work and every corridor looks the same. Your players going into the city need to actively solve the problem of knowing where they are. Getting lost isn't a narrative contrivance — it's the default state.

**Newspapers as primary media.** Lovecraft specifies that Lake's wireless bulletins were "relayed through the *Arkham Advertiser* and Associated Press." In the early 1930s, newspapers are how the public learns about anything. Radio news exists but is young. There's no television. An expedition's reports are transmitted by wireless Morse to their home base, then released to wire services, then printed in papers that people read the next morning. This means there's a public record of what Lake found — but it's filtered, delayed, and edited. The patron's briefing in your game draws on this public record, and your players might not realize how much was lost or distorted in the chain from Lake's frantic wireless to a newspaper headline.

**The Necronomicon and rare book culture.** Lovecraft's characters reference the Necronomicon, the Pnakotic Manuscripts, and other occult texts as though they're rare library books — because that's exactly what they are in his world. In the early 20th century, rare and forbidden books circulated through university libraries, private collectors, and antiquarian dealers. There was no internet to look things up. If you wanted to read a rare text, you physically traveled to the library that held a copy. Lovecraft specifies that Miskatonic University's library holds a copy of the Necronomicon — one of very few in existence. Danforth is one of the people who has actually read it, which is presented as a dangerous and unusual achievement, not a casual thing. For your players, if someone's character has read forbidden texts, that's a major backstory element with real consequences — it means they had institutional access and the will to seek out material that most scholars avoid.

**Poe's *Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym*.** Lovecraft references this directly. Poe's 1838 novel ends with a mysterious expedition to the Antarctic, a milky white curtain, and the cry "Tekeli-li." Lovecraft deliberately connects his story to Poe's — the Shoggoths' cry of "Tekeli-li" is a direct callback. In the 1930s, educated people would have read Poe and recognized the reference. Modern players almost certainly haven't read Pym and won't catch it unless you tell them. This matters because Lovecraft is doing something specific: he's saying that Poe wasn't writing fiction, he was recording garbled accounts of real encounters. The literary reference *is* evidence within the story's logic. If a player character is literary or well-read, the Keeper can use Pym as a planted clue — something the character recognizes with growing horror.

**Nicholas Roerich — the painter.** Lovecraft compares the Antarctic mountains to "the strange and disturbing Asian paintings of Nicholas Roerich." Roerich was a real Russian painter, famous in the 1920s and 1930s for luminous, mystical depictions of Himalayan and Central Asian landscapes — jagged peaks, otherworldly light, impossible colors. He was also a genuine mystic who believed in hidden cities and lost civilizations in the mountains of Asia. He led a real expedition through Central Asia in the 1920s searching for Shambhala. When Lovecraft invokes Roerich, he's not just describing a visual style — he's connecting Antarctica to a tradition of hidden places and forbidden knowledge that his contemporary readers would have recognized. For the game, Roerich paintings could literally appear in the briefing materials or aboard the ship, and a knowledgeable character would understand the implication: someone thinks there's something hidden in those mountains.

**Whaling — the last people who know these waters.** Antarctic whaling was at its peak in the 1920s and 1930s. Factory ships operated in the Ross Sea and surrounding waters. Whalers were often the most experienced Antarctic navigators alive — they knew the ice patterns, the weather, the coastlines. Lovecraft's expedition uses a ship captained by experienced Antarctic hands. Your game's ship captain is described as a "veteran Antarctic whaler." For modern players, whaling is a distant or distasteful concept. In the 1930s, it was a major industry, and whalers were the rough, practical experts you hired when you needed someone who'd actually survived these waters before. They're also a source of rumors, superstitions, and observations that scientists dismiss — until the scientists see the same things.

**Fuel and heating — the constant battle.** Everything runs on gasoline or kerosene. The planes, the drill motor, the Primus stoves for cooking and melting snow for water, the heating in tents and huts. Fuel is heavy, finite, and precious. Running out of fuel in Antarctica means you freeze to death — it's that direct. The Ross Sea party nearly died because their fuel was aboard the Aurora when it drifted away. Lovecraft doesn't dwell on fuel logistics, but they underpin everything. For the game, fuel is a resource the Keeper tracks. Every flight burns fuel. Every night in camp burns fuel. Going deeper into the mountains means carrying fuel further from the supply chain. The moment players realize they might not have enough fuel to get back is a horror beat that requires no monsters.

**Medical knowledge — they know less than you think.** Scurvy was understood (it killed Spencer-Smith in the Ross Sea party), but treatment was still basic — fresh meat, particularly seal or penguin liver. Frostbite treatment was crude. Hypothermia wasn't well understood as a medical condition. There were no antibiotics — penicillin wasn't available until the 1940s. An infected wound in Antarctica in 1933 is potentially fatal. For the game, injuries are serious in a way modern players might not intuit. There's no medevac. The expedition surgeon has laudanum, morphine, bandages, and basic surgical tools. That's about it.

**The "heroic age" as recent memory.** Scott died in 1912 — only twenty years before your game's setting. Shackleton died in 1922 — eleven years ago. Amundsen disappeared in 1928 — five years ago. These aren't historical figures to your characters; they're recent legends, some of whom people in the expedition might have personally known. The Ross Sea party survivors are still alive. Byrd is actively operating in Antarctica during this period. The Antarctic exploration community is small, interconnected, and haunted by its losses. Your players' characters exist in a world where going to Antarctica is known to kill people, where the names of the dead are familiar, and where going anyway is considered either heroic or insane depending on who you ask.

**Telegrams and cables — how urgency works.** Before a phone call could be placed across oceans, urgent communication meant telegrams: brief, expensive, charged by the word. This is why Lake's wireless bulletins are dense and clipped — every word costs, and the operator is transcribing from Morse. For your game, messages from the patron or from the outside world arrive in telegram style: terse, ambiguous, sometimes garbled. A message that says "STOP EXPEDITION IMMEDIATELY STOP DYER" reads very differently from a polite email. The format itself carries urgency and leaves room for misinterpretation.
