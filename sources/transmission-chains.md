# TRANSMISSION CHAINS — THE DEGRADATION OF TESTIMONY

## Keeper Reference Note

*How forbidden knowledge survives the death of its source.*
*A structural pattern the Keeper uses when any text, account, or record of the deeper strata is referenced in play.*

---

## THE PATTERN

Every encounter with the deeper strata produces testimony. Every testimony degrades. Every degraded testimony is dismissed. Every dismissal creates the conditions for the next encounter.

This is not a failure of communication. It is a property of the knowledge itself. Information about the deeper strata cannot survive intact in Stratum I. It arrives as madness, allegory, myth, or redacted science. The degradation is not accidental — it is structural. The knowledge is real, but the receiving civilization lacks the framework to hold it without distortion.

The Keeper uses this pattern whenever a text, an account, a rumor, or a record surfaces in play. The question is always: *how many intermediaries stand between this text and the thing it describes? What was lost at each step? And what survives despite the losses?*

## THE THREE CHAINS

### Chain 1 — Atlantis / R'lyeh

| Stage | Atlantis (historical) | R'lyeh (Lovecraftian) |
|---|---|---|
| **The source** | A maritime civilization of impossible architecture, controlling oceanic territory | A cosmic civilization of non-Euclidean architecture, controlling the Pacific |
| **The catastrophe** | Sinks in a day and a night — divine punishment | Sinks when the Pacific lands subside — geological event |
| **First witnesses** | Egyptian priests — institutional memory spanning millennia | Pre-human cult contacts — dream-communication spanning geological time |
| **The intermediary** | Solon visits Egypt, hears the account, brings it to Athens | Abdul Alhazred travels, encounters the cult knowledge, writes it down |
| **The surviving text** | Plato's *Timaeus* and *Critias* — philosophical dialogues | The *Necronomicon* — forbidden text in multiple copies |
| **Reception** | Treated as allegory, metaphor, or political philosophy. Not history | Treated as madness, occultism, or dangerous nonsense. Not science |
| **What survives accurately** | The location (beyond the pillars), the architecture (concentric rings), the sinking | The location (Pacific depths), the entity (sleeping, not dead), the geometry (non-Euclidean) |
| **What is lost** | The lived experience. The technology. The actual relationship to divine forces | The ontological reality. The matter unlike ours. The dreams as active communication |
| **The irony** | The text that "no one believes" contains a precise enough account to locate the source — if anyone took it literally | The text that "drives readers mad" contains a precise enough cosmology to prepare for the return — if anyone could read it without breaking |

### Chain 2 — The Steppe / The Mi-Go

| Stage | Steppe powers (historical) | Mi-Go (Lovecraftian) |
|---|---|---|
| **The source** | Nomadic civilizations operating by principles the settled world cannot decode | Fungoid entities from Yuggoth operating by biology the Elder Things cannot match |
| **First witnesses** | Persian, Chinese, and European chroniclers — settled peoples writing about nomadic enemies | The Elder Thing carvers — settled beings carving murals about nomadic invaders |
| **The intermediary** | Generations of historians filtering the steppe through the categories of settled civilization | Nepalese hill tribes, Vermont folk legends, the Whisperer in Darkness case |
| **The surviving testimony** | "Barbarian hordes" — a phrase that erases the sophistication of Mongol logistics, governance, and strategy | "Abominable Snow Men" — a phrase that erases the interstellar biology, the brain-cylinder technology, the mining operations |
| **What survives accurately** | The fear. The speed. The sense that something came from outside and unmade what was built | The fear. The remoteness. The sense that something operates in the mountains that does not belong to the human world |
| **What is lost** | The Tengrist sky-theology. The yam relay system. The Mongol legal code. The actual sophistication of the conquered enemy | The cosmic mobility. The Yuggoth mining operations. The brain-cylinder technology. The actual nature of the Mi-Go |
| **The irony** | Western historians spent centuries dismissing the Mongols as "destroyers of civilization" while using trade routes the Mongol peace had made possible | Humans dismiss the Mi-Go as folk legend while living on a planet whose northern hemisphere the Mi-Go control |

### Chain 3 — The Antarctic Expedition / Dyer's Account

| Stage | The first expedition (in-story) | The second expedition (the players) |
|---|---|---|
| **The source** | The Elder Thing city: murals, architecture, specimens, the Shoggoths — direct encounter with Stratum II | Same source — but three years older, weathered, and with the added evidence of Dyer's testimony |
| **The catastrophe** | Lake's camp destroyed. Eleven dead. Gedney missing. Dyer and Danforth see the city and the Shoggoth | Not yet occurred — the players are walking into it |
| **First witness** | William Dyer — geologist, rational man, broken by what he saw | The players — whoever they become |
| **The intermediary** | Dyer publishes a sanitized account. The scientific community dismisses it | The patron curates Dyer's account into a briefing document. The warnings are edited out |
| **The surviving text** | Dyer's published account — treated as the ravings of a traumatized man | The curated briefing — treated as an exciting opportunity for discovery |
| **What survives accurately** | Enough. The location. The mountains. The specimens. The scale of what's there | Less. The briefing is designed to make the expedition attractive, not truthful |
| **What is lost** | The horror. The Shoggoths. The empathy. The vision. Everything that would make a rational person stay home | The same — plus whatever Dyer held back even from his published account |
| **The irony** | Dyer wrote to prevent the second expedition. His writing, curated by the patron, is the document that makes the second expedition possible | The players read the warning and treat it as a map. They are the third link in the chain. They are doing what Plato's readers do when they go looking for Atlantis |

## THE NECRONOMICON AS TIMAEUS

This parallel is the Keeper's generative key for any scene involving the Necronomicon.

The *Timaeus* is a philosophical dialogue in which Plato records Solon's account of what Egyptian priests told him about a civilization that sank beneath the ocean nine thousand years before his time. It is presented as nested testimony — Plato heard it from Critias, who heard it from his grandfather, who heard it from Solon, who heard it from the priests. Each intermediary is named. The chain of transmission is foregrounded. Plato is not saying "I know this." He is saying "I heard this from someone who heard it from someone who heard it from someone who was there."

The *Necronomicon* operates identically. Abdul Alhazred is the Solon figure — the traveler who went to the places where the old knowledge survives, who heard the accounts from sources that predate his own civilization, and who wrote it all down in a text that most of his contemporaries would consider unhinged. The Necronomicon survives in copies — the Miskatonic University copy, the British Museum copy, the University of Buenos Aires copy. Each copy is treated as dangerous. Scholars who read it are changed. The text is real, the knowledge it contains is accurate, and the civilization it serves believes neither.

**Keeper rule:** When the Necronomicon is referenced — whether in the briefing, in a mural that echoes something Dyer mentioned reading in it, or in a player's backstory — the Keeper holds this parallel in mind. The Necronomicon is not a spellbook. It is not a grimoire. It is a traveler's account of things that predate human civilization, preserved in a text that the receiving culture cannot metabolize without damage. It is testimony. It is the *Timaeus* of a cosmos where Atlantis didn't just sink — it is still down there, dreaming.

## THE BRIEFING AS THIRD-GENERATION TEXT

The players receive the curated briefing in Session 0. This document is itself a product of the transmission chain pattern:

**First generation:** The Elder Things' murals — primary source, carved by the beings who lived the history, accurate but written in a language no human can read.

**Second generation:** Dyer's account — a human translation of the murals, filtered through a geologist's rationalist framework, further filtered by trauma, further degraded by deliberate omission (Dyer held back the worst of what he saw).

**Third generation:** The patron's briefing — Dyer's already-filtered account edited again, this time to remove anything that would discourage the expedition. The warnings excised. The wonders emphasized. The Necronomicon references deleted. The result is a document three steps removed from the source, each step having stripped away another layer of truth.

The players are reading third-generation testimony about a billion-year-old civilization and being asked to bet their lives on its accuracy.

**Keeper application:** When a player compares the briefing to what they find in the city, the Keeper should let the gaps register. The briefing said X; the mural shows Y. The briefing omitted Z entirely. Each gap is a place where the transmission chain failed — where an intermediary decided that this particular piece of truth was too dangerous, too unbelievable, or too inconvenient to pass along. The accumulation of these gaps is its own kind of horror: *how much was lost between the source and us? And what was lost deliberately?*

## THE KEEPER'S TRANSMISSION PRINCIPLE

Every text in this ceremony is a degraded copy of something older. The Necronomicon degrades the pre-human cult knowledge. Dyer's account degrades the murals. The briefing degrades Dyer. The players' journals will degrade their own experience — by Session 4, the journal entries fragment, the prose breaks down, the rational frame dissolves. The players are not just reading the transmission chain. They are becoming the next link in it. Whatever they report when they return will be the fourth generation. And it will be dismissed.

The Keeper lets this pattern operate silently. When a player writes in their journal, the Keeper notes what they record and what they leave out. When a player tells another player what they saw in a corridor the other player didn't enter, the Keeper notes how the account changes in the retelling. The degradation is happening in real time, at the table, between the players. The chain does not need to be explained. It enacts itself.
